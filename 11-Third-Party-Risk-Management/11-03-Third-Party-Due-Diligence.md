# Introduction

Selecting a third party is one of the most important decisions an organization makes. Whether engaging a cloud provider, managed security service provider, software vendor, outsourcing partner, or consultant, organizations place trust in external entities to perform business functions, protect sensitive information, and comply with contractual and regulatory obligations.

Without proper due diligence, organizations may unknowingly engage vendors with weak cybersecurity controls, poor financial health, inadequate privacy practices, or limited operational resilience. These weaknesses can expose the organization to cyberattacks, operational disruptions, regulatory penalties, financial losses, and reputational damage.

Third-party due diligence is the process of evaluating a prospective vendor before entering into a business relationship. It enables organizations to identify risks, assess vendor capabilities, verify governance practices, and determine whether the vendor can safely and reliably deliver the required products or services.

---

# What is Third-Party Due Diligence?

Third-party due diligence is a structured assessment performed before onboarding a vendor to evaluate whether the organization can confidently establish and maintain the business relationship.

The assessment considers multiple areas including:

- Cybersecurity.
- Operational capability.
- Financial stability.
- Regulatory compliance.
- Privacy practices.
- Legal obligations.
- Business continuity.
- Reputation.

Due diligence helps organizations make informed, risk-based decisions before contracts are signed.

---

# Purpose of Due Diligence

The primary purpose of due diligence is to understand the risks associated with a third party before engagement.

Its objectives include:

- Identifying potential risks.
- Evaluating vendor capabilities.
- Verifying security controls.
- Assessing regulatory compliance.
- Confirming financial stability.
- Supporting procurement decisions.
- Protecting organizational assets.
- Reducing the likelihood of future incidents.

Due diligence enables organizations to proactively manage risks rather than reacting after problems occur.

---

# Why Due Diligence Matters

Organizations increasingly rely on external providers for critical business services.

Without proper due diligence:

- Sensitive data may be exposed.
- Weak vendors may be selected.
- Regulatory obligations may be overlooked.
- Contracts may fail to address key risks.
- Operational disruptions may increase.
- Security incidents may become more likely.
- Business continuity may be affected.
- Organizational reputation may suffer.

A thorough assessment significantly reduces these risks before the vendor relationship begins.

---

# Types of Due Diligence

Organizations perform different types of due diligence depending on the vendor's level of risk and business criticality.

| Due Diligence Area | Purpose |
|------|------|
| Cybersecurity | Evaluate information security controls and cyber resilience |
| Financial | Assess financial stability and long-term viability |
| Operational | Review service delivery capability and operational maturity |
| Legal | Evaluate legal obligations and contractual risks |
| Compliance | Verify compliance with applicable laws and regulations |
| Privacy | Assess protection of personal information |
| Business Continuity | Review disaster recovery and resilience capabilities |
| Reputation | Evaluate public reputation, ethics, and business practices |

The depth of assessment should be proportional to the vendor's risk level.

---

# Risk-Based Due Diligence

Organizations should apply a risk-based approach rather than treating every vendor equally.

Factors influencing assessment depth include:

- Business criticality.
- Data sensitivity.
- System access.
- Regulatory impact.
- Financial dependency.
- Geographic location.
- Cloud service usage.
- Use of subcontractors.

Critical vendors typically require more comprehensive assessments than low-risk suppliers.

---

# Information Collected During Due Diligence

Organizations gather information from multiple sources to evaluate vendors.

Common evidence includes:

- Security questionnaires.
- ISO certifications.
- SOC reports.
- Audit reports.
- Financial statements.
- Business continuity plans.
- Insurance certificates.
- Privacy documentation.
- Regulatory certifications.
- Publicly available information.

The collected evidence supports objective risk evaluation.

---

# Due Diligence Outcomes

At the conclusion of the assessment, organizations typically make one of several decisions.

Possible outcomes include:

- Approve the vendor.
- Approve with conditions.
- Require remediation before onboarding.
- Escalate for executive review.
- Reject the vendor.

These decisions should be documented and supported by risk assessment results.

Third-party due diligence supports the requirements and guidance found in numerous standards and regulations.

Examples include:

|------|------|
| ISO/IEC 27001 | Supplier information security evaluations |
| ISO 31000 | Risk assessment and treatment |
| ISO 22301 | Supplier resilience assessments |
| NIST CSF 2.0 | Cyber supply chain risk management |
| NIST SP 800-161 | Supply chain due diligence |
| NIS2 Directive | Supply chain cybersecurity oversight |
| DORA | ICT third-party assessments |
| GDPR | Assessment of data processors |

Aligning due diligence with these frameworks strengthens governance and compliance.

---

# Best Practices

Organizations should:

- Perform due diligence before engaging any significant vendor.
- Apply a risk-based assessment methodology.
- Use standardized questionnaires and assessment criteria.
- Collect objective evidence to support decisions.
- Involve cybersecurity, legal, privacy, compliance, and procurement teams.
- Document assessment findings and approvals.
- Escalate high-risk vendors for executive review.
- Periodically reassess long-term vendors.
- Maintain complete due diligence records.
- Continuously improve assessment methodologies based on lessons learned.

---

# Practical Example

A healthcare organization plans to implement a cloud-based patient management platform that will process sensitive medical information. Because the vendor will host confidential patient records and support critical healthcare operations, the organization classifies the provider as a critical third party.

Before signing the contract, Procurement coordinates a comprehensive due diligence assessment involving Information Security, Privacy, Legal, Compliance, and Business Continuity teams. The assessment includes a review of ISO/IEC 27001 certification, penetration testing reports, SOC reports, disaster recovery capabilities, GDPR compliance, financial stability, and cybersecurity policies. Although the vendor demonstrates strong security controls, the assessment identifies an opportunity to strengthen incident notification procedures. This requirement is added to the contract before approval.

By completing thorough due diligence before onboarding, the healthcare organization reduces operational, cybersecurity, and regulatory risks while ensuring the selected vendor meets the organization's governance expectations.

---

# Key Takeaways

- Third-party due diligence is a structured process for evaluating vendors before engagement.
- Due diligence helps identify cybersecurity, operational, financial, legal, privacy, compliance, and business continuity risks.
- Risk-based assessments allow organizations to focus resources on the vendors that present the greatest business impact.
- Objective evidence supports informed procurement and governance decisions.
- Due diligence aligns with international standards such as ISO/IEC 27001, ISO 31000, NIST CSF 2.0, NIST SP 800-161, NIS2, DORA, and GDPR.
- Effective due diligence strengthens Third-Party Risk Management, improves vendor selection, and reduces enterprise risk.

# Introduction

Cybersecurity has become one of the most important considerations when selecting and managing third parties. Vendors frequently receive access to organizational systems, process sensitive information, host critical applications, or support essential business operations. As a result, weaknesses in a vendor's cybersecurity program can directly expose the organization to cyberattacks, data breaches, ransomware incidents, and regulatory violations.

A third-party security assessment is a structured evaluation of a vendor's cybersecurity capabilities and security controls. The objective is to determine whether the vendor can adequately protect the organization's information, systems, and services throughout the business relationship.

Security assessments should be performed before onboarding high-risk vendors and repeated periodically based on the vendor's risk classification and changing threat landscape.

---

# Purpose of Security Assessments

The purpose of a security assessment is to evaluate whether a third party has implemented appropriate cybersecurity controls.

Its objectives include:

- Identifying cybersecurity risks.
- Evaluating security governance.
- Verifying technical controls.
- Assessing operational security.
- Protecting sensitive information.
- Supporting regulatory compliance.
- Reducing supply chain risks.
- Supporting informed risk decisions.

Security assessments help organizations establish confidence before granting vendor access to critical systems or information.

---

# Scope of a Security Assessment

The scope of the assessment depends on the services provided and the associated business risk.

Common assessment areas include:

- Information security governance.
- Identity and access management.
- Network security.
- Endpoint protection.
- Cloud security.
- Application security.
- Data protection.
- Incident response.
- Vulnerability management.
- Business continuity.

Higher-risk vendors generally require more comprehensive assessments.

---

# Information Security Governance

Organizations should evaluate how the vendor manages cybersecurity at the organizational level.

Typical review areas include:

- Information security policies.
- Security governance structure.
- Security roles and responsibilities.
- Risk management processes.
- Security awareness training.
- Executive oversight.
- Internal audits.
- Continual improvement activities.

Strong governance demonstrates organizational commitment to cybersecurity.

---

# Technical Security Controls

Technical controls protect systems and information against cyber threats.

Examples include:

- Multi-factor authentication (MFA).
- Encryption.
- Firewalls.
- Endpoint detection and response (EDR).
- Security monitoring.
- Secure configuration management.
- Patch management.
- Backup protection.

Organizations should verify that these controls are appropriate for the services being provided.

---

# Identity and Access Management

Many vendors require privileged or remote access to organizational resources.

Assessment areas include:

- User authentication.
- Privileged access management.
- Role-based access control.
- Password policies.
- Account provisioning.
- Access reviews.
- Account termination.
- Multi-factor authentication.

Proper access management significantly reduces cybersecurity risk.

---

# Vulnerability and Patch Management

Organizations should assess how vendors identify and remediate security weaknesses.

Evaluation areas include:

- Vulnerability scanning.
- Penetration testing.
- Patch management processes.
- Remediation timelines.
- Risk prioritization.
- Security monitoring.
- Configuration management.

Timely remediation helps reduce exposure to known vulnerabilities.

---

# Incident Response Capabilities

Organizations should evaluate the vendor's ability to detect and respond to cybersecurity incidents.

Assessment topics include:

- Incident response plans.
- Security Operations Center (SOC).
- Incident reporting procedures.
- Forensic capabilities.
- Recovery procedures.
- Communication processes.
- Regulatory notification procedures.

Effective incident response minimizes the impact of security events.

---

# Security Evidence

Organizations should request objective evidence to support assessment findings.

Examples include:

- ISO/IEC 27001 certificates.
- SOC 1 or SOC 2 reports.
- Penetration testing reports.
- Vulnerability assessment reports.
- Security policies.
- Risk assessment reports.
- Cyber insurance certificates.
- Independent audit reports.

Evidence provides greater confidence than relying solely on vendor self-assessments.

Security assessments should align with recognized cybersecurity standards and frameworks.

| CIS Controls | Cybersecurity best practices |
| PCI DSS | Payment card security controls |
| SOC 2 | Security, availability, and confidentiality controls |

Using recognized standards improves assessment consistency and comparability.

---

# Best Practices

Organizations should:

- Perform security assessments before onboarding high-risk vendors.
- Apply a risk-based assessment methodology.
- Review independent certifications and audit reports.
- Verify the effectiveness of security controls through objective evidence.
- Assess cloud security when vendors host organizational data.
- Include cybersecurity requirements in vendor contracts.
- Reassess critical vendors periodically.
- Coordinate assessments across cybersecurity, procurement, legal, and compliance teams.
- Document findings and remediation activities.
- Continuously improve assessment processes as threats evolve.

---

# Practical Example

A retail company plans to implement a Software as a Service (SaaS) platform to manage customer loyalty programs. Because the platform will process customer information and integrate with the organization's payment systems, the vendor is classified as high risk.

Before onboarding, the Information Security team conducts a comprehensive security assessment. The review includes the vendor's ISO/IEC 27001 certification, SOC 2 report, penetration testing results, vulnerability management process, encryption practices, access management controls, and incident response capabilities. The assessment identifies weaknesses in privileged access monitoring, and the vendor agrees to implement additional controls before the contract is finalized.

By performing a structured security assessment, the organization gains confidence that the vendor can adequately protect customer information and support secure business operations.

---

# Key Takeaways

- Security assessments are a critical component of Third-Party Risk Management.
- Assessments evaluate governance, technical controls, identity management, vulnerability management, incident response, and data protection.
- Objective evidence such as ISO/IEC 27001 certifications and SOC reports strengthens assessment accuracy.
- A risk-based approach ensures that assessment activities are proportionate to the vendor's level of risk.
- Aligning assessments with recognized cybersecurity standards improves consistency and regulatory compliance.
- Effective security assessments reduce supply chain risks and support informed vendor selection decisions.
