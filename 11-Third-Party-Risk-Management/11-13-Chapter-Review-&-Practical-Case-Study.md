# Introduction

Throughout this chapter, you learned the principles, processes, frameworks, and technologies used to manage third-party risks. However, successful TPRM is not about performing isolated activities—it is about integrating governance, security, compliance, procurement, legal, and business operations into a coordinated program.

This lesson presents a practical case study that demonstrates how a mature organization manages a critical vendor throughout its lifecycle. The objective is to illustrate how the concepts discussed in previous lessons work together in a real-world environment.

---

# Case Study Background

**Organization:** GlobalBank International

**Industry:** Financial Services

**Employees:** 18,000

**Countries of Operation:** 35

**Critical Business Services:**

- Online Banking
- Mobile Banking
- Payment Processing
- Digital Identity Services
- Customer Relationship Management
- Financial Reporting

To support its digital transformation strategy, GlobalBank plans to migrate several customer-facing applications to a cloud-based infrastructure provided by a third-party vendor.

Because the vendor will process sensitive customer information and support critical banking services, the relationship is classified as **high risk** and requires enhanced Third-Party Risk Management.

---

# Phase 1 — Business Need Identification

The Technology Division identifies the need for a cloud infrastructure provider to improve scalability, disaster recovery, and service availability.

Before procurement begins, the business owner submits a vendor request describing:

- Business objectives.
- Expected services.
- Information assets involved.
- Regulatory considerations.
- Business criticality.
- Estimated contract value.
- Expected implementation timeline.
- Operational dependencies.

This information allows the TPRM team to begin the initial risk classification process.

---

# Phase 2 — Initial Vendor Risk Classification

The TPRM team performs an inherent risk assessment based on predefined criteria.

| Risk Category | Initial Assessment |
|------|------|
| Business Criticality | High |
| Information Sensitivity | High |
| Customer Data Access | Yes |
| Regulatory Impact | High |
| Cloud Hosting | Yes |
| Operational Dependency | High |
| Financial Exposure | Medium |
| Geographic Risk | Medium |

Based on these factors, the vendor is classified as a **Critical Third Party**, requiring enhanced due diligence and executive oversight.

---

# Phase 3 — Governance Activation

Following the high-risk classification, multiple departments become involved.


Each stakeholder has clearly defined responsibilities to ensure the vendor is evaluated from multiple perspectives before onboarding.

---

# Phase 4 — Due Diligence Planning

The TPRM team develops a due diligence plan tailored to the vendor's risk profile.

Activities include:

- Security questionnaire.
- ISO/IEC 27001 certification review.
- SOC 2 report evaluation.
- Financial stability review.
- Business continuity assessment.
- Privacy impact review.
- Regulatory compliance verification.
- Contract security requirements.

Because the vendor supports critical banking services, the assessment scope is broader than for standard vendors.

---

# Key Decisions During This Phase

The governance committee considers several important questions:

- Does the vendor support critical business operations?
- Will customer data leave the organization's control?
- Are additional contractual safeguards required?
- Is regulatory notification necessary?
- Should executive approval be required before onboarding?
- Are compensating controls needed?

These questions guide risk-based decision-making throughout the engagement.

---

# Lessons Learned

This first stage of the case study demonstrates several important principles:

- Vendor management begins before contracts are signed.
- Risk classification determines the level of due diligence.
- Governance ensures that appropriate stakeholders participate.
- Business context influences every risk decision.
- Early planning reduces delays later in the vendor lifecycle.

Organizations that invest time in proper planning establish a strong foundation for successful vendor risk management.

# Practical Reflection

At this point in the vendor lifecycle, GlobalBank has not yet signed a contract or transferred any customer data. Instead, it has focused on understanding the business need, identifying inherent risks, engaging the appropriate stakeholders, and defining the due diligence activities required for a high-risk cloud provider.

This proactive approach helps ensure that security, compliance, operational resilience, and business objectives are considered before the vendor relationship begins.

---

# Key Takeaways

- Effective TPRM begins with understanding the business need and classifying vendor risk.
- Risk classification determines the level of due diligence and governance required.
- Cross-functional collaboration ensures that security, legal, procurement, compliance, and business teams contribute to informed decisions.
- High-risk vendors require enhanced assessment and executive oversight.
- Early planning reduces future operational, security, and compliance risks.
- A structured onboarding process creates the foundation for successful vendor governance throughout the entire vendor lifecycle.

# Vendor Due Diligence, Security Assessment & Onboarding

---

# Learning Objectives

By the end of this section, you will be able to:

- Apply vendor due diligence activities within a real-world business scenario.
- Understand how organizations evaluate the security posture of high-risk vendors.
- Explain the importance of contractual controls and legal safeguards.
- Recognize how residual risks are evaluated before vendor approval.
- Understand how executive governance supports vendor onboarding decisions.

# Introduction

Following the completion of the initial vendor classification and governance review, GlobalBank proceeds with a comprehensive due diligence assessment of the selected cloud service provider.

Because the vendor has been classified as a **Critical Third Party**, the organization applies enhanced assessment procedures that go beyond standard procurement activities. The objective is to validate the vendor's financial stability, cybersecurity capabilities, regulatory compliance, operational resilience, and ability to securely support GlobalBank's critical banking services.

This stage of the vendor lifecycle demonstrates how multiple business functions collaborate to ensure that only trustworthy vendors are approved to handle sensitive information and critical operations.

---

# Phase 5 — Comprehensive Vendor Due Diligence

The Third-Party Risk Management (TPRM) team coordinates the due diligence activities with several internal stakeholders.

The following departments participate in the assessment:

- Procurement
- Information Security
- Legal
- Compliance
- Privacy Office
- Enterprise Risk Management
- Technology Division
- Business Owner

The vendor is requested to provide detailed documentation to support the assessment process.

Requested documents include:

- ISO/IEC 27001 Certificate
- SOC 2 Type II Report
- Information Security Policies
- Business Continuity Plan (BCP)
- Disaster Recovery Plan (DRP)
- Latest Penetration Testing Report
- Vulnerability Management Process
- Financial Statements
- Cyber Insurance Coverage
- Data Processing Agreement (DPA)
- Regulatory Certifications

Each document is reviewed by the appropriate subject matter experts before the vendor proceeds to the next stage.

---

# Phase 6 — Information Security Assessment

The Information Security team performs a comprehensive evaluation of the vendor's cybersecurity controls.

The assessment focuses on multiple security domains.

| Security Domain | Assessment Result |
|-----------------|-------------------|
| Information Security Governance | Satisfactory |
| Identity & Access Management | Strong |
| Multi-Factor Authentication | Implemented |
| Encryption Standards | Strong |
| Vulnerability Management | Satisfactory |
| Patch Management | Strong |
| Security Monitoring | Strong |
| Incident Response Capability | Satisfactory |
| Business Continuity | Strong |
| Cloud Security Architecture | Strong |

Overall, the assessment concludes that the vendor maintains a mature cybersecurity program aligned with recognized industry standards. A small number of improvement opportunities are identified, but no critical weaknesses prevent the engagement from moving forward.

---

# Phase 7 — Residual Risk Assessment

Following completion of due diligence, the TPRM team reassesses the vendor's risk exposure after considering the effectiveness of existing security controls.

| Risk Category | Inherent Risk | Residual Risk |
|---------------|---------------|---------------|
| Information Security | High | Medium |
| Privacy | High | Medium |
| Operational Risk | High | Medium |
| Financial Risk | Medium | Low |
| Compliance Risk | High | Low |
| Business Continuity | Medium | Low |
| Reputational Risk | Medium | Medium |

The assessment demonstrates that the implementation of appropriate security controls has significantly reduced the vendor's overall risk profile.

Although some risks remain, they fall within GlobalBank's approved risk tolerance.

---

# Phase 8 — Risk Treatment Plan

Rather than attempting to eliminate every identified risk, the organization develops a formal risk treatment plan.

The agreed actions include:

- Enforcing Multi-Factor Authentication for privileged accounts.
- Performing quarterly vulnerability reporting.
- Requiring annual independent penetration testing.
- Implementing continuous security monitoring.
- Reviewing SOC 2 reports annually.
- Requiring notification of security incidents within 24 hours.
- Conducting annual Business Continuity testing.
- Performing annual vendor reassessments.

These controls reduce both the likelihood and potential impact of future security incidents.

---

# Phase 9 — Contract Negotiation

Once the technical assessment is completed, the Legal and Procurement teams negotiate the contractual terms governing the vendor relationship.

Key contractual provisions include:

- Confidentiality obligations
- Data ownership
- Data Processing Agreement (DPA)
- GDPR compliance requirements
- Service Level Agreements (SLAs)
- Right-to-audit clause
- Security control obligations
- Incident notification requirements
- Subcontractor approval requirements
- Contract termination procedures
- Secure data destruction requirements

These contractual safeguards ensure that security and compliance responsibilities become legally enforceable obligations throughout the vendor relationship.

---

# Phase 10 — Executive Approval

The completed assessment package is presented to the Third-Party Risk Governance Committee for final review.

The committee evaluates:

- Business justification
- Security assessment results
- Financial assessment
- Privacy assessment
- Compliance review
- Legal review
- Residual risk rating
- Proposed risk treatment plan

Following detailed discussion, executive management determines that the remaining residual risks are acceptable and formally approves the vendor for onboarding.

The approval is documented within the organization's GRC platform together with the official risk acceptance record.

---

# Phase 11 — Vendor Onboarding

With executive approval obtained, the onboarding process begins.

The implementation team performs the following activities:

- Create vendor accounts.
- Configure secure network connectivity.
- Implement Identity and Access Management controls.
- Establish centralized security logging.
- Register the vendor within the enterprise vendor inventory.
- Configure continuous monitoring tools.
- Assign a Vendor Relationship Manager.
- Store assessment documentation within the TPRM repository.
- Schedule annual reassessments.
- Define ongoing performance reporting requirements.

Only after all onboarding activities have been successfully completed is the vendor authorized to process production banking data.

# Practical Reflection

GlobalBank's onboarding process demonstrates how a mature Third-Party Risk Management program balances business objectives with cybersecurity and regulatory requirements. Rather than relying solely on vendor certifications or questionnaires, the organization validates evidence, performs technical assessments, negotiates contractual protections, and applies risk treatment measures before granting the vendor access to critical systems.

This structured approach significantly reduces operational, cybersecurity, compliance, and reputational risks while ensuring that executive management remains accountable for accepting any remaining residual risk.

---

# Key Takeaways

- Comprehensive due diligence validates whether a vendor can securely support critical business operations.
- Security assessments evaluate the effectiveness of technical, administrative, and operational controls.
- Residual risk assessments determine whether vendor risks fall within the organization's approved risk appetite.
- Contractual safeguards reinforce security and compliance obligations throughout the vendor relationship.
- Executive approval provides governance and accountability for accepting residual risks.
- A structured onboarding process ensures that vendors meet business, security, legal, privacy, and regulatory requirements before gaining access to production environments.

> # Continuous Monitoring, Security Incident & Regulatory Response

---

# Learning Objectives

By the end of this section, you will be able to:

- Understand how organizations continuously monitor third-party vendors after onboarding.
- Explain the importance of vendor performance monitoring and security oversight.
- Recognize how organizations respond to third-party security incidents.
- Understand regulatory reporting obligations during vendor-related incidents.
- Identify the importance of continuous risk reassessment throughout the vendor lifecycle.
- Apply practical Third-Party Risk Management (TPRM) principles in an operational environment.

---

# Introduction

Following successful onboarding, GlobalBank's cloud provider begins hosting several customer-facing banking applications. Although the vendor has successfully completed due diligence and contractual negotiations, Third-Party Risk Management does not end once the contract is signed.

Vendor risks continuously evolve due to changes in technology, business operations, emerging cyber threats, regulatory requirements, and organizational changes. As a result, GlobalBank implements continuous monitoring processes to ensure that the vendor continues to operate securely and remains compliant throughout the duration of the relationship.

This phase illustrates how mature organizations actively monitor third-party risks rather than relying solely on periodic assessments.

---

# Phase 12 — Continuous Vendor Monitoring

The Vendor Relationship Manager works closely with the Security Operations Center (SOC), Information Security, Procurement, and Compliance teams to continuously monitor the vendor's performance and security posture.

Monitoring activities include:

- Service availability monitoring.
- Security event monitoring.
- SLA performance tracking.
- Vulnerability monitoring.
- Patch management verification.
- Regulatory compliance reviews.
- Financial health monitoring.
- Annual security reassessments.
- Continuous threat intelligence monitoring.
- Review of independent audit reports.

Monitoring results are reviewed monthly by the TPRM Governance Committee.

---

# Phase 13 — Vendor Performance Monitoring

GlobalBank establishes Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) to measure the vendor's operational performance.

| Performance Metric | Target | Current Status |
|--------------------|---------|----------------|
| System Availability | 99.95% | 99.98% |
| Incident Response Time | < 30 Minutes | 22 Minutes |
| Critical Patch Deployment | < 7 Days | 5 Days |
| Backup Success Rate | 100% | 100% |
| Recovery Test Success | 100% | Successful |
| SLA Compliance | ≥ 99% | 99.6% |

The vendor consistently meets contractual obligations and continues to support critical banking operations.

---

# Phase 14 — Security Incident

Eight months after deployment, the Security Operations Center detects unusual outbound network traffic originating from one of the vendor-hosted application servers.

Automated monitoring tools generate high-priority alerts, prompting immediate investigation.

The investigation reveals:

- Unauthorized access attempts targeting an application server.
- Exploitation of a recently disclosed software vulnerability.
- Suspicious outbound communications to an external IP address.
- No evidence of customer data exfiltration.
- No impact on financial transactions.

Although the incident is quickly contained, GlobalBank activates its Third-Party Incident Response Plan.

---

# Phase 15 — Coordinated Incident Response

GlobalBank and the cloud provider establish a joint incident response team.

The coordinated response includes:

- Isolating affected systems.
- Blocking malicious network traffic.
- Conducting forensic analysis.
- Deploying emergency security patches.
- Reviewing system logs.
- Validating backup integrity.
- Monitoring customer-facing services.
- Updating executive management.

The incident is successfully contained within several hours without affecting customer banking services.

---

# Phase 16 — Regulatory Notification

Because the affected systems process customer information, GlobalBank evaluates whether regulatory notification is required.

The Privacy Office, Legal Department, and Compliance Team jointly assess:

- Whether personal data was compromised.
- Potential regulatory reporting obligations.
- Customer notification requirements.
- Contractual notification timelines.
- Internal reporting procedures.

Following forensic investigation, the organization concludes that no personal data was compromised. Consequently, regulatory notification is not legally required; however, executive management and internal governance committees receive comprehensive incident reports.

---

# Phase 17 — Vendor Risk Reassessment

Following closure of the incident, the TPRM team performs an unscheduled vendor reassessment.

Areas reviewed include:

- Patch management effectiveness.
- Vulnerability management process.
- Security monitoring capabilities.
- Incident response maturity.
- Root cause analysis.
- Corrective action implementation.
- Business continuity readiness.
- Security governance.

The reassessment confirms that the vendor has implemented corrective measures and strengthened several internal security controls.

The vendor's overall residual risk remains within GlobalBank's approved risk appetite.

---

# Lessons Learned Workshop

GlobalBank conducts a formal lessons learned session involving all stakeholders.

Key improvements identified include:

- Faster vulnerability intelligence integration.
- Enhanced continuous monitoring rules.
- Improved threat intelligence sharing.
- More frequent penetration testing.
- Stronger executive reporting.
- Improved vendor communication procedures.
- Updated incident response playbooks.
- Enhanced contractual security requirements.

These improvements are incorporated into the organization's continuous improvement program.

# Practical Reflection

The cloud provider experienced a cybersecurity incident despite completing extensive due diligence before onboarding. Rather than indicating a failure of the TPRM program, the incident demonstrated the effectiveness of GlobalBank's continuous monitoring and incident response capabilities.

Early detection by the Security Operations Center, coordinated collaboration with the vendor, and well-defined governance processes enabled the organization to contain the threat quickly, protect customer services, and implement corrective actions without significant operational disruption.

This illustrates an important principle of Third-Party Risk Management: organizations cannot eliminate all vendor risks, but they can significantly reduce their impact through continuous oversight, proactive governance, and effective incident management.

---

# Key Takeaways

- Third-Party Risk Management continues throughout the vendor relationship and does not end after onboarding.
- Continuous monitoring enables organizations to detect emerging vendor risks early.
- Vendor performance should be measured using operational, security, and compliance metrics.
- Third-party security incidents require coordinated response between the organization and the vendor.
- Incident investigations should include regulatory impact assessments and formal risk reassessments.
- Lessons learned and corrective actions strengthen the overall maturity of the TPRM program and improve future resilience.
