# Introduction

Third-party risk does not end once a vendor has been approved or a contract has been signed. A vendor's cybersecurity posture, financial condition, regulatory compliance, operational performance, and business environment can change over time. New cyber threats emerge, regulations evolve, technologies change, and organizations may introduce new services that increase overall risk.

For these reasons, Third-Party Risk Management (TPRM) requires organizations to continuously monitor vendors throughout the entire business relationship rather than relying solely on periodic assessments.

Continuous vendor monitoring is an ongoing governance process that helps organizations identify changes in vendor risk, verify that security controls remain effective, and ensure vendors continue to meet contractual, regulatory, and business requirements.

---

# What is Continuous Vendor Monitoring?

Continuous vendor monitoring is the ongoing process of evaluating a vendor's risk posture, security performance, compliance status, and operational effectiveness throughout the vendor lifecycle.

Continuous monitoring may include:

- Security monitoring.
- Compliance reviews.
- Performance monitoring.
- Risk reassessments.
- Financial monitoring.
- Incident tracking.
- Audit reviews.
- Regulatory monitoring.

The objective is to identify significant changes before they become business risks.

---

# Purpose of Continuous Monitoring

Continuous monitoring helps organizations:

- Detect emerging risks.
- Verify ongoing compliance.
- Monitor vendor performance.
- Protect critical business services.
- Strengthen cybersecurity oversight.
- Support regulatory expectations.
- Improve operational resilience.
- Enable informed risk-based decisions.

Monitoring allows organizations to respond proactively instead of reacting after problems occur.

---

# Why Continuous Monitoring is Important

Vendor risks change over time for many reasons.

Examples include:

- New cybersecurity threats.
- Changes in vendor ownership.
- Financial instability.
- New regulatory requirements.
- Service expansions.
- Technology changes.
- Security incidents.
- Organizational restructuring.

Without continuous monitoring, these changes may remain undetected until they significantly impact the organization.

---

# What Should Be Monitored?

Organizations should monitor multiple aspects of vendor performance and risk.

| Monitoring Area | Example |
|------|------|
| Cybersecurity | Security incidents, vulnerabilities, external attack surface |
| Compliance | Regulatory compliance status and certifications |
| Operational Performance | SLA performance and service availability |
| Financial Health | Bankruptcy risk, financial stability |
| Business Continuity | Disaster recovery testing and resilience |
| Privacy | Personal data protection compliance |
| Contracts | Expiring agreements and contractual obligations |
| Risk Ratings | Changes in inherent or residual risk |

The monitoring scope should align with the vendor's criticality and risk level.

---

# Monitoring Frequency

Not all vendors require the same monitoring frequency.

Organizations commonly adopt a risk-based approach.

| Vendor Risk Level | Typical Monitoring Frequency |
|------|------|
| Critical | Continuous or monthly |
| High | Quarterly |
| Medium | Semi-annually |
| Low | Annually |

Higher-risk vendors require more frequent oversight and reassessment.

---

# Trigger Events

Certain events should trigger an immediate vendor review, regardless of the scheduled assessment cycle.

Examples include:

- Major cybersecurity incidents.
- Regulatory investigations.
- Data breaches.
- Significant service outages.
- Contract changes.
- Business acquisitions or mergers.
- Financial distress.
- Discovery of significant control weaknesses.

Trigger-based monitoring enables organizations to respond quickly to changing risks.

---

# Relationship with Vendor Lifecycle Management

Continuous monitoring supports every stage of the vendor lifecycle.

It contributes to:

- Vendor onboarding.
- Risk reassessments.
- Contract renewals.
- Performance evaluations.
- Executive reporting.
- Regulatory compliance.
- Vendor offboarding.
- Continual improvement.

Monitoring ensures vendor risk management remains active throughout the relationship.

---

# Common Challenges

Organizations may experience several challenges when implementing continuous monitoring.

| Challenge | Description |
|------|------|
| Large Vendor Population | Monitoring hundreds of vendors requires significant resources |
| Limited Visibility | Organizations may not receive timely information from vendors |
| Manual Processes | Monitoring activities become slow and inconsistent |
| Alert Fatigue | Excessive notifications reduce response effectiveness |
| Rapid Risk Changes | Vendor risk may evolve faster than assessment cycles |
| Resource Constraints | Limited personnel affect monitoring effectiveness |

Automation and risk-based prioritization help address these challenges.

---

# Best Practices

Organizations should:

- Establish a formal continuous monitoring program.
- Prioritize monitoring based on vendor criticality and risk.
- Define measurable monitoring objectives.
- Combine automated and manual monitoring activities.
- Monitor cybersecurity, operational, financial, and compliance risks.
- Document significant vendor risk changes.
- Perform reassessments following trigger events.
- Integrate monitoring results into enterprise GRC platforms.
- Report significant risks to senior management.
- Continuously improve monitoring processes based on lessons learned.

---

# Practical Example

A global financial services company relies on a cloud provider to host customer-facing applications. Although the provider successfully completed onboarding and security due diligence, the organization continues to monitor the vendor throughout the contract period.

The TPRM team reviews monthly service availability reports, tracks security advisories, monitors the vendor's ISO/IEC 27001 certification status, reviews quarterly SOC 2 reports, and receives alerts regarding publicly disclosed vulnerabilities. When a significant vulnerability affecting the provider's cloud platform is announced, the organization immediately performs a targeted risk assessment and confirms that remediation has been completed within the agreed timeframe. Continuous monitoring enables the organization to identify emerging risks quickly and maintain confidence in the vendor's security posture.

---

# Key Takeaways

- Continuous vendor monitoring is an ongoing process that extends throughout the vendor lifecycle.
- Vendor risks evolve over time and require regular oversight.
- Monitoring should include cybersecurity, operational, financial, compliance, and contractual risks.
- Risk-based monitoring frequencies help organizations allocate resources effectively.
- Trigger events require immediate reassessment regardless of scheduled review cycles.
- Continuous monitoring strengthens Third-Party Risk Management by enabling proactive identification and management of evolving vendor risks.

# Introduction

Traditional vendor risk assessments provide valuable information at a specific point in time, but cybersecurity risks evolve continuously. New vulnerabilities, ransomware campaigns, data breaches, phishing attacks, and software supply chain attacks can significantly change a vendor's risk profile between formal assessments.

To improve visibility into these changing risks, organizations increasingly use security ratings and cyber threat intelligence as part of their continuous monitoring programs. These tools provide ongoing insights into a vendor's external cybersecurity posture and help organizations identify potential issues before they result in business disruption.

Although security ratings and threat intelligence cannot replace detailed security assessments, they provide valuable early warning indicators that support proactive risk management.

---

# What are Security Ratings?

Security ratings are independent evaluations of an organization's external cybersecurity posture based on observable security indicators.

Security ratings commonly evaluate:

- Internet-facing systems.
- Domain security.
- SSL/TLS configuration.
- Email security.
- Vulnerability exposure.
- Patch management.
- Network configuration.
- Publicly known security incidents.

Security ratings help organizations identify potential cybersecurity weaknesses without requiring direct access to vendor systems.

---

# Purpose of Security Ratings

Organizations use security ratings to:

- Monitor vendor cybersecurity posture.
- Identify emerging risks.
- Prioritize vendor reviews.
- Support risk-based monitoring.
- Compare vendors consistently.
- Detect security deterioration.
- Support procurement decisions.
- Improve executive reporting.

Security ratings provide continuous visibility into external security performance.

---

# What is Threat Intelligence?

Threat intelligence is the collection, analysis, and sharing of information about current and emerging cyber threats.

Threat intelligence helps organizations understand:

- Active threat actors.
- Malware campaigns.
- Ransomware activity.
- Exploited vulnerabilities.
- Supply chain attacks.
- Data breach notifications.
- Industry-specific threats.
- Emerging attack techniques.

Threat intelligence enables organizations to anticipate risks rather than simply responding after incidents occur.

---

# Sources of Threat Intelligence

Organizations obtain threat intelligence from multiple trusted sources.

Common sources include:

- Government cybersecurity agencies.
- National Computer Security Incident Response Teams (CSIRTs).
- Commercial threat intelligence providers.
- Security vendors.
- Industry information-sharing organizations.
- Open-source intelligence (OSINT).
- Security research organizations.
- Internal security operations centers (SOCs).

Combining multiple intelligence sources provides broader visibility into evolving cyber threats.

---

# Using Security Ratings in Vendor Monitoring

Security ratings can support several monitoring activities.

Examples include:

- Identifying declining security posture.
- Detecting exposed internet services.
- Monitoring patch management effectiveness.
- Tracking configuration weaknesses.
- Supporting periodic vendor reassessments.
- Triggering targeted security reviews.
- Supporting executive dashboards.
- Prioritizing remediation efforts.

Security ratings should be interpreted alongside other assessment information.

---

# Limitations of Security Ratings

Organizations should understand the limitations of security ratings.

Examples include:

- Only externally visible systems are evaluated.
- Internal security controls cannot be fully assessed.
- Ratings may not reflect recent improvements.
- Different providers use different methodologies.
- False positives may occur.
- Security ratings do not measure governance maturity.
- Ratings cannot replace independent security assessments.
- Vendor cooperation may still be required for investigation.

Security ratings should complement—not replace—traditional risk assessments.

---

# Integrating Threat Intelligence

Threat intelligence strengthens continuous monitoring by identifying new risks affecting vendors.

Organizations may use intelligence to:

- Identify vendors affected by new vulnerabilities.
- Monitor ransomware campaigns.
- Track software supply chain attacks.
- Evaluate emerging regulatory risks.
- Prioritize incident response.
- Update vendor risk ratings.
- Trigger reassessments.
- Support executive decision-making.

Timely intelligence improves organizational preparedness and resilience.

# Common Challenges

Organizations may encounter several challenges when using security ratings and threat intelligence.

| Challenge | Description |
|------|------|
| False Positives | Reported issues may not accurately reflect actual risk |
| Different Rating Methodologies | Providers may produce different results for the same vendor |
| Limited Internal Visibility | External monitoring cannot assess internal security controls |
| Information Overload | Large volumes of intelligence require effective prioritization |
| Rapid Threat Evolution | New threats emerge faster than traditional assessment cycles |
| Resource Constraints | Limited personnel may delay analysis and response |

Combining automated monitoring with expert analysis improves decision-making.

---

# Best Practices

Organizations should:

- Use security ratings as one input to vendor risk assessments.
- Subscribe to trusted threat intelligence sources.
- Monitor high-risk vendors more frequently.
- Validate significant findings before taking action.
- Correlate security ratings with vendor questionnaires and audit reports.
- Update vendor risk ratings when material changes occur.
- Establish escalation procedures for critical threats.
- Integrate monitoring tools with enterprise GRC and security platforms.
- Review monitoring effectiveness periodically.
- Continuously improve monitoring criteria as threats evolve.

---

# Practical Example

A multinational retail company continuously monitors its payment processing provider using external security ratings and commercial threat intelligence feeds. During routine monitoring, the organization's security platform detects that one of the vendor's internet-facing systems is running software affected by a newly disclosed critical vulnerability.

At the same time, threat intelligence reports indicate that attackers are actively exploiting the vulnerability in the financial services sector. The TPRM team immediately contacts the vendor to verify its exposure and requests evidence of remediation. After the vendor applies the required security updates and confirms successful validation, the organization closes the monitoring alert and updates the vendor's assessment record. By combining security ratings with threat intelligence, the organization identifies and addresses emerging cyber risks before they affect business operations.

---

# Key Takeaways

- Security ratings provide continuous visibility into a vendor's external cybersecurity posture.
- Threat intelligence helps organizations identify emerging cyber threats affecting third-party vendors.
- Security ratings should complement—not replace—traditional security assessments.
- External monitoring enables organizations to detect changing risks between formal assessments.
- Threat intelligence supports faster, risk-based decision-making and incident response.
- Integrating security ratings and threat intelligence into continuous monitoring strengthens Third-Party Risk Management and improves organizational resilience.

# Introduction

Cybersecurity is only one aspect of Third-Party Risk Management. Even if a vendor maintains strong security controls, poor service quality, missed deadlines, operational failures, or repeated contractual breaches can expose an organization to significant business risks.

Vendor performance monitoring is the ongoing process of measuring how effectively a vendor delivers products and services against agreed expectations. It enables organizations to identify declining performance, verify compliance with Service Level Agreements (SLAs), and address issues before they affect business operations.

An effective performance monitoring program combines operational metrics, risk indicators, regular reviews, and corrective actions to ensure vendors continue to meet business objectives throughout the relationship.

---

# What is Vendor Performance Monitoring?

Vendor performance monitoring is the continuous evaluation of a vendor's ability to deliver products or services in accordance with contractual, operational, and business requirements.

Performance monitoring typically evaluates:

- Service quality.
- SLA compliance.
- Operational efficiency.
- Incident management.
- Customer satisfaction.
- Regulatory compliance.
- Risk management.
- Continuous improvement.

Regular monitoring helps organizations maintain strong vendor relationships while reducing operational and business risks.

---

# Purpose of Vendor Performance Monitoring

Organizations monitor vendor performance to:

- Verify contractual compliance.
- Measure service quality.
- Identify declining performance.
- Support business continuity.
- Improve vendor accountability.
- Reduce operational risk.
- Support contract renewal decisions.
- Strengthen governance.

Performance data provides objective evidence for vendor management decisions.

---

# Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)

KPIs measure how effectively vendors deliver agreed services.

Common KPIs include:

| KPI | Example Measurement |
|------|------|
| Service Availability | 99.9% system uptime |
| Incident Response Time | Average time to acknowledge incidents |
| Incident Resolution Time | Average time to resolve incidents |
| Service Request Completion | Percentage completed within target time |
| Change Success Rate | Successful implementation of approved changes |
| Customer Satisfaction | Survey results or satisfaction scores |
| Project Delivery | Percentage of milestones completed on schedule |
| SLA Compliance | Percentage of service levels achieved |

KPIs focus on operational and service performance.

---

# Key Risk Indicators (KRIs)

KRIs measure factors that may indicate increasing vendor risk.

Examples include:

| KRI | Example Indicator |
|------|------|
| Security Incidents | Number of reported security events |
| Critical Vulnerabilities | Unresolved high-risk vulnerabilities |
| Regulatory Findings | Number of compliance issues identified |
| Audit Findings | Outstanding audit observations |
| Service Interruptions | Frequency of unplanned outages |
| Financial Stability | Declining financial performance |
| Staff Turnover | Loss of key technical personnel |
| Contract Breaches | Repeated contractual violations |

KRIs provide early warning signs of potential problems.

---

# Performance Review Activities

Organizations should perform regular vendor performance reviews.

Typical activities include:

- Reviewing SLA reports.
- Evaluating KPI trends.
- Assessing KRIs.
- Reviewing incident reports.
- Analyzing customer feedback.
- Evaluating corrective actions.
- Conducting vendor meetings.
- Updating vendor risk ratings.

Regular reviews help maintain transparency and accountability.

---

# Performance Reporting

Performance information should be communicated to relevant stakeholders.

Reports may include:

- KPI dashboards.
- KRI summaries.
- SLA compliance reports.
- Executive scorecards.
- Incident trend analysis.
- Risk assessment updates.
- Audit results.
- Improvement recommendations.

Consistent reporting supports informed governance decisions.

---

# Corrective Actions

When vendor performance falls below expectations, organizations should initiate corrective actions.

Possible actions include:

- Vendor notification.
- Root cause analysis.
- Performance improvement plans.
- Additional monitoring.
- Contract amendments.
- Executive escalation.
- Financial penalties where applicable.
- Contract termination if performance does not improve.

Corrective actions should be tracked until successful completion.

---

# Common Challenges

Organizations may encounter several challenges when monitoring vendor performance.

| Challenge | Description |
|------|------|
| Poorly Defined KPIs | Performance cannot be measured consistently |
| Inaccurate Reporting | Vendor data may be incomplete or inconsistent |
| Lack of Regular Reviews | Performance issues remain unresolved |
| Excessive Metrics | Too many indicators reduce management focus |
| Weak Corrective Action Tracking | Improvement activities are not completed |
| Changing Business Requirements | Existing KPIs no longer reflect organizational priorities |

A structured governance process helps address these challenges.

---

# Best Practices

Organizations should:

- Define measurable KPIs and KRIs aligned with business objectives.
- Monitor performance against agreed SLAs.
- Conduct regular vendor performance review meetings.
- Use dashboards to visualize performance trends.
- Investigate recurring service issues promptly.
- Document corrective actions and monitor completion.
- Integrate performance monitoring with vendor risk management.
- Share performance reports with relevant stakeholders.
- Adjust performance metrics as business requirements evolve.
- Use performance results to support contract renewal and strategic sourcing decisions.

---

# Practical Example

A telecommunications company contracts a managed network services provider to support critical infrastructure across multiple regions. The organization monitors monthly KPIs, including network availability, incident response time, change success rate, and customer satisfaction, alongside KRIs such as unresolved critical vulnerabilities and repeated service outages.

After three consecutive months of declining network availability and increasing incident resolution times, the vendor is required to present a performance improvement plan. Additional monitoring is introduced, and monthly executive review meetings are scheduled to track progress. Within the following quarter, service levels return to agreed targets, demonstrating how structured performance monitoring enables organizations to identify issues early and improve vendor performance before significant business disruption occurs.

---

# Key Takeaways

- Vendor performance monitoring evaluates how effectively vendors deliver products and services throughout the relationship.
- KPIs measure operational performance, while KRIs identify emerging risks.
- Regular reviews, dashboards, and reporting improve accountability and governance.
- Corrective actions help vendors address performance deficiencies before they become major business issues.
- Performance monitoring supports informed contract renewal, vendor management, and risk management decisions.
- A mature vendor performance monitoring program strengthens Third-Party Risk Management by promoting continual improvement and operational resilience.
