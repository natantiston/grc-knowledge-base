# Introduction

After an Information Security Management System (ISMS) has been planned, implemented, and operated, an important question remains:

> **Is the ISMS actually working?**

This is the purpose of **Clause 9 – Performance Evaluation**.

Implementing security controls alone does not guarantee that information is adequately protected. Organizations must regularly evaluate whether their controls, processes, and the ISMS itself are achieving their intended outcomes.

Clause 9 represents the **"Check"** phase of the **Plan-Do-Check-Act (PDCA)** cycle.

It requires organizations to:

- Monitor security performance.
- Measure the effectiveness of security controls.
- Analyze operational results.
- Conduct internal audits.
- Perform management reviews.
- Use performance data to improve the ISMS.

Without performance evaluation, organizations cannot determine whether their information security investments are delivering the expected results. Clause 9 ensures that decisions are based on objective evidence rather than assumptions. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

---

# Why Clause 9 Is Important

Many organizations successfully implement security controls but never verify whether those controls remain effective.

For example:

- Firewalls may be deployed but never reviewed.
- Employees may complete awareness training, yet phishing success rates remain high.
- Security incidents may continue increasing despite new technologies.
- Risk treatment activities may be completed without measuring their effectiveness.

Clause 9 addresses these issues by requiring organizations to evaluate both **information security performance** and the **overall effectiveness of the ISMS**. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

---

# The Three Subclauses of Clause 9

Clause 9 consists of three major activities.

| Subclause | Title | Purpose |
|-----------|-------|---------|
| **9.1** | Monitoring, Measurement, Analysis and Evaluation | Determine whether the ISMS and security controls are performing effectively |
| **9.2** | Internal Audit | Verify conformity with ISO/IEC 27001 and the organization's own ISMS requirements |
| **9.3** | Management Review | Ensure top management evaluates the ISMS and makes strategic decisions for improvement |

Together, these three subclauses provide assurance that the ISMS continues to be suitable, adequate, effective, and aligned with business objectives. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

These clauses form a continuous improvement cycle.

- Control effectiveness.
- Security performance.
- Audit results.
- Management oversight.

↓

↓

This sequence ensures that the ISMS continuously evolves as risks, technologies, and business requirements change.

# Performance Evaluation Is Continuous

Clause 9 is not limited to annual audits.

Organizations should continually monitor and evaluate:

- Security incidents.
- Vulnerability management.
- Patch compliance.
- Access control effectiveness.
- Security awareness performance.
- Supplier security performance.
- Information security objectives.
- Risk treatment effectiveness.

Continuous evaluation enables organizations to detect trends early and respond before issues become significant.

# Practical Example

A healthcare organization has operated its ISO/IEC 27001-certified ISMS for one year.

To evaluate its effectiveness, the organization:

- Reviews monthly security performance dashboards.
- Measures phishing simulation success rates.
- Tracks the percentage of critical vulnerabilities remediated within service level targets.
- Conducts internal audits of key ISMS processes.
- Holds quarterly management review meetings to evaluate security performance and business risks.
- Uses audit findings and performance trends to identify opportunities for improvement.

As a result, management discovers that although technical controls are performing well, employee phishing susceptibility has increased. The organization responds by strengthening its security awareness program and introducing additional phishing simulations.

This demonstrates the intent of Clause 9: using objective performance data to verify that the ISMS is functioning effectively and to drive continual improvement rather than relying on assumptions or one-time compliance activities.

# Clause 9.1 – Monitoring, Measurement, Analysis, and Evaluation

An Information Security Management System (ISMS) cannot be effectively managed without understanding how well it is performing.

Organizations need objective evidence to determine whether:

- Security controls are operating effectively.
- Information security objectives are being achieved.
- Risks remain within acceptable levels.
- Improvement initiatives are producing the expected results.

This is the purpose of **Clause 9.1 – Monitoring, Measurement, Analysis, and Evaluation**.

Clause 9.1 requires organizations to determine:

- **What** needs to be monitored and measured.
- **Which methods** will be used.
- **When** monitoring and measurement will occur.
- **When** results will be analyzed and evaluated.
- **Who** will analyze and evaluate the results.

The objective is not simply to collect security data but to convert that data into meaningful information that supports informed management decisions. ([iso27001.com](https://iso27001.com/standard/clauses/9-performance-evaluation/?utm_source=chatgpt.com))

---

# Purpose of Clause 9.1

The primary objective is to determine whether the ISMS is:

- Suitable.
- Adequate.
- Effective.
- Achieving its intended outcomes.

Monitoring and measurement allow organizations to identify trends, verify the effectiveness of controls, and detect opportunities for continual improvement before problems become significant.

---

# Monitoring vs Measurement

Although often used together, monitoring and measurement are different activities.

### Monitoring

Monitoring is the ongoing observation of security activities and processes.

Examples include:

- Reviewing security logs.
- Monitoring firewall activity.
- Observing SIEM alerts.
- Tracking security incidents.
- Monitoring backup jobs.
- Watching system availability.

Monitoring answers:

> **"What is happening?"**

---

### Measurement

Measurement assigns values to determine performance.

Examples include:

- Number of phishing incidents.
- Percentage of systems patched.
- Mean Time to Detect (MTTD).
- Mean Time to Respond (MTTR).
- Training completion rate.
- Number of audit findings.

Measurement answers:

> **"How well are we performing?"**

Monitoring identifies events, while measurement quantifies performance.

---

# What Should Be Monitored?

Each organization determines what is relevant based on:

- Business objectives.
- Risk assessment results.
- Risk Treatment Plan.
- Information security objectives.
- Regulatory requirements.
- Customer expectations.

Common monitoring activities include:

- Security incidents.
- User access.
- Privileged account activities.
- Vulnerability management.
- Patch management.
- Malware detection.
- Backup success.
- Network security events.
- Supplier performance.
- Cloud security events.

Monitoring should focus on activities that significantly affect the ISMS.

---

# What Should Be Measured?

Organizations commonly measure Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) and Key Risk Indicators (KRIs).

Examples include:

| Area | Example KPI |
|------|-------------|
| Patch Management | % of critical vulnerabilities patched within SLA |
| Incident Response | Mean Time to Respond (MTTR) |
| Security Awareness | Employee training completion rate |
| Access Management | Number of overdue access reviews |
| Internal Audit | Number of audit findings closed on time |
| Supplier Security | Percentage of suppliers assessed annually |

These metrics help management evaluate whether security objectives are being achieved.

---

# Determining Measurement Methods

- Internal audit reports.
- Incident management systems.
- Learning Management Systems (LMS).
- Manual compliance reviews.

Measurement methods should be consistent to ensure results remain reliable and comparable over time.

# Analysis and Evaluation

Collecting data alone has little value unless it is analyzed.

Organizations should evaluate:

- Trends over time.
- Achievement of security objectives.
- Changes in risk levels.
- Performance against targets.
- Effectiveness of implemented controls.
- Areas requiring corrective action.

For example:

If phishing simulation failures increase from **6%** to **18%** over six months, management should investigate the cause and consider strengthening awareness training or implementing additional email security controls.

Analysis transforms raw security data into actionable management information.

---

# Evidence Auditors Look For

During an ISO/IEC 27001 certification audit, auditors typically verify that the organization has:

- Defined what will be monitored and measured.
- Established appropriate measurement methods.
- Determined monitoring frequencies.
- Assigned responsibility for analysis and evaluation.
- Used monitoring results to assess ISMS performance.
- Retained documented evidence of monitoring and measurement activities.

Typical evidence includes:

- KPI dashboards.
- Security performance reports.
- SIEM reports.
- Vulnerability scan reports.
- Incident trend reports.
- Risk dashboards.
- Performance review presentations.
- Management review inputs. ([iso27001.com](https://iso27001.com/standard/clauses/9-performance-evaluation/?utm_source=chatgpt.com))

# Practical Example

A global telecommunications company defines several information security KPIs to evaluate its ISMS.

Each month, the Information Security Manager reviews:

- Percentage of critical vulnerabilities remediated within 14 days.
- Mean Time to Detect (MTTD) security incidents.
- Mean Time to Respond (MTTR) to incidents.
- Number of successful phishing attacks.
- Percentage of employees completing mandatory security awareness training.
- Number of unresolved audit findings.

The results are presented during monthly security governance meetings.

When management observes that the average incident response time has increased over three consecutive months, additional incident response training is scheduled and staffing levels are reviewed.

By continuously monitoring, measuring, analyzing, and evaluating security performance, the organization demonstrates compliance with Clause 9.1 while ensuring that management decisions are based on objective evidence and that the ISMS continues to improve over time.

# Clause 9.2 – Internal Audit

An Information Security Management System (ISMS) cannot rely solely on monitoring and performance metrics to determine whether it is effective.

Organizations also need an independent assessment to verify that:

- ISMS processes are being followed.
- Security controls are implemented correctly.
- ISO/IEC 27001 requirements are being met.
- Opportunities for improvement are identified before external certification audits.

This is the purpose of **Clause 9.2 – Internal Audit**.

Clause 9.2 requires organizations to conduct **internal audits at planned intervals** to determine whether the Information Security Management System:

- Conforms to the organization's own ISMS requirements.
- Conforms to the requirements of ISO/IEC 27001.
- Is effectively implemented and maintained.

Internal audits act as the organization's own quality assurance mechanism, allowing it to identify weaknesses before they result in security incidents or certification audit findings. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

---

# Purpose of Clause 9.2

The primary objective of internal audits is to provide independent assurance that the ISMS is operating effectively.

Internal audits help organizations:

- Verify compliance with ISO/IEC 27001.
- Confirm that internal policies and procedures are followed.
- Identify nonconformities.
- Detect process weaknesses.
- Verify that corrective actions have been implemented.
- Support continual improvement.

Rather than being viewed as inspections, internal audits should be seen as opportunities to strengthen the ISMS.

---

# Internal Audit vs External Audit

Many people confuse internal audits with certification audits.

Although both evaluate the ISMS, they serve different purposes.

| Internal Audit | External Audit |
|---------------|----------------|
| Conducted by the organization or an independent internal auditor | Conducted by an accredited certification body |
| Required by Clause 9.2 | Required for ISO/IEC 27001 certification |
| Identifies improvement opportunities | Determines certification status |
| Focuses on continual improvement | Focuses on conformity with ISO/IEC 27001 |
| Usually performed several times during the certification cycle | Typically includes Stage 1, Stage 2, and surveillance audits |

Internal audits prepare the organization for successful external certification audits.

---

# The Internal Audit Programme

Clause 9.2 requires organizations to **plan, establish, implement, and maintain** an internal audit programme.

The audit programme should define:

- Audit frequency.
- Audit methods.
- Audit responsibilities.
- Planning requirements.
- Reporting requirements.
- Audit criteria.
- Audit scope.

The audit programme should consider:

- The importance of the processes being audited.
- Changes affecting the organization.
- Results of previous audits.
- Information security risks.

High-risk or high-impact processes may require more frequent audits than lower-risk activities. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

---

# Determining Audit Frequency

ISO/IEC 27001 does **not** prescribe how often internal audits must be performed.

Instead, organizations determine an appropriate schedule based on their circumstances.

Common approaches include:

- Annual ISMS audit.
- Quarterly audits of critical processes.
- Risk-based audit schedules.
- Audits following significant organizational changes.
- Follow-up audits after major nonconformities.

The audit programme should ensure that all ISMS processes are reviewed within an appropriate timeframe.

---

# Audit Criteria and Scope

Each audit should clearly define:

## Audit Criteria

The standards or requirements against which the audit will be performed.

Examples include:

- ISO/IEC 27001 requirements.
- Information Security Policy.
- Internal procedures.
- Regulatory requirements.
- Customer contractual obligations.

---

## Audit Scope

Defines what will be audited.

Examples include:

- Access Management.
- Risk Management.
- Incident Response.
- Human Resources Security.
- Supplier Security.
- Cloud Security.
- Business Continuity.
- Physical Security.

A clearly defined scope ensures that audit objectives remain focused and manageable.

---

# Auditor Independence

One of the most important requirements of Clause 9.2 is **objectivity and impartiality**.

Internal auditors should not audit their own work.

For example:

❌ An Information Security Manager should not audit the ISMS processes they designed.

✅ Another qualified internal auditor, an auditor from another department, or an independent external resource should perform the audit.

Independence improves the credibility of audit findings and reduces conflicts of interest. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

# Types of Audit Findings

Internal audits generally produce one of four outcomes.

### Conformity

The requirement is fully met and implemented effectively.

---

### Opportunity for Improvement (OFI)

The process complies with requirements but could be enhanced.

---

### Minor Nonconformity

A small deviation that does not significantly affect the effectiveness of the ISMS.

Example:

A document review date has expired, but the procedure is still being followed correctly.

---

### Major Nonconformity

A significant failure that affects the effectiveness of the ISMS.

Example:

Risk assessments have not been performed for newly deployed business-critical systems.

Organizations should investigate nonconformities, identify root causes, implement corrective actions, and verify effectiveness.

---

# Practical Example

A software development company conducts its annual ISO/IEC 27001 internal audit.

The internal audit programme includes audits of:

- Information Security Risk Management.
- Access Control.
- Secure Software Development.
- Incident Response.
- Supplier Management.

An internal auditor from the Quality Assurance department—who was not involved in designing the ISMS—is assigned to audit the Access Control process.

During the audit, the auditor reviews policies, interviews employees, examines access approval records, and verifies user account management procedures.

The audit identifies two findings:

- A **minor nonconformity**, where one contractor account remained active after the contract ended.
- An **opportunity for improvement**, recommending automated reminders for quarterly access reviews.

The audit report is submitted to management, corrective actions are assigned, and a follow-up audit confirms that the issues have been resolved.

By maintaining an independent internal audit programme, the organization demonstrates compliance with Clause 9.2 while ensuring that weaknesses are identified and corrected before they affect business operations or certification outcomes. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

# Clause 9.3 – Management Review

An Information Security Management System (ISMS) requires active leadership to remain effective.

While monitoring activities and internal audits provide valuable operational information, it is **top management** that decides whether the ISMS continues to support the organization's strategic direction, business objectives, and risk appetite.

This is the purpose of **Clause 9.3 – Management Review**.

Clause 9.3 requires **top management** to review the ISMS at planned intervals to ensure that it remains:

- Suitable.
- Adequate.
- Effective.

Unlike operational meetings, a management review is a strategic governance activity where senior leadership evaluates the overall health of the ISMS, reviews its performance, and makes decisions regarding improvements, resources, and future priorities. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

---

# Purpose of Clause 9.3

The primary objective of the management review is to ensure that the ISMS continues to support the organization's business goals while adapting to changes in risks, technology, regulations, and stakeholder expectations.

Management reviews help organizations:

- Evaluate overall ISMS performance.
- Confirm that security objectives remain relevant.
- Review organizational risks.
- Assess resource adequacy.
- Approve improvement initiatives.
- Demonstrate leadership commitment.

This review ensures that information security remains a business issue rather than solely an IT responsibility.

---

# Who Participates?

Management reviews should involve individuals who have the authority to make strategic decisions.

Typical participants include:

- Chief Executive Officer (CEO)
- Chief Information Security Officer (CISO)
- Chief Information Officer (CIO)
- Chief Risk Officer (CRO)
- Compliance Manager
- Information Security Manager
- Business Unit Managers
- Internal Audit Representative (when appropriate)

Top management participation is essential because decisions often involve business priorities, funding, staffing, and organizational risk acceptance. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

---

# How Often Should Management Reviews Be Conducted?

ISO/IEC 27001 does not specify an exact frequency.

Instead, reviews must occur at **planned intervals**.

Common practices include:

- Quarterly.
- Semi-annually.
- Annually.

Organizations operating in highly regulated or rapidly changing environments often conduct management reviews more frequently.

Additional reviews may also be necessary after:

- Major cyber incidents.
- Business acquisitions.
- Significant regulatory changes.
- Major technology transformations.
- Significant organizational restructuring.

---

# Required Inputs to the Management Review

Clause 9.3 specifies the information that management should consider during the review.

Typical inputs include:

### Previous Actions

- Status of actions from earlier management reviews.

### Organizational Changes

- Changes in internal and external issues.
- Changes in interested parties' requirements.

### ISMS Performance

- Monitoring and measurement results.
- Internal audit results.
- Nonconformities and corrective actions.
- Achievement of information security objectives.

### Risk Management

- Results of information security risk assessments.
- Status of the Risk Treatment Plan.

### Feedback

- Feedback from customers.
- Feedback from regulators.
- Feedback from employees.
- Feedback from other interested parties.

### Opportunities

- Opportunities for continual improvement.

These inputs give management a complete view of the current state of the ISMS. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

---

# Outputs of the Management Review

The management review should produce clear decisions and actions.

Typical outputs include:

- Improvement opportunities.
- Changes to the ISMS.
- Updated security objectives.
- Resource allocation decisions.
- Approval of corrective actions.
- Strategic security initiatives.
- Updated priorities for risk treatment.

The review should result in measurable actions rather than simply documenting discussions.

# Evidence Auditors Look For

During an ISO/IEC 27001 certification audit, auditors typically verify that:

- Records of management review meetings are retained.

- Management Review Minutes.

- KPI and performance reports.
- Risk dashboards.
- Internal audit summaries.
- Action registers.

# Practical Example

A multinational financial institution conducts quarterly ISMS Management Review meetings chaired by the Chief Information Security Officer (CISO) and attended by executive leadership.

During one quarterly review, management examines:

- Security performance dashboards showing incident trends.
- Results of the latest internal audit.
- Progress against information security objectives.
- Updates to the Risk Register and Risk Treatment Plan.
- Customer feedback regarding information security.
- The impact of new financial regulations on the ISMS.

The review identifies an increase in phishing-related incidents despite high employee training completion rates.

After discussing the findings, top management approves funding for advanced email threat protection, authorizes additional phishing simulation exercises, and increases the cybersecurity awareness budget for the following year.

The meeting concludes with documented action items, assigned owners, target completion dates, and updated priorities for the ISMS.

By conducting regular management reviews and documenting strategic decisions, the organization demonstrates compliance with Clause 9.3 while ensuring that executive leadership remains actively engaged in governing and continually improving the Information Security Management System.
