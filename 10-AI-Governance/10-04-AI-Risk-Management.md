# Introduction

Artificial Intelligence offers significant opportunities for innovation, operational efficiency, and business transformation. However, as organizations increasingly rely on AI to automate processes and support critical decisions, they also face new categories of risk that traditional risk management approaches may not fully address. Unlike conventional software, AI systems continuously learn from data, adapt to changing environments, and may produce outcomes that are difficult to predict or explain.

ISO/IEC 42001 recognizes that effective AI Governance depends on a structured and risk-based approach. Organizations must identify, assess, evaluate, treat, monitor, and continually review risks associated with AI throughout its entire lifecycle. AI risk management is therefore not a one-time activity performed during development but an ongoing governance process that continues from planning through retirement of the AI system.

A mature AI risk management program helps organizations balance innovation with responsible governance. It enables business leaders to make informed decisions regarding AI adoption while protecting customers, employees, business operations, and organizational reputation. AI risk management also supports compliance with emerging AI regulations, strengthens cybersecurity, improves stakeholder confidence, and promotes the development of trustworthy AI systems.

---

# What is AI Risk Management?

AI Risk Management is the systematic process of identifying, assessing, evaluating, treating, monitoring, and reviewing risks associated with Artificial Intelligence systems.

The objective is to ensure that AI systems:

- Support business objectives.
- Operate safely and reliably.
- Comply with legal and regulatory requirements.
- Protect individuals and organizations.
- Maintain stakeholder trust.
- Continuously improve over time.

Rather than preventing innovation, AI risk management enables organizations to adopt AI confidently while maintaining appropriate governance.

---

# Why AI Risk Management is Important

AI systems introduce risks that differ significantly from traditional information technology systems.

Examples include:

- Incorrect AI recommendations
- Algorithmic bias
- Privacy violations
- Cybersecurity threats
- Model drift
- Hallucinated AI outputs
- Lack of explainability
- Regulatory non-compliance
- Ethical concerns
- Business disruption

Without effective governance, these risks can result in financial losses, legal liabilities, operational failures, and reputational damage.

# Best Practices

Organizations should:

- Integrate AI risk management into enterprise Governance, Risk, and Compliance (GRC) programs.
- Identify AI risks before developing or deploying AI systems.
- Evaluate strategic, operational, cybersecurity, privacy, ethical, and regulatory risks.
- Assign ownership for every identified AI risk.
- Continuously monitor AI systems for emerging threats and changing risk levels.
- Review AI risk assessments whenever significant changes occur.
- Conduct regular management reviews and internal audits.
- Promote continual improvement of AI risk management processes.

---

# Practical Example

A multinational financial services organization introduces AI to automate fraud detection, customer onboarding, and credit risk analysis. Before deployment, the organization performs comprehensive AI risk assessments to identify cybersecurity threats, privacy concerns, model bias, regulatory obligations, operational risks, and potential impacts on customers. Each identified risk is evaluated according to its likelihood and business impact, with appropriate treatment plans assigned to business owners, cybersecurity teams, and AI specialists. High-risk AI systems require approval from the AI Governance Committee before they can enter production.

Following deployment, the organization continuously monitors AI performance through governance dashboards, security monitoring tools, fairness assessments, and regulatory compliance reviews. Internal Audit periodically evaluates the effectiveness of AI risk management processes, while executive management reviews risk reports during governance meetings. As new threats, technologies, and regulations emerge, the organization updates its AI risk assessments and governance controls to maintain responsible AI operations and continual compliance with ISO/IEC 42001.

# AI Risk Identification

The first stage of AI risk management is identifying the risks that could affect the successful development, deployment, operation, and governance of Artificial Intelligence systems. Risk identification establishes the foundation for all subsequent risk management activities. If risks are not properly identified, they cannot be assessed, treated, monitored, or controlled effectively.

Unlike traditional IT systems, AI introduces risks that span multiple disciplines, including cybersecurity, privacy, ethics, legal compliance, operational resilience, data governance, and business strategy. Organizations implementing ISO/IEC 42001 should therefore adopt a comprehensive and multidisciplinary approach to identifying AI-related risks across the entire AI lifecycle.

# Best Practices

Organizations should:

- Establish a formal AI risk identification process integrated with enterprise risk management.
- Involve business, technical, legal, privacy, cybersecurity, and compliance teams in identifying AI risks.
- Maintain and regularly update an enterprise AI Risk Register.
- Consider strategic, operational, cybersecurity, privacy, ethical, financial, and regulatory risks.
- Review AI risks whenever significant changes occur to models, datasets, technologies, or regulations.
- Include third-party AI providers within the scope of risk identification activities.
- Document assumptions, dependencies, and known limitations associated with AI systems.
- Continuously improve risk identification processes using lessons learned from audits, incidents, and governance reviews.

---

# Practical Example

A multinational logistics company deploys AI to optimize delivery routes, forecast customer demand, and automate warehouse operations. Before deployment, the organization conducts AI risk identification workshops involving business leaders, cybersecurity specialists, data scientists, legal advisors, privacy officers, and risk managers. Together, they identify numerous risks, including inaccurate forecasting caused by poor data quality, adversarial attacks targeting AI models, unauthorized access to logistics data, third-party cloud service dependencies, regulatory compliance requirements, and potential operational disruption if AI systems become unavailable. Each identified risk is documented within the enterprise AI Risk Register together with ownership, business impact, and proposed mitigation measures.

Following deployment, AI risks are reviewed quarterly and whenever major changes occur, such as model retraining, expansion into new countries, adoption of additional cloud AI services, or changes to applicable regulations. Governance dashboards provide executives with visibility into newly identified risks, outstanding mitigation activities, and emerging threat trends. By treating risk identification as a continuous governance activity rather than a one-time exercise, the organization maintains a proactive AI risk management program that supports responsible innovation, operational resilience, and ongoing compliance with ISO/IEC 42001.

# AI Risk Assessment and Risk Treatment

After identifying AI-related risks, organizations must evaluate their significance and determine how those risks should be managed. AI risk assessment enables organizations to understand the likelihood and potential impact of identified risks, while risk treatment defines the actions required to reduce those risks to an acceptable level. Together, these activities form the core of AI risk management within ISO/IEC 42001.

Unlike traditional software risks, AI risks are dynamic. AI models continuously evolve through new data, retraining, and changing operational environments. Consequently, organizations should regularly reassess risks throughout the AI lifecycle to ensure governance controls remain effective and aligned with business objectives.

---

# AI Risk Assessment

AI risk assessment is the process of analyzing identified risks to determine their likelihood, potential impact, and overall risk level.

The assessment helps organizations:

- Prioritize AI risks.
- Allocate resources effectively.
- Support informed decision-making.
- Determine appropriate security controls.
- Demonstrate regulatory compliance.
- Improve governance effectiveness.

Risk assessments should be performed before deploying AI systems and repeated whenever significant changes occur.

---

# Assessing Likelihood

Likelihood represents the probability that a particular AI risk will occur.

Factors affecting likelihood include:

- AI model complexity
- Data quality
- Security posture
- Exposure to external users
- Dependence on third-party AI services
- Historical incidents
- Threat intelligence
- Frequency of model updates

Organizations may classify likelihood using qualitative or quantitative methods.

Example:

| Likelihood | Description |
|------------|-------------|
| Very Low | Rare occurrence |
| Low | Unlikely to occur |
| Medium | Possible occurrence |
| High | Likely to occur |
| Very High | Expected to occur frequently |

---

# Assessing Business Impact

Impact measures the potential consequences if an AI risk materializes.

Possible impacts include:

- Financial loss
- Operational disruption
- Regulatory penalties
- Privacy violations
- Cybersecurity incidents
- Loss of customer trust
- Reputational damage
- Safety risks

Organizations should define consistent impact criteria across all AI initiatives.

---

# Risk Matrix

Many organizations use a risk matrix to determine overall risk ratings.

Example:

| Likelihood | Low Impact | Medium Impact | High Impact |
|------------|-----------|---------------|-------------|
| Low | Low | Low | Medium |
| Medium | Low | Medium | High |
| High | Medium | High | Critical |

The resulting risk rating helps prioritize treatment activities and management attention.

---

# AI Risk Treatment

Once risks have been assessed, organizations should determine how each risk will be managed.

Common treatment options include:

- Avoid the risk
- Reduce the risk
- Transfer the risk
- Accept the risk

The selected approach should align with the organization's risk appetite and governance policies.

---

# Examples of Risk Treatment

Typical AI risk treatments include:

| AI Risk | Example Treatment |
|---------|-------------------|
| Model Bias | Retrain models using representative datasets |
| Prompt Injection | Implement input validation and filtering |
| Privacy Risk | Encrypt sensitive data and apply access controls |
| Model Drift | Perform continuous performance monitoring and scheduled retraining |
| Third-Party AI Risk | Conduct vendor due diligence and contractual reviews |
| Unauthorized Access | Implement multi-factor authentication and role-based access control |
| Hallucinated Outputs | Add human review and confidence thresholds |
| Regulatory Risk | Update governance processes to meet legal requirements |

Treatment plans should be documented and monitored until implementation is complete.

---

| Role | Responsibilities |
|------|------------------|

---

# Monitoring Residual Risk

After treatment measures have been implemented, organizations should evaluate the remaining level of risk.

Residual risk represents the level of risk that remains after controls have been applied.

Organizations should:

- Validate implemented controls.
- Monitor risk indicators.
- Review treatment effectiveness.
- Update risk ratings.
- Escalate unacceptable residual risks.
- Obtain management approval when residual risk is accepted.

Residual risk monitoring supports continual improvement and informed governance decisions.

---

- New datasets are introduced
- Business objectives change

Continuous assessment ensures governance remains aligned with the evolving AI landscape.

- Assign clear ownership for every identified AI risk.

---

# Practical Example

A multinational e-commerce company deploys an AI recommendation engine to personalize customer shopping experiences and optimize product suggestions. During the AI risk assessment, the organization identifies several risks, including algorithmic bias, exposure of customer data, prompt injection attacks against customer-facing AI assistants, and declining recommendation accuracy due to model drift. Each risk is evaluated using a standardized likelihood and impact methodology, resulting in prioritized risk ratings. High-risk items are assigned to designated owners, and treatment plans include enhanced access controls, encryption of customer information, bias testing, continuous model monitoring, and periodic retraining using updated datasets.

Following implementation, governance teams regularly review the effectiveness of these controls through performance dashboards, security monitoring, internal audits, and management reviews. Residual risks are reassessed after each model update, and any risks exceeding the organization's defined risk appetite are escalated to the AI Governance Committee for further action. By integrating structured risk assessment and risk treatment into its Artificial Intelligence Management System, the organization strengthens operational resilience, improves regulatory readiness, and supports the responsible deployment of AI in accordance with ISO/IEC 42001.
