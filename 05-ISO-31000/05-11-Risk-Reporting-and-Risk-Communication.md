# Introduction

Risk management cannot be effective if important information remains with only a few individuals.

Executives need strategic risk information to make informed decisions.

Managers need operational risk information to allocate resources and monitor controls.

Employees need timely information to understand their responsibilities and respond appropriately.

Likewise, external stakeholders—including regulators, customers, investors, suppliers, and business partners—may require information about significant organizational risks.

For this reason, **communication and reporting are continuous activities throughout the entire risk management process**, not just activities performed after a risk assessment is completed. ISO 31000 identifies communication, consultation, recording, and reporting as essential components that support informed decision-making and continual improvement. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

# What Is Risk Communication?

Risk communication is the continuous exchange of information about risks between stakeholders.

It includes:

- Sharing information.
- Discussing uncertainties.
- Consulting stakeholders.
- Explaining decisions.
- Receiving feedback.
- Building shared understanding.

Risk communication is a **two-way process**.

It is not simply management telling employees about risks.

Employees, customers, suppliers, regulators, and other stakeholders also provide valuable information that improves organizational decision-making.

---

# What Is Risk Reporting?

Risk reporting is the structured process of documenting and presenting risk information to decision-makers.

Typical risk reports include:

- Enterprise Risk Registers.
- Executive Risk Dashboards.
- Board Risk Reports.
- Compliance Reports.
- Internal Audit Reports.
- Incident Reports.
- Key Risk Indicator (KRI) Reports.
- Project Risk Reports.

Unlike communication, reporting is usually formal, structured, and documented.

---

# Risk Communication vs Risk Reporting

Although closely related, these concepts have different purposes.

| Risk Communication | Risk Reporting |
|--------------------|----------------|
| Continuous dialogue | Formal documentation |
| Two-way exchange | Structured information delivery |
| Encourages consultation | Supports governance and oversight |
| May be informal | Usually follows a standard format |
| Occurs throughout daily operations | Produced periodically or after significant events |

Both activities complement one another and are necessary for effective Enterprise Risk Management.

# Best Practices

Organizations with mature ERM programs typically:

- Promote continuous communication across all business functions.
- Tailor reports for different stakeholder groups.
- Encourage two-way communication and consultation.
- Standardize reporting formats.
- Integrate risk reporting into governance processes.
- Communicate emerging risks promptly.
- Ensure reports support decision-making rather than merely documenting risks.
- Review communication processes regularly for effectiveness.

These practices improve transparency, strengthen governance, and support better risk-informed decisions throughout the organization.

---

# Practical Example

A multinational healthcare organization introduces a new cloud-based electronic medical records system.

During implementation, project managers conduct weekly risk meetings with IT, cybersecurity, compliance, and clinical representatives to discuss emerging risks, treatment progress, and operational impacts. This ongoing dialogue allows stakeholders to identify concerns early, adjust implementation plans, and coordinate mitigation activities.

Each month, the Chief Risk Officer prepares an executive risk dashboard summarizing Key Risk Indicators (KRIs), unresolved cybersecurity vulnerabilities, regulatory compliance issues, and project risks for the executive committee and Board of Directors. At the same time, frontline employees receive simplified awareness updates explaining new security procedures and incident reporting expectations.

By combining continuous risk communication with structured risk reporting, the organization ensures that every stakeholder receives relevant information at the appropriate level of detail, enabling informed decisions and supporting the successful implementation of the project.

# Types of Risk Reports and Effective Risk Reporting Practices

Effective Enterprise Risk Management (ERM) depends on providing the **right information to the right people at the right time**.

Not every stakeholder needs the same level of detail. A Board of Directors requires strategic information, while operational managers need detailed information to manage day-to-day risks.

ISO 31000 emphasizes that **recording and reporting** should support communication, decision-making, accountability, continual improvement, and stakeholder understanding throughout the risk management process. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

Risk reporting enables organizations to:

- Support informed decision-making.

# Common Reporting Mistakes

Organizations often reduce the value of risk reporting by:

- Including excessive detail.
- Reporting too infrequently.
- Using inconsistent formats.
- Focusing only on historical events.
- Ignoring emerging risks.
- Reporting data without recommendations.
- Using technical language that decision-makers cannot easily understand.

Effective reports explain **what the risk means**, **why it matters**, and **what action is recommended**.

# Practical Example

A multinational manufacturing company produces separate risk reports for different levels of management.

Each month, department managers review operational risk reports showing equipment failures, supplier performance, workplace safety incidents, and corrective action status. The Chief Risk Officer consolidates this information into an executive dashboard highlighting enterprise-wide Key Risk Indicators (KRIs), top strategic risks, regulatory issues, and high-priority treatment plans.

# Practical Example

A multinational pharmaceutical company discovers unusual activity in one of its manufacturing systems that could affect product quality and regulatory compliance.

A production engineer immediately reports the issue through the organization's incident management platform. The risk owner assesses the situation and determines that the potential impact exceeds the approved operational risk tolerance. Following the established escalation process, the issue is reported to the plant manager, the Enterprise Risk Committee, and the Chief Risk Officer. Executive management is informed because the incident could disrupt production and trigger regulatory reporting obligations.

At the same time, quality assurance, cybersecurity, legal, and communications teams coordinate through the organization's collaboration platform to investigate the issue and prepare consistent internal and external communications. Daily executive dashboards track the incident until corrective actions are completed. Because the organization has clearly defined communication channels and escalation procedures, the issue is resolved quickly, regulatory obligations are met, and business disruption is minimized.

# Measuring and Continually Improving Risk Communication and Reporting

Effective risk communication and reporting are not one-time activities.

As organizations evolve, new technologies emerge, regulations change, and business strategies shift. Risk information must therefore be continuously monitored, evaluated, and improved to ensure that decision-makers receive timely, accurate, and actionable information.

ISO 31000 emphasizes that communication, consultation, recording, reporting, monitoring, and continual improvement are interconnected activities that support effective risk management and organizational governance. Communication processes should be reviewed regularly to ensure they continue meeting stakeholder needs and organizational objectives.

---

# Why Continuous Improvement Matters

Poor communication can be just as damaging as poor risk management.

Examples include:

- Critical incidents reported too late.
- Important risks not escalated.
- Conflicting information provided to executives.
- Employees unaware of new threats.
- Regulators receiving incomplete reports.
- Lessons learned not shared across the organization.

Continuous improvement helps organizations identify weaknesses before they become major governance failures.

---

# Measuring Communication Effectiveness

Organizations should regularly evaluate whether risk communication is achieving its intended objectives.

Typical evaluation questions include:

- Are risks reported promptly?
- Do executives receive sufficient information?
- Are reports understandable?
- Are escalation procedures followed?
- Do employees understand reporting responsibilities?
- Are stakeholders satisfied with communication?
- Are lessons learned communicated effectively?

The answers help management improve communication processes over time.

# Key Risk Indicators (KRIs)

KRIs help determine whether communication failures are increasing organizational risk.

Examples include:

| KRI | Example Threshold |
|-----|-------------------|
| Overdue Incident Reports | More than 5 per month |
| Late Risk Escalations | More than 3 critical cases |
| Unresolved High Risks | More than 10 overdue treatments |
| Repeat Audit Findings | Increase over previous quarter |
| Policy Acknowledgement | Below 90% completion |

When KRIs exceed approved thresholds, management should investigate the underlying causes and implement corrective actions.

# Key Takeaways

Effective risk communication and reporting require continuous evaluation and improvement.

Organizations should:

- Measure communication effectiveness using KPIs and KRIs.
- Conduct regular management reviews.
- Obtain independent assurance through Internal Audit.
- Learn from incidents and audit findings.
- Improve reporting processes and dashboards.
- Update communication procedures as business conditions change.
- Leverage technology to automate reporting and escalation.
- Continuously strengthen stakeholder engagement and transparency.

By continually improving communication and reporting, organizations enhance governance, strengthen Enterprise Risk Management (ERM), support informed decision-making, and increase organizational resilience.

---

# Practical Example

A multinational technology company conducts an annual review of its enterprise risk communication program. The review combines feedback from executives, employees, Internal Audit, regulators, and business unit managers. While executive dashboards are considered effective, the review identifies delays in escalating third-party cybersecurity risks because different departments use inconsistent reporting templates.

To address the issue, the organization standardizes its risk reporting templates, automates notifications through its GRC platform, and updates escalation procedures. It also introduces new KPIs to measure reporting timeliness and KRIs to monitor overdue risk escalations. Managers receive refresher training on reporting responsibilities, and Internal Audit validates the effectiveness of the new process during the next audit cycle.

Within six months, reporting consistency improves, executive dashboards provide more accurate real-time information, and critical risks are escalated more quickly. As a result, leadership makes faster, better-informed decisions, strengthening governance and aligning the organization's communication practices with the continual improvement principles of ISO 31000.
