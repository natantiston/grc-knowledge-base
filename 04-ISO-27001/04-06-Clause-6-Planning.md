# Introduction

After establishing the organization's context (Clause 4) and leadership commitment (Clause 5), the next step is deciding **how the Information Security Management System (ISMS) will achieve its intended outcomes**.

This is the purpose of **Clause 6 – Planning**.

Planning is where strategy is transformed into action.

Instead of simply identifying security risks, organizations must determine:

- Which risks need to be addressed.
- Which opportunities can improve information security.
- Which security objectives should be achieved.
- How those objectives will be accomplished.
- How future changes to the ISMS will be managed.

Clause 6 is often considered the **heart of ISO/IEC 27001** because it introduces the risk-based planning process that drives every other activity within the ISMS. It requires organizations to establish a risk assessment methodology, perform risk assessments, define risk treatment plans, establish measurable information security objectives, and ensure that changes to the ISMS are planned. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

---

# Why Planning Is Important

Information security cannot rely on reacting to incidents after they occur.

Organizations need a structured planning process that enables them to:

- Anticipate risks.
- Prioritize security investments.
- Allocate resources effectively.
- Meet legal and regulatory obligations.
- Support business objectives.
- Improve security performance over time.

Without proper planning, organizations often implement security controls without understanding whether they address the most significant risks.

Clause 6 ensures that information security activities are driven by business priorities rather than assumptions or isolated technical decisions.

---

# The Three Subclauses of Clause 6

Clause 6 consists of three major requirements.

| Subclause | Title | Purpose |
|-----------|-------|---------|
| **6.1** | Actions to Address Risks and Opportunities | Identify, assess, and treat information security risks and opportunities |
| **6.2** | Information Security Objectives and Planning to Achieve Them | Define measurable security objectives and how they will be achieved |
| **6.3** | Planning of Changes | Ensure changes to the ISMS are planned and controlled |

Together, these three requirements ensure that the ISMS remains proactive, risk-based, and aligned with the organization's strategic objectives. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

---

# Relationship Between Clauses 4, 5, and 6

Clause 6 builds directly upon the work completed in the previous clauses.

- **Clause 4** identifies the organization's context, interested parties, and ISMS scope.
- **Clause 5** provides leadership, governance, and organizational direction.
- **Clause 6** transforms this information into actionable security plans.

This logical progression ensures that planning decisions are based on business needs rather than generic security practices.

For example:

- Business objectives identified in Clause 4 influence security objectives in Clause 6.
- Leadership commitment established in Clause 5 provides the resources needed to implement those plans.
- Risk assessments performed under Clause 6 guide the selection of security controls later implemented under Clause 8.

---

# Risk-Based Thinking

A defining characteristic of ISO/IEC 27001 is its **risk-based approach**.

Instead of requiring every organization to implement the same security controls, the standard requires organizations to identify and evaluate their own information security risks.

Risk-based thinking allows organizations to:

- Focus resources on significant risks.
- Avoid unnecessary controls.
- Make informed business decisions.
- Balance security with operational needs.
- Support continual improvement.

Every major decision within the ISMS should be supported by an understanding of organizational risk.

# Practical Example

Consider a healthcare organization implementing ISO/IEC 27001.

After defining its business context and obtaining executive commitment, the organization begins planning its ISMS.

It identifies ransomware, unauthorized access to patient records, and third-party supplier failures as significant information security risks.

Based on these findings, the organization:

- Develops a formal risk assessment methodology.
- Creates a risk treatment plan.
- Establishes objectives such as reducing phishing-related incidents by 50% within one year.
- Plans future changes to accommodate a new cloud-based electronic medical records system.

By following Clause 6, the organization ensures that its information security program is **planned, measurable, risk-based, and aligned with business objectives**, creating a solid foundation for the operational activities introduced in the following lessons.

# Clause 6.1 – Actions to Address Risks and Opportunities

Clause **6.1** is the foundation of the risk management process within ISO/IEC 27001.

It requires organizations to identify and address the **risks and opportunities** that could affect the Information Security Management System (ISMS).

Unlike traditional approaches that focus only on responding to security incidents, ISO/IEC 27001 promotes **proactive risk management**. Organizations are expected to anticipate potential problems, take preventive action, and identify opportunities that can improve the effectiveness of the ISMS. Clause 6.1 also requires organizations to plan how these actions will be integrated into ISMS processes and how their effectiveness will be evaluated. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

---

# Purpose of Clause 6.1

The objective of Clause 6.1 is to ensure that the ISMS:

- Achieves its intended outcomes.
- Prevents or reduces undesirable effects.
- Supports continual improvement.
- Responds to changing business and threat environments.
- Uses a structured, risk-based decision-making process.

Rather than implementing security controls based on assumptions or industry trends, organizations should base their decisions on the results of a formal information security risk assessment.

---

# Risks vs Opportunities

Clause 6.1 requires organizations to consider both **risks** and **opportunities**.

Although they are related, they have different purposes.

| Risks | Opportunities |
|--------|---------------|
| May negatively affect the ISMS | May improve the ISMS |
| Require treatment or mitigation | Should be evaluated and leveraged |
| Can cause security incidents or business disruption | Can improve efficiency, resilience, or compliance |
| Usually reduce business objectives if unmanaged | Usually enhance business objectives when implemented |

Examples of risks include:

- Ransomware attacks.
- Insider threats.
- Data breaches.
- System outages.
- Third-party supplier failures.
- Regulatory non-compliance.

Examples of opportunities include:

- Implementing Zero Trust Architecture.
- Automating security monitoring.
- Adopting cloud-native security controls.
- Improving employee awareness training.
- Deploying Artificial Intelligence for threat detection.
- Strengthening supplier security assessments.

Considering opportunities helps organizations improve security performance rather than focusing solely on preventing negative events.

---

# The Three Parts of Clause 6.1

Clause 6.1 is divided into three sections.

| Subclause | Purpose |
|-----------|---------|
| **6.1.1** | General requirements for identifying risks and opportunities |
| **6.1.2** | Information Security Risk Assessment |
| **6.1.3** | Information Security Risk Treatment |

Together, these subclauses create a complete risk management process that begins with identifying risks and ends with selecting appropriate security controls. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

---

# Clause 6.1.1 – General Requirements

Before performing a formal risk assessment, organizations must consider the information identified in previous clauses.

Specifically, they should use:

- Internal and external issues identified in Clause 4.1.
- Interested party requirements identified in Clause 4.2.
- The defined ISMS scope.
- Organizational objectives.
- Leadership direction.
- Applicable legal and contractual requirements.

These inputs help ensure that the risk assessment reflects the organization's actual operating environment rather than using a generic list of cybersecurity threats.

---

# Planning Actions

Once risks and opportunities have been identified, the organization must determine:

- What actions will be taken.
- Who is responsible.
- When the actions will be completed.
- How the actions will be integrated into ISMS processes.
- How effectiveness will be measured.

Planning should be documented so that responsibilities and progress can be monitored throughout the ISMS lifecycle.

# Evidence Auditors Look For

During an ISO/IEC 27001 audit, auditors typically verify that the organization has:

- Considered internal and external issues.
- Considered interested-party requirements.
- Identified relevant risks and opportunities.
- Established a documented risk assessment methodology.
- Planned actions to address identified risks.
- Defined how these actions will be implemented.
- Established methods to evaluate the effectiveness of the planned actions.

Auditors also expect to see a clear relationship between identified risks, selected Annex A controls, and the organization's Risk Treatment Plan. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

# Practical Example

A manufacturing company is implementing ISO/IEC 27001.

During Clause 6.1 planning, it identifies several significant risks:

- Ransomware affecting production systems.
- Unauthorized remote access by third-party maintenance vendors.
- Loss of intellectual property through phishing attacks.

The organization also identifies opportunities to improve its ISMS:

- Deploy Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for remote access.
- Introduce Security Information and Event Management (SIEM) for continuous monitoring.
- Automate vulnerability scanning across production environments.
- Expand employee phishing awareness training.

Management develops an action plan that assigns owners, establishes implementation timelines, and defines success metrics for each initiative.

By addressing both risks and opportunities, the organization not only reduces the likelihood of security incidents but also strengthens the maturity, resilience, and effectiveness of its Information Security Management System.

# Clause 6.1.2 – Information Security Risk Assessment

Risk assessment is the core activity of an Information Security Management System (ISMS).

Clause **6.1.2** requires organizations to establish, document, implement, and maintain a **consistent information security risk assessment process**.

The purpose of this process is to identify information security risks, evaluate their significance, assign ownership, and provide the information needed to make informed risk treatment decisions.

Rather than relying on intuition or generic best practices, ISO/IEC 27001 requires organizations to use a **repeatable and evidence-based methodology** that produces consistent, valid, and comparable results over time. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

---

# Purpose of the Risk Assessment

The objective of the information security risk assessment is to answer four important questions:

- **What could happen?**
- **How likely is it to happen?**
- **What would be the impact?**
- **Is the level of risk acceptable?**

The answers enable management to prioritize security efforts and allocate resources where they will have the greatest impact.

Without a formal risk assessment, organizations may spend time and money addressing low-priority issues while overlooking critical risks.

---

# Requirements of Clause 6.1.2

ISO/IEC 27001 requires organizations to define and apply a risk assessment process that:

- Establishes risk assessment criteria.
- Defines risk acceptance criteria.
- Produces consistent and repeatable results.
- Identifies information security risks.
- Identifies risk owners.
- Analyzes risks.
- Evaluates risks against defined criteria.

These requirements ensure that risk management is objective, structured, and aligned with business objectives rather than individual opinions. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

---

# Step 1 – Establish Risk Criteria

Before evaluating risks, the organization must define how risks will be measured.

Typical criteria include:

### Risk Acceptance Criteria

These determine which risks are acceptable and which require treatment.

For example:

| Risk Score | Decision |
|------------|----------|
| 1–4 | Accept |
| 5–9 | Monitor |
| 10–15 | Treat |
| 16–25 | Immediate Action |

Organizations should define these thresholds based on their risk appetite and business objectives.

---

### Assessment Criteria

Organizations should also define:

- Likelihood scale.
- Impact scale.
- Risk scoring method.
- Risk categories.
- Review frequency.

Using standardized criteria helps ensure that repeated assessments produce comparable results.

---

# Step 2 – Identify Information Security Risks

Risk identification begins by identifying assets and determining what could threaten them.

Common assets include:

- Information
- Databases
- Applications
- Cloud services
- Networks
- Employees
- Facilities
- Business processes

Organizations then identify threats and vulnerabilities that could affect these assets.

Example:

| Asset | Threat | Vulnerability |
|--------|---------|---------------|
| Customer Database | Ransomware | Outdated operating system |
| HR System | Unauthorized Access | Weak passwords |
| Email System | Phishing | Lack of user awareness |

The assessment should consider risks affecting the **Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability (CIA)** of information within the defined ISMS scope. Risk owners should also be identified for each risk. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

---

# Step 3 – Analyze Risks

Once risks have been identified, the organization analyzes them by evaluating:

### Likelihood

How likely is the risk to occur?

Example scale:

| Score | Likelihood |
|------:|------------|
| 1 | Rare |
| 2 | Unlikely |
| 3 | Possible |
| 4 | Likely |
| 5 | Almost Certain |

---

### Impact

What would happen if the risk occurred?

Example scale:

| Score | Impact |
|------:|--------|
| 1 | Insignificant |
| 2 | Minor |
| 3 | Moderate |
| 4 | Major |
| 5 | Severe |

---

### Risk Level

A common approach is:

**Risk = Likelihood × Impact**

Example:

Likelihood = **4**

Impact = **5**

Risk Score = **20**

This score allows risks to be ranked according to priority.

# Risk Owners

Every identified risk should have an assigned **risk owner**.

A risk owner is the individual who has the authority and accountability to manage a specific risk.

Typical risk owners include:

- Business Process Owners
- Department Managers
- System Owners
- Asset Owners
- Executive Management

The risk owner is responsible for:

- Reviewing the assessed risk.
- Deciding whether the risk is acceptable.
- Approving treatment actions where necessary.
- Monitoring the risk over time.

Assigning risk ownership ensures accountability and prevents important risks from being overlooked.

---

# Practical Example

A healthcare provider performs an information security risk assessment for its electronic medical records (EMR) system.

**Asset:** Electronic Medical Records (EMR)

**Threat:** Ransomware attack

**Vulnerability:** Unpatched operating system

**Likelihood:** 4 (Likely)

**Impact:** 5 (Severe)

**Risk Score:** 20 (High)

**Risk Owner:** Head of IT Operations

Based on the organization's risk acceptance criteria, a score of **20** exceeds the acceptable threshold and must proceed to **risk treatment**, where appropriate security controls will be selected and implemented.

This structured approach ensures that information security decisions are based on measurable risk rather than assumptions, enabling management to prioritize resources effectively and maintain a consistent, auditable risk management process across the organization.

# Clause 6.1.3 – Information Security Risk Treatment

After risks have been identified, analyzed, and evaluated, the organization must decide **what actions to take**.

This is the purpose of **Clause 6.1.3 – Information Security Risk Treatment**.

Risk treatment is the process of selecting and implementing actions that reduce information security risks to an acceptable level.

While Clause 6.1.2 answers the question:

> **"What are our information security risks?"**

Clause 6.1.3 answers:

> **"What are we going to do about them?"**

ISO/IEC 27001 requires organizations to establish and apply a risk treatment process, select appropriate treatment options, determine the controls needed, compare them against Annex A, produce a **Statement of Applicability (SoA)**, prepare a treatment plan, and obtain approval for residual risks. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

---

# Purpose of Risk Treatment

The objective of risk treatment is to reduce information security risks to a level that the organization is willing to accept.

Rather than attempting to eliminate every risk, organizations should make informed business decisions based on:

- Business objectives.
- Risk appetite.
- Regulatory requirements.
- Available resources.
- Cost versus benefit.
- Operational impact.

This ensures that security controls are both effective and proportionate.

---

# Risk Treatment Options

ISO/IEC 27001 does not prescribe a single way to handle risks.

Organizations generally have four treatment options.

| Treatment Option | Description | Example |
|------------------|-------------|---------|
| **Reduce (Mitigate)** | Implement controls to lower likelihood or impact | Deploy Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) |
| **Avoid** | Eliminate the activity that creates the risk | Stop storing unnecessary sensitive data |
| **Transfer (Share)** | Shift some or all of the risk to another party | Purchase cyber insurance or outsource to a secure provider |
| **Accept (Retain)** | Accept the remaining risk because it falls within the organization's risk appetite | Accept a low-risk system with minimal business impact |

The selected option should be appropriate for the organization's business objectives and supported by management approval where required.

---

# Selecting Security Controls

Once a treatment option has been selected, the organization determines which security controls are necessary.

Controls may come from:

- ISO/IEC 27001 Annex A.
- Other recognized standards.
- Industry frameworks.
- Internal organizational controls.
- Custom-designed security measures.

ISO/IEC 27001 does **not** require organizations to implement every Annex A control.

Instead, controls are selected based on the results of the risk assessment.

This is one of the defining characteristics of the standard—it is **risk-driven rather than checklist-driven**. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

---

# The Statement of Applicability (SoA)

One of the most important outputs of Clause 6.1.3 is the **Statement of Applicability (SoA).**

The SoA is a document that explains:

- Which Annex A controls are applicable.
- Which controls are not applicable.
- Why each control is included or excluded.
- Whether each applicable control has been implemented.

The SoA provides the link between:

- Risk Assessment
- Risk Treatment
- Annex A Controls
- ISMS Implementation

For certification audits, the SoA is one of the most heavily reviewed ISMS documents because it demonstrates that control selection is based on identified risks rather than arbitrary decisions. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

---

# Risk Treatment Plan

Organizations should also create a **Risk Treatment Plan (RTP).**

A typical Risk Treatment Plan includes:

| Risk | Selected Control | Owner | Target Date | Status |
|------|------------------|-------|-------------|--------|
| Ransomware | Endpoint Detection & Response (EDR) | IT Manager | 30 Sept | In Progress |
| Weak Passwords | Multi-Factor Authentication | Infrastructure Manager | 15 Aug | Completed |
| Supplier Risk | Supplier Security Assessment | Procurement Manager | 30 Oct | Planned |

The Risk Treatment Plan helps management monitor implementation and track progress over time.

# Residual Risk

Even after implementing security controls, some level of risk usually remains.

This is known as **residual risk**.

For example:

Initial Risk:

- Likelihood: **5**
- Impact: **5**
- Risk Score: **25 (Critical)**

After implementing:

- Multi-Factor Authentication
- Endpoint Detection & Response
- Employee Awareness Training

Residual Risk:

- Likelihood: **2**
- Impact: **4**
- Risk Score: **8 (Moderate)**

Management must determine whether this remaining level of risk is acceptable based on the organization's defined risk acceptance criteria.

Residual risk should be formally reviewed and approved by the appropriate risk owner. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

---

# Evidence Auditors Look For

During an ISO/IEC 27001 certification audit, auditors typically verify that the organization has:

- A documented risk treatment process.
- Selected appropriate treatment options for identified risks.
- Determined the controls needed to treat each risk.
- Produced a current Statement of Applicability (SoA).
- Created and maintained a Risk Treatment Plan.
- Assigned risk owners.
- Obtained approval for residual risks.
- Implemented the selected controls or documented implementation plans.

Auditors also verify that every significant control can be traced back to an identified risk or another justified business, legal, or contractual requirement.

---

# Practical Example

A financial institution identifies the following risk during its information security risk assessment:

**Risk:** Unauthorized access to online banking systems through compromised user credentials.

**Risk Score:** High

The organization selects the **Reduce (Mitigate)** treatment option.

The following controls are implemented:

- Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA).
- Privileged Access Management (PAM).
- Security monitoring through a SIEM platform.
- User awareness training on phishing attacks.

These controls are documented in the **Statement of Applicability (SoA)** and scheduled within the **Risk Treatment Plan** with assigned owners and target completion dates.

After implementation, the residual risk is reassessed and approved by senior management because it falls within the organization's defined risk appetite.

This structured, risk-based approach ensures that information security investments are aligned with business priorities and provides clear evidence that the organization is managing information security risks in accordance with ISO/IEC 27001.
