# Introduction

After completing the Business Impact Analysis (BIA) and Risk Assessment, an organization knows:

- Which business activities are critical.
- What could disrupt those activities.
- How quickly they must recover.
- Which resources are required.

The next step is determining **how** the organization will maintain or restore critical operations during a disruptive incident.

This is achieved through **Business Continuity Strategies**.

Business continuity strategies provide practical solutions that enable organizations to continue delivering products and services despite disruptions.

---

# What is a Business Continuity Strategy?

A Business Continuity Strategy is a planned approach that enables an organization to:

- Continue critical operations during disruptions.
- Recover within approved recovery objectives.
- Protect people and assets.
- Minimize business impact.
- Support long-term organizational resilience.

Strategies are developed using the outputs of the Business Impact Analysis and Risk Assessment.

---

# Purpose of Business Continuity Strategies

Business continuity strategies help organizations:

- Maintain essential products and services.
- Achieve Recovery Time Objectives (RTOs).
- Meet Recovery Point Objectives (RPOs).
- Reduce operational downtime.
- Protect customers and stakeholders.
- Meet regulatory obligations.
- Improve organizational resilience.

Rather than reacting during an incident, organizations prepare recovery solutions in advance.

---

# Inputs Used to Develop Strategies

Several BCMS activities provide information for selecting appropriate strategies.

These include:

- Business Impact Analysis (BIA).
- Risk Assessment.
- Recovery Time Objectives (RTOs).
- Recovery Point Objectives (RPOs).
- Maximum Tolerable Period of Disruption (MTPD).
- Minimum Business Continuity Objectives (MBCO).
- Organizational risk appetite.
- Legal and regulatory requirements.

Together, these inputs ensure that continuity strategies are aligned with business needs.

---

# Characteristics of Effective Strategies

An effective business continuity strategy should be:

### Practical

The strategy should be realistic and achievable.

---

### Cost-Effective

Recovery capability should be appropriate for business requirements.

---

### Scalable

The strategy should support organizational growth.

---

### Flexible

It should adapt to different disruption scenarios.

---

### Tested

Recovery strategies should be validated through exercises.

---

### Sustainable

Strategies should remain effective over time through regular maintenance and improvement.

### Information Strategies

Examples:

# Best Practices

Organizations should:

- Base continuity strategies on validated Business Impact Analysis and Risk Assessment results.
- Align recovery solutions with approved Recovery Time Objectives (RTOs) and Recovery Point Objectives (RPOs).
- Consider people, facilities, technology, suppliers, and information when developing strategies.
- Balance resilience requirements with cost and organizational risk appetite.
- Document continuity strategies clearly within the BCMS.
- Validate strategies through regular exercises and testing.
- Review strategies following significant organizational or technological changes.
- Continuously improve strategies using lessons learned from incidents and exercises.

These practices help ensure that business continuity strategies remain practical, effective, and aligned with the organization's evolving business objectives and operational environment.

---

# Practical Example

A multinational retail organization operates physical stores, e-commerce platforms, and regional distribution centers across several countries.

Following its Business Impact Analysis and Risk Assessment, the organization determines that online sales, payment processing, and warehouse operations are its highest-priority services. To support these critical activities, it implements multiple business continuity strategies, including cloud-based failover for e-commerce applications, geographically separated data centers, alternate warehouse facilities, cross-trained logistics personnel, and agreements with secondary transportation providers. Each strategy is selected based on approved Recovery Time Objectives (RTOs), cost-benefit analysis, and operational feasibility.

The selected strategies are documented within the Business Continuity Management System and validated through annual simulation exercises. As the business expands into new markets, management reviews and updates the strategies to address changing risks, technology, and customer expectations, ensuring that the organization maintains a resilient and adaptable continuity capability.

# Developing Business Continuity Strategies for People, Facilities, Technology, and Suppliers

Business continuity strategies should address every resource required to deliver critical products and services.

A disruption may affect employees, facilities, technology, suppliers, information, or a combination of these resources simultaneously. Therefore, organizations should develop integrated continuity strategies that ensure critical operations can continue even when one or more resources become unavailable.

ISO 22301 encourages organizations to develop continuity solutions based on the results of the Business Impact Analysis (BIA), Risk Assessment, and approved recovery objectives.

---

# Strategy Categories

Business continuity strategies are commonly grouped into five major categories.

```
Business Continuity Strategies

├── People
├── Facilities
├── Technology
├── Information
└── Suppliers
```

Each category supports a different aspect of organizational resilience.

---

# People Strategies

Employees are often the most important resource during a disruption.

Organizations should prepare for situations where key personnel are unavailable due to illness, natural disasters, labor disputes, travel restrictions, or other emergencies.

Examples of people strategies include:

- Cross-training employees.
- Succession planning.
- Remote work arrangements.
- Temporary staffing contracts.
- Flexible work schedules.
- Job rotation.
- Emergency contact procedures.
- Employee wellness and support programs.

Cross-training is particularly valuable because it reduces dependence on individual employees and improves operational flexibility.

---

# Facility Strategies

Physical locations may become unavailable because of fires, floods, earthquakes, utility failures, or security incidents.

Organizations should identify alternate methods of maintaining operations.

Common facility strategies include:

- Alternate office locations.
- Hot, warm, or cold recovery sites.
- Shared office agreements.
- Mobile command centers.
- Backup warehouses.
- Distributed operations.
- Emergency workspace arrangements.

Facility strategies should align with the Recovery Time Objectives (RTOs) established during the Business Impact Analysis.

---

# Technology Strategies

Technology often supports nearly every critical business process.

Organizations should implement resilient technology solutions capable of meeting business recovery requirements.

Examples include:

- High-availability infrastructure.
- Redundant network connections.
- Virtualized environments.
- Cloud disaster recovery.
- Automated failover.
- Data replication.
- Backup systems.
- Cybersecurity monitoring.
- Endpoint protection.
- Identity and access management.

Technology strategies should support both business continuity and information security objectives.

---

# Information Strategies

Organizations should protect:

- Customer information.

- Contracts.

Common information continuity strategies include:

- Regular backups.
- Off-site storage.

- Version control.
- Secure document repositories.
- Data encryption.
- Information classification.

Protecting information ensures that recovery teams can resume business activities quickly and accurately.

# Supplier Strategies

- Manufacturing.

# Matching Strategies to Recovery Objectives

Continuity strategies should support approved recovery requirements.

Example:

| Business Requirement | Example Strategy |
|----------------------|------------------|
| RTO: 30 minutes | Active-active data center |
| RTO: 2 hours | Hot recovery site |
| RTO: 8 hours | Warm recovery site |
| RTO: 24 hours | Remote work and manual procedures |
| RTO: 72 hours | Standard backup restoration |

The shorter the required recovery time, the more resilient—and often more costly—the strategy.

# Best Practices

Organizations should:

- Develop continuity strategies across all critical resource categories rather than focusing only on technology.
- Align recovery solutions with approved Recovery Time Objectives (RTOs) and business priorities.
- Reduce single points of failure by implementing redundant people, facilities, technology, and supplier capabilities.
- Evaluate strategies based on cost, feasibility, resilience, and regulatory requirements.
- Integrate business continuity strategies with information security, enterprise risk management, and other management systems.
- Review strategies after major organizational, technological, or supplier changes.
- Validate continuity strategies through regular exercises and scenario-based testing.
- Update strategies using lessons learned from incidents and post-exercise reviews.

These practices help organizations build comprehensive continuity capabilities that address all critical resources required to maintain and recover business operations during disruptive events.

---

# Practical Example

A global healthcare provider operates hospitals, diagnostic laboratories, and telemedicine services across multiple countries.

To support uninterrupted patient care, the organization develops integrated business continuity strategies across five resource categories. It cross-trains clinical staff and establishes agreements with temporary medical staffing agencies to address workforce shortages. Alternate treatment facilities and mobile medical units are prepared for facility disruptions. Technology resilience is enhanced through cloud-based electronic health records, redundant network connections, and geographically separated data centers. Critical patient records are protected through encrypted backups and real-time replication, while multiple pharmaceutical suppliers and logistics partners reduce dependency on any single vendor.

By combining these people, facility, technology, information, and supplier strategies, the healthcare provider is able to continue delivering essential medical services during natural disasters, cyberattacks, and supply chain disruptions while meeting patient safety requirements and regulatory obligations.

# Selecting and Implementing Business Continuity Solutions

Developing business continuity strategies is only the first step. Organizations must also determine which solutions are the most appropriate, implement them effectively, and ensure they are capable of supporting critical business operations during a disruption.

ISO 22301 emphasizes that continuity solutions should be based on business requirements identified during the Business Impact Analysis (BIA) and Risk Assessment, rather than on assumptions or technology preferences.

The objective is to implement practical, cost-effective, and resilient solutions that enable the organization to recover within its approved recovery objectives.

---

# From Strategy to Solution

A strategy defines the overall approach to maintaining business operations.

A solution is the specific capability or control implemented to support that strategy.

For example:

| Strategy | Business Continuity Solution |
|----------|------------------------------|
| Remote workforce | Secure VPN and collaboration platform |
| Alternate workplace | Fully equipped recovery office |
| Data protection | Automated cloud backup and replication |
| Supplier resilience | Secondary qualified supplier |
| Network resilience | Redundant Internet Service Providers (ISPs) |

Strategies provide direction, while solutions provide the operational capability.

---

# Criteria for Selecting Solutions

Organizations should evaluate potential solutions using consistent criteria.

Common considerations include:

- Ability to meet Recovery Time Objectives (RTOs).
- Ability to meet Recovery Point Objectives (RPOs).
- Cost of implementation and maintenance.
- Operational complexity.
- Scalability.
- Availability of skilled personnel.
- Regulatory and contractual requirements.
- Compatibility with existing infrastructure.
- Long-term sustainability.

The selected solution should provide the required level of resilience without introducing unnecessary complexity or cost.

---

# Technology Continuity Solutions

Technology is often central to business continuity.

Examples include:

### High Availability (HA)

Critical systems operate continuously through redundant components.

Examples:

- Server clustering.
- Load balancing.
- Redundant storage.
- Multiple network paths.

---

### Disaster Recovery (DR)

Technology environments are restored following major disruptions.

Examples:

- Secondary data centers.
- Cloud disaster recovery.
- Infrastructure as Code (IaC).
- Automated system restoration.

---

### Backup and Recovery

Organizations should protect critical data through:

- Full backups.
- Incremental backups.
- Differential backups.
- Immutable backups.
- Offline backups.

Backup frequency should support approved Recovery Point Objectives (RPOs).

---

### Cloud Resilience

Cloud services provide additional continuity capabilities.

Examples include:

- Multi-region deployments.
- Auto-scaling.
- Geographic redundancy.
- Cloud storage replication.
- Managed disaster recovery services.

Cloud technologies can improve resilience but should still be assessed for availability, security, and vendor dependency.

---

# Facility Continuity Solutions

Typical solutions include:

- Hot recovery sites.
- Warm recovery sites.
- Cold recovery sites.
- Shared office facilities.
- Temporary workspaces.

# Workforce Continuity Solutions

# Cost-Benefit Analysis

Business continuity solutions should provide value relative to their cost.

Example:

| Solution | Cost | Recovery Benefit |
|----------|------|------------------|
| Active-active data centers | Very High | Near-zero downtime |
| Hot recovery site | High | Rapid recovery |
| Warm recovery site | Medium | Moderate recovery speed |
| Cold recovery site | Low | Longer recovery time |
| Manual recovery procedures | Very Low | Limited resilience |

Organizations should balance investment with business criticality and risk appetite.

---

# Implementing Business Continuity Solutions

Implementation typically includes:

5. Train personnel.

7. Test implemented solutions.
8. Monitor performance and maintain solutions.

Implementation should be documented and integrated into the Business Continuity Management System (BCMS).

# Practical Example

A multinational financial services organization identifies its online banking platform as one of its most critical business services. The Business Impact Analysis establishes a Recovery Time Objective (RTO) of one hour and a Recovery Point Objective (RPO) of five minutes. To meet these requirements, the organization evaluates several technology solutions, including active-active data centers, cloud disaster recovery, and traditional backup restoration.

Following a cost-benefit analysis, management selects a multi-region cloud architecture with automated failover, real-time database replication, and immutable backups. The implementation includes staff training, updates to Business Continuity Plans, and scheduled failover exercises. Annual testing demonstrates that the platform consistently meets its recovery objectives, providing confidence that the selected solutions can maintain customer banking services during major technology failures or cyber incidents.

# Maintaining, Testing, and Continually Improving Business Continuity Strategies

Implementing business continuity strategies is not the final stage of a Business Continuity Management System (BCMS).

Business continuity capabilities must be maintained, tested, evaluated, and continually improved to ensure they remain effective as business operations, technology, and risks evolve.

ISO 22301 requires organizations to verify that continuity strategies continue to support business requirements and approved recovery objectives throughout the lifecycle of the BCMS.

---

# Why Business Continuity Strategies Must Be Maintained

Business environments change continuously.

Examples include:

- New products and services.
- Organizational restructuring.
- Technology upgrades.
- Cloud migrations.
- Supplier changes.
- Regulatory changes.
- Workforce changes.
- Emerging cyber threats.
- Climate-related risks.

If continuity strategies are not reviewed regularly, they may no longer provide adequate protection for critical business activities.

---

# Strategy Maintenance Activities

Organizations should establish a structured maintenance program.

Typical activities include:

- Reviewing continuity strategies.
- Updating recovery documentation.
- Validating recovery resources.
- Reviewing supplier capabilities.
- Updating contact information.
- Verifying technology configurations.
- Confirming alternate facility readiness.
- Reviewing workforce availability.

Maintenance should occur according to a defined schedule and after significant organizational changes.

---

# Testing Business Continuity Strategies

Testing provides confidence that recovery solutions will function during an actual disruption.

Testing should confirm that:

- Recovery objectives can be achieved.
- Resources remain available.
- Personnel understand their responsibilities.
- Recovery procedures are practical.
- Technology operates as expected.
- Communication processes are effective.

Testing should be appropriate to the organization's size, complexity, and risk profile.

---

# Types of Business Continuity Tests

Organizations commonly perform several types of testing.

### Documentation Review

Participants review Business Continuity Plans to confirm that procedures remain accurate and complete.

---

### Tabletop Exercise

Key personnel discuss how they would respond to a simulated incident without disrupting normal operations.

Tabletop exercises validate decision-making and communication processes.

---

### Simulation Exercise

Teams respond to a realistic disruption scenario using actual procedures.

Examples include:

- Cyberattack simulations.
- Power outage scenarios.
- Supply chain disruptions.
- Pandemic response exercises.

---

### Technical Recovery Test

Technology teams verify recovery capabilities.

Examples include:

- Backup restoration.
- Database recovery.
- Failover testing.
- Network recovery.

---

### Full Operational Exercise

The organization performs a comprehensive test involving people, facilities, technology, suppliers, and communications.

These exercises provide the highest level of confidence but also require significant planning and resources.

---

# Evaluating Test Results

Following each exercise, organizations should evaluate:

- Were Recovery Time Objectives (RTOs) achieved?
- Were Recovery Point Objectives (RPOs) achieved?
- Were communication procedures effective?
- Were personnel adequately trained?
- Were sufficient resources available?
- Did suppliers perform as expected?
- Were recovery procedures practical?
- What improvements are required?

Every exercise should result in documented observations and recommendations.

---

# Lessons Learned

Exercises and real incidents provide valuable learning opportunities.

Organizations should document:

- Successful practices.
- Process weaknesses.
- Technology limitations.
- Communication issues.
- Resource shortages.
- Supplier performance.
- Improvement opportunities.

Lessons learned should be incorporated into future planning and strategy updates.

---

# Best Practices

Organizations should:

- Maintain business continuity strategies as living components of the BCMS rather than static documents.
- Conduct regular reviews and update strategies following organizational, technological, or regulatory changes.
- Validate continuity capabilities through a combination of documentation reviews, tabletop exercises, simulations, and technical recovery tests.
- Measure strategy performance using defined KPIs, including recovery objectives and exercise outcomes.
- Document lessons learned from incidents and exercises and integrate them into future planning.
- Ensure senior management actively supports continuity strategy reviews and continual improvement.
- Coordinate business continuity strategy maintenance with other management systems, including information security and enterprise risk management.
- Apply the PDCA cycle to ensure business continuity capabilities remain effective, resilient, and aligned with organizational objectives.

These practices help organizations sustain effective business continuity strategies, strengthen organizational resilience, and ensure that recovery capabilities remain reliable in the face of evolving business conditions and disruptive events.

---

# Practical Example

An international manufacturing company operates multiple production facilities across different countries. To maintain resilience, it schedules quarterly reviews of its business continuity strategies and conducts annual simulation exercises involving production, logistics, IT, and executive management. During one exercise simulating a regional power outage, the organization discovers that contact information for an alternate logistics provider is outdated and that recovery procedures for a recently upgraded manufacturing system are incomplete.

Following the exercise, corrective actions are assigned to update supplier records, revise the Business Continuity Plan, retrain relevant personnel, and test the revised recovery procedures. Management reviews the exercise results, approves additional investments in backup power infrastructure, and monitors the completion of improvement actions. By continually maintaining, testing, and refining its continuity strategies, the organization strengthens its ability to sustain critical manufacturing operations and meet customer commitments during future disruptions.
