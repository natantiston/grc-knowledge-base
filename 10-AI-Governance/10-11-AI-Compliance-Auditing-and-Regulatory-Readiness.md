# Introduction

As Artificial Intelligence becomes increasingly embedded in business operations, governments and regulators worldwide are introducing new legal and regulatory requirements to ensure that AI systems are developed and used responsibly. Organizations must be prepared to demonstrate that their AI systems operate in a lawful, ethical, transparent, secure, and accountable manner. Compliance is therefore no longer limited to information security or privacy—it now extends to the governance of AI itself. Organizations that fail to establish effective AI compliance programs may face regulatory penalties, legal disputes, operational disruptions, and reputational damage.

ISO/IEC 42001 provides organizations with a structured Artificial Intelligence Management System (AIMS) that supports regulatory readiness through documented governance processes, risk management, operational controls, monitoring, internal audits, management reviews, and continual improvement. While ISO/IEC 42001 is a voluntary international standard, its governance framework helps organizations demonstrate due diligence and align with evolving AI regulations, including regional laws, sector-specific requirements, and contractual obligations. By integrating compliance into AI governance, organizations strengthen trust, improve audit readiness, and support responsible AI adoption.

# Practical Example

A multinational financial institution deploys AI systems to support fraud detection, credit risk assessment, and customer service automation across multiple countries. To maintain regulatory compliance, the organization establishes an enterprise AI compliance program led by its AI Governance Committee, Legal & Compliance department, Privacy Office, Information Security team, and Internal Audit function. The program includes continuous monitoring of new AI regulations, periodic compliance assessments, documented governance policies, AI risk assessments, employee training, and centralized compliance reporting. Governance processes ensure that every AI system undergoes regulatory and legal review before deployment and is continuously monitored for ongoing compliance.

As AI regulations evolve in the jurisdictions where the organization operates, the compliance team updates governance policies, revises operational procedures, and coordinates additional risk assessments to address new legal requirements. Internal Audit independently verifies that governance controls remain effective and that documentation demonstrates compliance with applicable obligations. Executive management reviews compliance performance during scheduled management meetings and allocates resources to address identified gaps. Through this structured compliance framework, the organization strengthens regulatory readiness, reduces legal risk, and maintains an Artificial Intelligence Management System aligned with ISO/IEC 42001.

# AI Regulatory Requirements and Compliance Obligations

Artificial Intelligence regulations are evolving rapidly as governments and regulatory authorities seek to ensure that AI systems are safe, transparent, accountable, and respectful of fundamental rights. Unlike traditional information technology regulations that primarily focus on cybersecurity or privacy, AI regulations address the entire lifecycle of AI systems, including governance, risk management, data quality, human oversight, transparency, documentation, and ongoing monitoring. Organizations must therefore establish governance processes capable of adapting to an increasingly complex regulatory environment.

ISO/IEC 42001 helps organizations prepare for regulatory compliance by providing a structured management system for AI governance. Although the standard itself is voluntary, its risk-based approach, documented governance processes, operational controls, internal audits, and continual improvement activities support organizations in demonstrating due diligence and readiness for applicable legal and regulatory requirements. A mature Artificial Intelligence Management System (AIMS) enables organizations to respond efficiently as new AI regulations emerge across different industries and jurisdictions.

# Practical Example

A multinational healthcare technology company develops AI solutions that assist hospitals with clinical decision support, diagnostic imaging, and patient scheduling across several countries. Because each jurisdiction has different legal requirements for healthcare, privacy, cybersecurity, and Artificial Intelligence, the organization maintains a centralized regulatory register managed by its Legal & Compliance department. Before deploying any AI solution, multidisciplinary teams perform compliance assessments covering governance controls, privacy protections, cybersecurity safeguards, human oversight requirements, and documentation obligations. The results are reviewed by the AI Governance Committee before deployment approval is granted.

Following implementation, regulatory specialists continuously monitor legislative updates, guidance issued by regulators, and changes to contractual obligations with healthcare providers. When new requirements are introduced, governance policies, operational procedures, employee training, and AI risk assessments are updated accordingly. Internal Audit verifies that compliance activities are properly documented and that AI systems continue to meet applicable obligations throughout their lifecycle. This proactive approach enables the organization to remain regulatory-ready while maintaining an Artificial Intelligence Management System aligned with ISO/IEC 42001 and supporting responsible AI deployment across multiple jurisdictions.

# AI Internal Audits and Compliance Assessments

Internal audits and compliance assessments are essential components of an effective Artificial Intelligence Management System (AIMS). They provide independent assurance that AI governance processes, risk management activities, operational controls, and compliance obligations are functioning as intended. Through regular audits and assessments, organizations can identify weaknesses, verify the effectiveness of implemented controls, demonstrate regulatory compliance, and drive continual improvement across the AI governance program.

ISO/IEC 42001 requires organizations to evaluate the performance of their AI governance framework through planned internal audits and management reviews. Internal audits should be objective, evidence-based, and conducted by competent personnel who are independent of the activities being audited whenever practical. The results should be reported to management, corrective actions should be tracked, and governance processes should be improved based on audit findings and organizational learning.

# Best Practices

Organizations should:

- Establish a risk-based internal audit program for AI governance.
- Ensure auditors possess appropriate competence and independence.
- Review governance, cybersecurity, privacy, ethical, and compliance controls during audits.
- Maintain comprehensive audit documentation and objective evidence.
- Prioritize corrective actions based on risk and business impact.
- Verify the effectiveness of corrective actions before closing audit findings.

- Use audit results to strengthen governance maturity and support continual improvement.

---

# Practical Example

A multinational pharmaceutical company uses AI to accelerate drug discovery, monitor manufacturing quality, and support clinical research. As part of its annual audit program, the Internal Audit department performs a risk-based review of the organization's Artificial Intelligence Management System. The audit evaluates AI governance policies, risk assessments, model validation activities, cybersecurity controls, privacy protections, documentation quality, third-party AI providers, and compliance with internal governance requirements. Auditors interview key personnel, review governance records, examine technical evidence, and assess whether human oversight controls are operating effectively for high-impact AI applications.

The audit identifies several opportunities for improvement, including inconsistent documentation of model retraining activities and delayed reviews of third-party AI suppliers. Management develops corrective action plans, assigns responsible owners, updates operational procedures, and provides additional training to relevant teams. Follow-up audits verify that corrective actions have been successfully implemented and that governance controls have improved. By integrating internal audits with continual improvement activities, the organization strengthens regulatory readiness, enhances governance maturity, and maintains compliance with ISO/IEC 42001.

# Management Reviews and Regulatory Readiness

An effective Artificial Intelligence Management System (AIMS) requires more than operational controls and internal audits. Executive leadership must periodically review the overall performance of the AI governance program to determine whether it continues to achieve organizational objectives, manage risks effectively, and comply with applicable legal and regulatory requirements. Management reviews provide strategic oversight, ensure accountability, and enable senior leadership to make informed decisions regarding the future direction of AI governance.

ISO/IEC 42001 identifies management review as a key requirement for continual improvement. During these reviews, leadership evaluates audit results, AI risks, governance performance, compliance status, incidents, resource needs, and opportunities for improvement. The outcomes of management reviews support informed decision-making, strengthen regulatory readiness, and ensure that the Artificial Intelligence Management System remains effective as technologies, business priorities, and regulatory expectations evolve.

# Lesson Summary

Management reviews provide executive leadership with the information needed to evaluate the effectiveness of the Artificial Intelligence Management System and ensure that AI governance remains aligned with organizational objectives, regulatory requirements, and stakeholder expectations. By reviewing audit findings, compliance assessments, AI risks, governance performance, corrective actions, and resource requirements, management can make informed decisions that strengthen governance maturity and improve organizational resilience.

Regulatory readiness is achieved through continuous preparation rather than reactive compliance activities. Organizations that maintain comprehensive documentation, perform regular audits, monitor regulatory developments, and continually improve governance processes are better positioned to demonstrate compliance with evolving AI regulations and industry expectations. These practices support the objectives of ISO/IEC 42001 while promoting responsible, transparent, and trustworthy Artificial Intelligence across the enterprise.

# Best Practices

Organizations should:

- Conduct scheduled management reviews with active executive participation.
- Review AI governance performance using KPIs, KRIs, audit findings, and compliance assessments.
- Maintain comprehensive evidence supporting regulatory readiness.
- Monitor changes to AI regulations and evaluate their impact on governance processes.
- Ensure corrective actions are implemented and verified following audits and management reviews.

- Maintain complete documentation demonstrating governance effectiveness.
- Promote continual improvement through leadership commitment, periodic reviews, and proactive governance enhancements.

---

# Practical Example

A multinational telecommunications provider operates AI systems for network optimization, fraud detection, predictive maintenance, and customer service automation across multiple regions. Every quarter, executive management conducts a formal management review of the organization's Artificial Intelligence Management System. The review includes AI risk assessments, KPI and KRI dashboards, internal audit findings, compliance assessment results, cybersecurity incidents, privacy reports, model performance metrics, and updates on emerging AI regulations. Based on these reviews, leadership approves new governance objectives, allocates additional resources for high-risk AI initiatives, and authorizes updates to governance policies and operational procedures.

When a new regional AI regulation introduces additional transparency and documentation requirements, the AI Governance Committee coordinates a gap assessment and updates governance controls accordingly. Employee training is refreshed, AI documentation templates are revised, and internal audits verify that the new requirements have been successfully implemented. During a subsequent regulatory inspection, the organization demonstrates comprehensive governance records, management review minutes, audit reports, and evidence of continual improvement. This proactive approach enables the organization to maintain regulatory readiness, strengthen stakeholder confidence, and sustain compliance with ISO/IEC 42001 while supporting responsible AI innovation.
