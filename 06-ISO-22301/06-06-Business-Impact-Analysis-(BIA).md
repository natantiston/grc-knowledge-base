# Introduction

A Business Continuity Management System (BCMS) cannot protect everything equally.

Organizations have limited resources, time, and personnel. Therefore, they must determine which business activities are most critical and how quickly they need to be restored following a disruption.

This is the purpose of the **Business Impact Analysis (BIA).**

The BIA is one of the most important requirements of ISO 22301 because it provides the information needed to prioritize recovery efforts, allocate resources, and develop effective Business Continuity Plans (BCPs).

Without a BIA, organizations risk focusing on the wrong priorities during a crisis.

---

# What is a Business Impact Analysis?

A Business Impact Analysis is a structured process used to identify:

- Critical business activities.
- Dependencies.
- Potential impacts of disruptions.
- Maximum acceptable downtime.
- Recovery priorities.
- Resource requirements.

The BIA helps answer one of the most important business continuity questions:

> **"If this business activity stops, what happens to the organization?"**

The answer determines how recovery efforts should be prioritized.

---

# Purpose of a BIA

The primary objective of a BIA is to understand the operational and business consequences of disruptions.

Specifically, the BIA helps organizations:

- Identify critical products and services.
- Determine critical business processes.
- Understand operational dependencies.
- Assess business impacts over time.
- Establish recovery priorities.
- Define recovery objectives.
- Support continuity strategy development.

The BIA focuses on **business consequences**, not on identifying threats. Threat identification is addressed separately through risk assessment.

---

# BIA vs Risk Assessment

The BIA and Risk Assessment are closely related but have different purposes.

| Business Impact Analysis | Risk Assessment |
|--------------------------|-----------------|
| Identifies business consequences | Identifies threats and vulnerabilities |
| Focuses on business processes | Focuses on disruption scenarios |
| Determines recovery priorities | Evaluates likelihood and risk |
| Defines recovery objectives | Selects risk treatments |
| Answers "What happens if operations stop?" | Answers "What could cause operations to stop?" |

A BIA tells you **what is most important to recover**, while a Risk Assessment helps determine **what could interrupt those activities and how to reduce that risk**.

Together, they provide the foundation for business continuity planning.

---

# Key Questions Answered by a BIA

A well-executed Business Impact Analysis addresses questions such as:

- Which products and services are critical?
- Which business processes support these services?
- How long can each process remain unavailable?
- What are the financial consequences of downtime?
- What legal or regulatory obligations could be affected?
- Which customers would be impacted?
- What technology, facilities, personnel, and suppliers are required?
- What resources must be recovered first?

These answers enable organizations to make informed recovery decisions.

---

# Inputs to the BIA

A Business Impact Analysis relies on information gathered from across the organization.

Common inputs include:

- Organizational structure.
- Process documentation.
- Service catalogs.
- Organizational objectives.
- Financial reports.
- Regulatory requirements.
- Customer commitments.
- Service Level Agreements (SLAs).
- Existing risk assessments.
- Interviews with business process owners.

Accurate information is essential for producing reliable BIA results.

---

# Typical Outputs of a BIA

The BIA produces information that is used throughout the BCMS.

Common outputs include:

- List of critical business processes.
- Recovery priorities.
- Recovery Time Objectives (RTOs).
- Recovery Point Objectives (RPOs).
- Maximum Tolerable Period of Disruption (MTPD).
- Process dependencies.
- Critical resource requirements.
- Business impact ratings.
- Recommendations for continuity strategies.

These outputs become inputs for continuity planning and disaster recovery.

# Why Organizations Perform BIAs

Organizations conduct Business Impact Analyses to:

- Protect critical business services.
- Improve operational resilience.
- Meet regulatory expectations.
- Support informed investment decisions.
- Reduce recovery time.
- Improve crisis preparedness.
- Protect customers and stakeholders.
- Enhance organizational confidence.

Rather than relying on assumptions, the BIA provides evidence-based recovery priorities.

# Best Practices

Organizations conducting a Business Impact Analysis should:

- Involve business process owners and subject matter experts.
- Base decisions on business objectives rather than technology alone.
- Review the BIA regularly or after significant organizational changes.
- Validate recovery priorities with executive management.
- Document assumptions and supporting evidence.
- Align the BIA with Enterprise Risk Management (ERM) and strategic planning.
- Use standardized assessment criteria across all departments.
- Integrate BIA results into Business Continuity Plans and Disaster Recovery strategies.

These practices ensure that the Business Impact Analysis remains accurate, relevant, and aligned with the organization's evolving operational needs.

# Practical Example

A multinational financial services company provides online banking, payment processing, investment management, and customer support services.

During its annual Business Impact Analysis, business process owners identify online payment processing and customer authentication as the organization's most critical services. Interviews reveal that payment processing depends on cloud infrastructure, identity management systems, telecommunications providers, and third-party fraud detection services. The BIA determines that payment processing has a Recovery Time Objective (RTO) of one hour, a Recovery Point Objective (RPO) of five minutes, and a Maximum Tolerable Period of Disruption (MTPD) of four hours due to significant financial, regulatory, and reputational impacts.

Using these findings, the organization prioritizes investment in redundant payment platforms, automated failover capabilities, and enhanced disaster recovery solutions. The BIA provides the evidence needed to justify resilience investments while ensuring that Business Continuity Plans focus on restoring the organization's most critical services first.
