# Introduction

The most significant enhancement introduced in **NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF) 2.0** is the addition of the **Govern (GV)** Function.

In previous versions of the framework, governance activities were spread across multiple functions. CSF 2.0 recognizes that effective cybersecurity begins with strong governance. Cybersecurity is no longer viewed as solely an IT responsibility—it is an enterprise-wide business issue requiring executive leadership, board oversight, strategic planning, and organizational accountability.

The Govern Function establishes the policies, processes, roles, responsibilities, and decision-making structures needed to manage cybersecurity risk consistently across the organization. It provides the strategic direction that enables the other five CSF Functions—Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, and Recover—to operate effectively.

---

# Purpose of the Govern Function

The Govern Function helps organizations:

- Establish cybersecurity governance structures.
- Align cybersecurity with business objectives.
- Integrate cybersecurity into enterprise risk management.
- Define organizational roles and responsibilities.
- Establish cybersecurity policies.
- Monitor governance performance.
- Support continual improvement.
- Promote accountability throughout the organization.

Governance ensures cybersecurity decisions support long-term business success rather than isolated technical objectives.

---

# Why Governance Matters

Organizations operate in increasingly complex environments involving:

- Cloud computing.
- Artificial Intelligence.
- Operational Technology (OT).
- Internet of Things (IoT).
- Remote work.
- Digital supply chains.
- Third-party service providers.
- Expanding regulatory obligations.

Without governance:

- Security investments become inconsistent.
- Business priorities may conflict with security objectives.
- Responsibilities become unclear.
- Risks remain unmanaged.
- Executive visibility is limited.

Governance establishes clear direction, accountability, and oversight for cybersecurity activities.

Cyber risks can affect:

# Best Practices

Organizations should:

- Establish cybersecurity governance as an enterprise-wide responsibility supported by executive leadership and integrated into overall business strategy.
- Clearly define governance structures, decision-making authority, and accountability for cybersecurity across all organizational levels.
- Align cybersecurity objectives with enterprise risk management processes so that cyber risks are evaluated alongside financial, operational, legal, and strategic risks.
- Develop and maintain comprehensive cybersecurity policies that communicate management expectations and provide direction for operational security activities.
- Regularly review governance performance through executive reporting, internal audits, risk assessments, compliance monitoring, KPIs, and KRIs.
- Integrate cybersecurity supply chain risk management into procurement, vendor management, and contract management processes to reduce third-party risks.
- Foster a security-aware organizational culture by promoting leadership engagement, employee awareness, transparent communication, and continuous learning.
- Periodically evaluate governance effectiveness to ensure it remains aligned with evolving business objectives, emerging cyber threats, technological changes, and regulatory requirements.

These practices create a strong governance foundation that enables organizations to manage cybersecurity strategically, improve resilience, and support continual improvement across the enterprise.

---

# Practical Example

A multinational financial institution operates retail banking, investment services, cloud-based customer platforms, and international payment systems. Executive leadership recognizes that cybersecurity cannot be managed solely by the IT department and establishes a formal cybersecurity governance program based on the Govern Function of the NIST Cybersecurity Framework 2.0. A board-level risk committee assumes oversight of cybersecurity risk, the Chief Information Security Officer (CISO) is assigned enterprise-wide authority for cybersecurity governance, and cybersecurity objectives are incorporated into the organization's enterprise risk management strategy. Policies covering information security, supplier risk management, incident response, and data protection are updated to align with business objectives and regulatory requirements.

To strengthen governance, the organization implements executive dashboards displaying cybersecurity KPIs, KRIs, audit findings, supplier risk metrics, and incident trends. Regular management reviews evaluate cybersecurity performance alongside financial and operational risks, while procurement teams introduce mandatory cybersecurity assessments for critical suppliers. By embedding governance into strategic planning and executive decision-making, the institution establishes a mature cybersecurity program that supports regulatory compliance, improves operational resilience, and enables informed risk-based investment decisions across the enterprise.

# Organizational Context and Cybersecurity Risk Management Strategy (GV.OC & GV.RM)

Effective cybersecurity governance begins with understanding the organization and establishing a structured approach to managing cyber risk. Within the **Govern (GV)** Function of the NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF) 2.0, the first two categories—**Organizational Context (GV.OC)** and **Risk Management Strategy (GV.RM)**—provide the foundation for all cybersecurity planning and decision-making.

Organizations cannot protect what they do not understand. Before selecting security controls or investing in cybersecurity technologies, leadership must understand the organization's mission, critical business services, operating environment, stakeholders, legal obligations, and tolerance for risk. This knowledge allows cybersecurity activities to be aligned with business objectives and enterprise priorities.

# Best Practices

Organizations should:

- Develop a thorough understanding of internal and external organizational context before establishing cybersecurity governance or selecting security controls.
- Identify critical business services, information assets, technology platforms, and operational dependencies to ensure cybersecurity efforts protect what is most important to the organization.
- Consider regulatory obligations, industry expectations, threat intelligence, customer requirements, and third-party dependencies when evaluating cybersecurity risks.
- Define and communicate organizational risk appetite and risk tolerance so that cybersecurity decisions remain consistent across business units and leadership levels.
- Establish a documented cybersecurity risk management strategy that includes risk identification, assessment, prioritization, treatment, monitoring, and communication processes.
- Integrate cybersecurity risk management into the organization's enterprise risk management (ERM) framework to support informed executive decision-making.
- Review organizational context and cybersecurity risks regularly to address changes in business strategy, emerging technologies, evolving cyber threats, and regulatory developments.
- Use risk information to prioritize cybersecurity investments and governance initiatives according to business value and organizational objectives rather than adopting a purely technology-driven approach.

These practices help organizations establish a strong governance foundation where cybersecurity activities consistently support strategic objectives, operational resilience, and enterprise-wide risk management.

# Practical Example

A multinational logistics company manages global shipping operations, warehouse automation systems, cloud-based tracking platforms, and customer logistics portals. Before expanding its digital transformation program, executive leadership performs an assessment of the organization's internal and external context using the NIST Cybersecurity Framework 2.0. Critical business services, transportation management systems, operational technology (OT), cloud providers, regulatory obligations, and key suppliers are identified. Leadership also evaluates geopolitical cyber threats, ransomware trends affecting the transportation sector, and increasing dependence on third-party logistics software vendors.

Based on this assessment, the organization establishes a cybersecurity risk management strategy aligned with its enterprise risk management framework. Leadership defines a low risk appetite for disruptions affecting customer shipments and warehouse operations, while allowing greater flexibility for innovation projects involving artificial intelligence and automation. Cybersecurity risks are assessed using standardized methodologies, prioritized according to business impact, and incorporated into executive risk reporting. This structured approach enables leadership to allocate cybersecurity investments effectively, strengthen supplier governance, improve operational resilience, and ensure that cybersecurity supports long-term business growth and strategic objectives.

# Roles, Responsibilities, Policy, and Oversight (GV.RR, GV.PO & GV.OV)

# Best Practices

Organizations should:

- Clearly define cybersecurity roles, responsibilities, and decision-making authority across all organizational levels to establish accountability and eliminate governance gaps.
- Ensure executive leadership and the Board actively oversee cybersecurity performance and integrate cyber risk into strategic business planning.
- Develop and maintain cybersecurity policies that are approved by leadership, aligned with organizational objectives, and regularly reviewed to address evolving technologies, threats, and regulations.
- Translate high-level policies into detailed procedures, standards, and operational controls that can be consistently implemented throughout the organization.
- Establish executive reporting mechanisms that communicate cybersecurity performance using meaningful business-focused KPIs, KRIs, audit results, and risk indicators.
- Assign cybersecurity responsibilities to business units as well as technical teams to reinforce that cybersecurity is an enterprise-wide responsibility.
- Include cybersecurity requirements, contractual obligations, and governance expectations within third-party supplier management processes.
- Conduct periodic governance reviews to evaluate policy effectiveness, organizational accountability, cybersecurity performance, and opportunities for continual improvement.

These practices strengthen cybersecurity governance by ensuring that leadership maintains visibility, accountability, and strategic control over cybersecurity risk across the organization.

---

# Practical Example

A multinational manufacturing company operates production facilities, cloud-based enterprise systems, industrial control systems (ICS), and a global supplier network. To strengthen cybersecurity governance, executive leadership establishes a formal governance structure based on the NIST Cybersecurity Framework 2.0. The Board of Directors receives quarterly cybersecurity risk reports, executive management approves enterprise cybersecurity policies, and the CISO is responsible for coordinating cybersecurity strategy, operational risk management, and regulatory compliance. Business unit leaders are assigned responsibility for protecting critical manufacturing processes, while all employees complete annual cybersecurity awareness training and follow documented security procedures.

To support executive oversight, the organization develops dashboards that report cybersecurity KPIs such as vulnerability remediation times, security awareness completion rates, supplier assessment status, and incident response performance. KRIs highlight trends including unresolved critical vulnerabilities, high-risk third-party suppliers, and recurring phishing incidents. Governance reviews use this information to prioritize cybersecurity investments, update policies, and improve operational controls. As a result, leadership gains greater visibility into enterprise cyber risk, accountability is clearly defined across the organization, and cybersecurity governance becomes fully integrated into strategic business decision-making.

# Cybersecurity Supply Chain Risk Management (GV.SC) and Governance Maturity

Modern organizations rarely operate in isolation. They depend on cloud service providers, software vendors, managed service providers (MSPs), hardware manufacturers, consultants, outsourcing partners, and numerous third parties to deliver business services. While these relationships enable innovation and efficiency, they also introduce significant cybersecurity risks.

Recognizing this reality, the **Cybersecurity Supply Chain Risk Management (GV.SC)** category is one of the six governance categories within the **Govern (GV)** Function of the NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF) 2.0. Organizations are expected to govern not only their own cybersecurity risks but also those introduced through suppliers and business partners.

---

# Understanding Cybersecurity Supply Chain Risk

A supply chain consists of every external organization that contributes to the delivery, operation, support, or maintenance of business products and services.

Examples include:

- Cloud service providers
- SaaS providers
- Software vendors
- Hardware manufacturers
- Managed Security Service Providers (MSSPs)
- Internet Service Providers (ISPs)
- AI platform providers
- Payment processors
- Logistics partners
- Consultants and contractors

If one supplier experiences a cybersecurity incident, its customers may also be affected.

---

# Common Supply Chain Cybersecurity Risks

Third-party relationships introduce risks such as:

### Unauthorized Access

Suppliers may receive privileged access to:

- Networks
- Cloud platforms
- Applications
- Sensitive data
- Administrative systems

Poor access management increases organizational exposure.

---

### Software Vulnerabilities

Commercial software may contain:

- Security vulnerabilities
- Configuration weaknesses
- Outdated components
- Vulnerable open-source libraries

Organizations remain responsible for managing these risks even when software is purchased from trusted vendors.

---

### Cloud Security Risks

Cloud providers may experience:

- Service outages
- Misconfigurations
- Data exposure
- Insider threats
- Infrastructure attacks

Organizations retain responsibility for managing cloud-related cybersecurity risks under the shared responsibility model.

---

### AI Supply Chain Risks

Organizations increasingly use external AI solutions such as:

- Large Language Models (LLMs)
- AI APIs
- AI development platforms
- Third-party machine learning services

Potential risks include:

- Model poisoning
- Data leakage
- Prompt injection
- Intellectual property exposure
- Lack of transparency
- Regulatory non-compliance

AI supplier governance is becoming an increasingly important part of cybersecurity governance.

---

# Governance Maturity

Cybersecurity governance evolves progressively as organizations strengthen leadership, risk management, policies, oversight, and supplier governance.

A simplified governance maturity model may include:

| Level | Characteristics |
|--------|-----------------|
| Level 1 – Initial | Informal governance with limited executive oversight |
| Level 2 – Managed | Basic governance processes and documented policies |
| Level 3 – Defined | Standardized governance across the organization |
| Level 4 – Measured | Governance performance monitored using KPIs and KRIs |
| Level 5 – Optimized | Governance continuously improved using data, automation, and organizational learning |

Higher maturity levels generally enable stronger cyber resilience and more informed decision-making.

# Best Practices

Organizations should:

- Integrate cybersecurity supply chain risk management into enterprise governance, procurement, vendor management, and risk management processes from the earliest stages of supplier engagement.
- Classify suppliers according to business criticality, data sensitivity, system access, and operational impact so that governance efforts are proportional to the risks they introduce.
- Perform comprehensive security due diligence before engaging suppliers, including reviews of security certifications, compliance history, incident response capabilities, privacy practices, and business continuity arrangements.
- Establish clear contractual security requirements that define cybersecurity responsibilities, audit rights, incident notification obligations, regulatory compliance expectations, and data protection requirements.
- Continuously monitor supplier cybersecurity performance using periodic risk assessments, compliance reviews, external security ratings, audit reports, and threat intelligence to identify emerging risks.
- Consider fourth-party dependencies and shared responsibility models, particularly when using cloud services, managed service providers, and AI platforms.
- Measure cybersecurity governance maturity using objective indicators such as executive oversight, supplier assessment coverage, policy compliance, audit performance, risk management effectiveness, and continual improvement activities.
- Foster a governance culture that emphasizes collaboration, accountability, evidence-based decision-making, and continuous improvement across both internal operations and external supplier relationships.

These practices enable organizations to reduce third-party cyber risk, strengthen governance maturity, improve operational resilience, and support sustainable cybersecurity management across increasingly complex digital ecosystems.

# Practical Example

A multinational telecommunications provider delivers mobile services, cloud hosting, managed security services, and AI-powered customer support across several countries. The organization relies on hundreds of suppliers, including cloud infrastructure providers, software developers, hardware manufacturers, payment processors, and outsourced service partners. Using the NIST Cybersecurity Framework 2.0, leadership establishes a Cybersecurity Supply Chain Risk Management (GV.SC) program that classifies suppliers based on business criticality, data access, and system privileges. Critical suppliers must complete detailed security assessments, demonstrate compliance with recognized standards such as ISO/IEC 27001, agree to contractual security requirements, and participate in annual governance reviews.

Executive leadership receives quarterly dashboards showing supplier risk ratings, assessment completion rates, outstanding remediation actions, and cybersecurity incidents involving third parties. When a cloud service provider experiences a security event, predefined contractual notification requirements enable rapid communication, risk assessment, and coordinated response activities. Governance maturity assessments conducted each year measure leadership engagement, supplier oversight effectiveness, policy compliance, and continual improvement initiatives. By integrating supply chain governance into enterprise cybersecurity management, the organization strengthens operational resilience, reduces third-party cyber risk, and ensures that cybersecurity governance remains aligned with evolving business objectives and an increasingly interconnected digital environment.
