# Introduction

Artificial Intelligence cannot be governed effectively through technology alone. Successful AI Governance requires an organizational framework that defines how AI systems are planned, developed, approved, deployed, monitored, and continuously improved. As organizations adopt AI across multiple business functions, governance becomes increasingly important to ensure consistency, accountability, regulatory compliance, and responsible use of AI.

ISO/IEC 42001 establishes the concept of an **Artificial Intelligence Management System (AIMS)**, which provides organizations with a structured governance framework for managing AI throughout its lifecycle. Similar to other ISO management system standards, the AIMS integrates leadership, governance policies, risk management, operational controls, performance evaluation, and continual improvement into a single management framework.

An effective AI Governance Framework enables organizations to balance innovation with risk management. It ensures AI initiatives align with business objectives, comply with applicable laws and regulations, protect organizational assets, and maintain stakeholder trust. Rather than creating isolated governance processes for each AI project, organizations establish standardized governance practices that can be consistently applied across the enterprise.

# What is an AI Governance Framework?

An AI Governance Framework is a structured set of policies, processes, organizational roles, controls, and oversight mechanisms that guide how Artificial Intelligence systems are managed throughout their lifecycle.

The framework establishes:

- Governance principles
- Leadership responsibilities
- Decision-making processes
- Risk management practices
- Operational controls
- Compliance requirements
- Performance monitoring
- Continual improvement activities

A well-designed framework enables organizations to deploy AI safely, responsibly, and consistently.

# Best Practices

Organizations should:

- Establish a formal AI Governance Framework approved by executive management.
- Integrate AI governance with enterprise Governance, Risk, and Compliance (GRC) programs.
- Define governance policies that apply consistently across all AI initiatives.
- Incorporate risk management, cybersecurity, privacy, and compliance into AI governance activities.
- Apply governance controls throughout the AI lifecycle.
- Periodically review and update the governance framework to address new technologies and regulatory changes.
- Clearly communicate governance expectations to all stakeholders.
- Promote a culture of responsible AI through continuous awareness and training.

# Practical Example

A multinational manufacturing company begins implementing Artificial Intelligence across its production facilities, supply chain operations, and predictive maintenance programs. To ensure consistent governance, executive management establishes an enterprise AI Governance Framework aligned with ISO/IEC 42001. The framework defines governance policies, leadership responsibilities, risk management processes, cybersecurity requirements, privacy controls, model validation procedures, and performance monitoring activities. Every AI initiative must follow standardized governance processes before deployment.

As the organization expands its AI capabilities, the governance framework enables business units to adopt AI consistently while maintaining compliance with corporate policies and regulatory obligations. Executive leadership receives regular governance reports summarizing AI risks, operational performance, compliance status, and improvement initiatives. Internal Audit periodically reviews the effectiveness of the framework, ensuring governance processes continue to evolve alongside new AI technologies and business objectives. By implementing a structured AI Governance Framework, the organization strengthens accountability, improves operational resilience, and supports the responsible adoption of Artificial Intelligence across the enterprise.

# Leadership, Roles, and Responsibilities

An effective AI Governance Framework depends on clearly defined leadership, roles, and responsibilities. While AI technologies are often developed by technical teams, governance is an enterprise-wide responsibility that requires participation from executive leadership, business units, risk management, cybersecurity, legal, compliance, privacy, internal audit, and operational teams. ISO/IEC 42001 emphasizes that leadership must establish governance objectives, allocate resources, assign responsibilities, and promote a culture of responsible AI throughout the organization.

Clearly assigning ownership ensures that every AI system has accountable individuals responsible for its development, deployment, operation, monitoring, and continual improvement. Without defined responsibilities, organizations may experience inconsistent decision-making, ineffective risk management, regulatory non-compliance, and unclear accountability when AI-related incidents occur.

# Segregation of Duties

Organizations should implement segregation of duties to reduce operational and governance risks.

Examples include:

- Developers should not approve their own AI models for production.
- Independent reviewers should validate high-risk AI systems.
- Internal Audit should remain independent from AI operations.
- Risk acceptance should be approved by authorized business owners.
- Governance committees should independently review significant AI initiatives.

Segregation of duties strengthens governance integrity and reduces conflicts of interest.

# Practical Example

A multinational healthcare organization adopts AI to support clinical decision-making, patient scheduling, and operational planning. Executive management establishes an AI Governance Committee composed of leaders from clinical services, cybersecurity, privacy, legal, compliance, risk management, and information technology. Each AI application is assigned a designated business owner responsible for ensuring that the system supports organizational objectives and complies with governance requirements, while AI system owners oversee day-to-day operations, monitoring, and continual improvement.

To strengthen accountability, the organization separates development, validation, deployment, and audit responsibilities across different teams. High-risk AI systems cannot be deployed until they have been independently validated and approved by the AI Governance Committee. Internal Audit periodically evaluates governance effectiveness, while executive management reviews AI performance, risk metrics, and compliance reports during quarterly governance meetings. This clearly defined organizational structure enables the organization to manage AI responsibly, maintain regulatory compliance, and support continual improvement in accordance with ISO/IEC 42001.

# Practical Example

A multinational retail organization deploys AI to forecast inventory demand, personalize customer experiences, and automate supply chain planning. To ensure consistent governance, the company establishes a centralized documentation repository for its Artificial Intelligence Management System. Every AI project must maintain approved governance policies, risk assessments, data documentation, model validation reports, security assessments, deployment approvals, monitoring records, and management review evidence before entering production. Document owners are assigned to maintain accuracy, while automated version control ensures that only approved documentation is available for operational use.

During an ISO/IEC 42001 internal audit, auditors review the organization's AI Governance Framework, AI Risk Register, AI system inventory, model documentation, and corrective action records. Because documentation is complete, current, and centrally managed, the audit team can easily verify governance activities, demonstrate compliance, and identify opportunities for continual improvement. By implementing disciplined documentation and document control practices, the organization strengthens accountability, improves audit readiness, and supports the responsible governance of Artificial Intelligence across the enterprise.
