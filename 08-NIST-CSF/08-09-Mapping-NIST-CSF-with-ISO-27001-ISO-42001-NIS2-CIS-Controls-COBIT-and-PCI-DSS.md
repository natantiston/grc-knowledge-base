# Introduction

Modern organizations rarely operate under a single cybersecurity framework.

A financial institution may need to comply with **NIST CSF**, **ISO/IEC 27001**, **PCI DSS**, **NIS2**, and national regulations simultaneously. Likewise, an organization developing artificial intelligence systems may implement **ISO/IEC 42001** alongside its existing cybersecurity and risk management frameworks.

Rather than treating these frameworks as separate initiatives, organizations achieve better results by integrating them into a single governance model.

The NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF) 2.0 is particularly well suited for this purpose because it is flexible, outcome-based, and designed to complement other standards rather than replace them.

---

# Why Framework Mapping Matters

Framework mapping enables organizations to:

- Eliminate duplicate controls.
- Simplify compliance activities.
- Improve governance.
- Reduce audit effort.
- Support enterprise risk management.
- Improve reporting to executives.
- Increase operational efficiency.
- Build a unified cybersecurity program.

Instead of implementing multiple independent security programs, organizations implement one integrated program that satisfies multiple frameworks.

# Mapping NIST CSF with ISO/IEC 27001:2022 and ISO/IEC 42001

One of the greatest strengths of the NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF) 2.0 is its ability to integrate with international management system standards.

Organizations implementing **ISO/IEC 27001:2022** or **ISO/IEC 42001** will discover that many governance principles, risk management activities, and continual improvement processes align naturally with the CSF.

Rather than maintaining separate cybersecurity and management programs, organizations can build a unified governance model that satisfies multiple standards simultaneously.

---

# NIST CSF vs ISO/IEC 27001

Although they are closely related, NIST CSF and ISO/IEC 27001 serve different purposes.

| NIST CSF 2.0 | ISO/IEC 27001:2022 |
|--------------|--------------------|
| Cybersecurity governance framework | Information Security Management System (ISMS) standard |
| Outcome-based | Requirement-based |
| Flexible implementation | Formal management system requirements |
| Voluntary framework | Can be independently certified |
| Focuses on cybersecurity risk | Focuses on managing information security systematically |

Organizations often use:

- **NIST CSF** to determine *what* cybersecurity outcomes they want to achieve.
- **ISO/IEC 27001** to define *how* those outcomes are governed, managed, measured, and continually improved.

---

# Alignment Between NIST CSF Functions and ISO/IEC 27001

The six CSF Functions align closely with ISO/IEC 27001 clauses and Annex A controls.

| NIST CSF Function | ISO/IEC 27001 Alignment |
|-------------------|-------------------------|
| Govern (GV) | Clauses 4–10 (Context, Leadership, Planning, Support, Operation, Performance Evaluation, Improvement) |
| Identify (ID) | Risk Assessment, Asset Management, Annex A controls |
| Protect (PR) | Annex A preventive controls |
| Detect (DE) | Monitoring, logging, event detection, Annex A detection controls |
| Respond (RS) | Incident management processes, Annex A incident controls |
| Recover (RC) | Business continuity, disaster recovery, continual improvement |

Although terminology differs, both frameworks support a risk-based approach to cybersecurity.

---

# Mapping NIST CSF with NIS2, CIS Controls v8, COBIT 2019, and PCI DSS 4.0

Organizations frequently adopt multiple cybersecurity frameworks to address governance, operational security, regulatory compliance, and industry-specific requirements. The NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF) 2.0 provides a flexible governance structure that can be integrated with other internationally recognized frameworks and standards.

This lesson examines how NIST CSF aligns with the **NIS2 Directive**, **CIS Controls v8**, **COBIT 2019**, and **PCI DSS 4.0**, enabling organizations to create a unified cybersecurity governance program.

---

# Mapping NIST CSF with NIS2

The **NIS2 Directive** is the European Union's cybersecurity legislation designed to improve the resilience of essential and important entities.

Unlike the NIST CSF, NIS2 is **mandatory** for organizations within its scope.

### Primary Focus

| NIST CSF | NIS2 |
|-----------|------|
| Cybersecurity governance framework | European cybersecurity regulation |
| Risk-based guidance | Legal compliance obligations |
| Flexible implementation | Mandatory security requirements |
| Applicable worldwide | Applies to covered entities within the EU |

Many organizations use NIST CSF as their operational framework while implementing NIS2 to satisfy legal obligations.

---

# Common Areas of Alignment

NIS2 and NIST CSF share several common principles.

| NIST CSF Function | NIS2 Requirement |
|-------------------|------------------|
| Govern | Governance, leadership accountability, cybersecurity policies |
| Identify | Risk management and asset identification |
| Protect | Security controls and preventive measures |
| Detect | Monitoring and threat detection |
| Respond | Incident handling and reporting |
| Recover | Business continuity and disaster recovery |

Organizations can often demonstrate compliance with NIS2 by implementing mature CSF governance processes and documenting evidence.

---

# Mapping NIST CSF with CIS Controls v8

The **CIS Controls v8** provide a prioritized set of technical and operational safeguards designed to reduce cybersecurity risk.

Unlike NIST CSF, CIS Controls focus on **specific implementation activities**.

| NIST CSF | CIS Controls |
|-----------|--------------|
| Defines cybersecurity outcomes | Defines practical safeguards |
| Governance-focused | Control-focused |
| Strategic | Operational |
| Flexible | Highly prescriptive |

The two frameworks complement each other effectively.

---

# Example Alignment

| NIST CSF Function | Supporting CIS Controls |
|-------------------|------------------------|
| Govern | Security governance, policy management |
| Identify | Asset Management, Software Inventory |
| Protect | Access Control, Secure Configuration, Data Protection |
| Detect | Audit Logging, Continuous Monitoring |
| Respond | Incident Response Management |
| Recover | Backup and Recovery Management |

Organizations often use NIST CSF to establish governance and CIS Controls to implement technical safeguards.

---

# Mapping NIST CSF with COBIT 2019

COBIT 2019 focuses on the **governance and management of enterprise information and technology (EGIT)**.

It provides a broader enterprise governance model than NIST CSF.

| NIST CSF | COBIT 2019 |
|-----------|------------|
| Cybersecurity governance | Enterprise governance of IT |
| Cyber risk management | Business governance and IT governance |
| Cybersecurity outcomes | Governance objectives |
| Security-focused | Enterprise-wide IT governance |

COBIT extends governance beyond cybersecurity to include value creation, performance management, and strategic alignment.

# Building an Integrated Cybersecurity Governance Framework

Organizations rarely rely on a single cybersecurity framework. Instead, they combine governance frameworks, management systems, technical control frameworks, and regulatory requirements to build a comprehensive cybersecurity program.

The NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF) 2.0 serves as an excellent foundation because it provides a flexible, risk-based governance model that can integrate multiple standards without creating unnecessary complexity.

A mature cybersecurity program does not attempt to implement every framework independently. Instead, it establishes one governance model supported by shared policies, common risk assessments, unified controls, and coordinated assurance activities.

# Best Practices

Organizations should:

- Establish the NIST Cybersecurity Framework as the central governance model while integrating complementary standards, regulations, and control frameworks into a single enterprise cybersecurity program.
- Maintain a centralized control library that maps security controls across NIST CSF, ISO/IEC 27001, ISO/IEC 42001, CIS Controls, COBIT, PCI DSS, NIS2, DORA, and other applicable requirements.
- Develop common governance processes, unified policies, shared risk assessments, and standardized documentation that support multiple frameworks simultaneously.
- Coordinate audits, assessments, certification activities, and compliance reviews to maximize evidence reuse and reduce administrative burden.
- Provide executive leadership with consolidated dashboards that present cybersecurity performance, enterprise risk, regulatory compliance, operational resilience, and governance maturity through meaningful KPIs and KRIs.
- Leverage automation, continuous monitoring, governance dashboards, and artificial intelligence to improve compliance monitoring, control testing, and executive decision-making.
- Review framework mappings periodically to address new regulatory obligations, evolving cybersecurity threats, emerging technologies, mergers, acquisitions, and strategic business changes.
- Foster a culture of continual improvement by integrating lessons learned from incidents, audits, exercises, risk assessments, and regulatory reviews into governance processes and long-term cybersecurity strategy.

These practices help organizations establish a resilient, scalable, and sustainable cybersecurity governance program capable of supporting enterprise growth, digital transformation, and evolving regulatory expectations.

# Practical Example

A multinational financial institution operates across North America, Europe, and Asia while managing online banking services, payment processing, cloud-native applications, and artificial intelligence-based fraud detection systems. To simplify governance, executive leadership establishes the NIST Cybersecurity Framework 2.0 as the organization's primary cybersecurity governance model. Supporting management systems include ISO/IEC 27001 for information security, ISO/IEC 42001 for AI governance, and ISO 22301 for business continuity. COBIT 2019 aligns cybersecurity with enterprise governance, CIS Controls v8 provides prioritized technical safeguards, PCI DSS secures payment card processing, and NIS2 addresses European regulatory obligations. Rather than managing each framework independently, the organization creates a centralized control library, unified policy framework, shared enterprise risk assessment methodology, and integrated audit program that supports all applicable standards and regulations.

Executive dashboards consolidate cybersecurity metrics, regulatory compliance status, supplier risk, operational resilience, AI governance indicators, and implementation maturity into a single governance reporting platform. Continuous monitoring and automated compliance validation provide near real-time visibility into control effectiveness, while internal audits reuse evidence across certification audits, regulatory inspections, and customer assurance activities. As new technologies and regulations emerge, the governance framework is updated without redesigning the entire cybersecurity program. This integrated approach enables the organization to maintain strong cybersecurity governance, reduce compliance costs, improve operational efficiency, and strengthen enterprise resilience while supporting long-term business growth.
