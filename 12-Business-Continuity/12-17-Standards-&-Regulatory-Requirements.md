# Introduction

Business disruptions can arise from natural disasters, cyberattacks, pandemics, supply chain failures, utility outages, and many other unexpected events. Organizations need a structured and internationally recognized approach to prepare for, respond to, and recover from these disruptions while maintaining the delivery of critical products and services.

**ISO 22301:2019 – Security and Resilience – Business Continuity Management Systems (BCMS) – Requirements** is the internationally recognized standard for establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving a Business Continuity Management System.

Rather than focusing solely on disaster recovery or emergency response, ISO 22301 provides a comprehensive management framework that integrates governance, leadership, risk assessment, Business Impact Analysis (BIA), recovery planning, testing, performance evaluation, and continual improvement. It enables organizations of all sizes and industries to develop resilience and effectively manage disruptive incidents.

---

# What is ISO 22301?

ISO 22301 is an international standard published by the International Organization for Standardization (ISO) that specifies the requirements for a Business Continuity Management System (BCMS).

The standard helps organizations:

- Understand their operational risks.
- Identify critical business activities.
- Protect essential products and services.
- Prepare for disruptive incidents.
- Respond effectively to emergencies.
- Recover business operations efficiently.
- Improve organizational resilience.
- Continually enhance Business Continuity capabilities.

ISO 22301 follows the **Plan-Do-Check-Act (PDCA)** continual improvement model, ensuring that Business Continuity becomes an ongoing management process rather than a one-time project.

---

# Benefits of ISO 22301

Implementing ISO 22301 provides numerous organizational benefits.

Key benefits include:

- Improved organizational resilience.
- Reduced operational downtime.
- Faster recovery from disruptions.
- Better protection of critical business services.
- Increased customer confidence.
- Enhanced regulatory compliance.
- Improved governance and accountability.
- Stronger risk management.
- Greater stakeholder confidence.
- Continual improvement of Business Continuity capabilities.

These benefits help organizations remain operational during disruptions while protecting customers, employees, and business objectives.

---

# Structure of ISO 22301

Like other modern ISO management system standards, ISO 22301 follows the **Annex SL High-Level Structure (HLS)**, making it easier to integrate with standards such as ISO 27001, ISO 9001, and ISO 14001.

The main clauses include:

| Clause | Purpose |
|--------|---------|
| Clause 4 | Context of the Organization |
| Clause 5 | Leadership |
| Clause 6 | Planning |
| Clause 7 | Support |
| Clause 8 | Operation |
| Clause 9 | Performance Evaluation |
| Clause 10 | Improvement |

These clauses provide a structured framework for establishing and maintaining an effective BCMS.

---

# Key Requirements of ISO 22301

Organizations implementing ISO 22301 are expected to establish several essential Business Continuity processes.

These include:

- Understanding organizational context.
- Leadership commitment.
- Business Continuity policy.
- Risk assessment.
- Business Impact Analysis (BIA).
- Business Continuity strategies.
- Business Continuity Plans.
- Training and awareness.
- Business Continuity exercises.
- Performance monitoring.
- Internal audits.
- Management reviews.
- Corrective actions.
- Continual improvement.

Together, these requirements ensure that Business Continuity becomes embedded within organizational governance and operations.

# Practical Example

A multinational pharmaceutical company decides to implement an ISO 22301-compliant Business Continuity Management System after experiencing several supply chain disruptions and regional operational outages. Executive leadership appoints a BCM Manager, establishes a Business Continuity Steering Committee, and defines a Business Continuity Policy aligned with the organization's strategic objectives.

Business Impact Analyses and risk assessments are conducted across manufacturing plants, research facilities, and distribution centers. Recovery strategies are developed for critical business services, continuity plans are documented, and regular exercises are scheduled to validate recovery capabilities. Internal audits and management reviews identify improvement opportunities, while performance metrics are monitored through executive dashboards.

After successfully implementing the BCMS and completing an external certification audit, the organization achieves ISO 22301 certification, demonstrating its commitment to operational resilience and internationally recognized Business Continuity best practices.

# Key Takeaways

- ISO 22301 is the international standard for establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving a Business Continuity Management System (BCMS).
- The standard provides a structured framework for preparing for, responding to, and recovering from disruptive incidents.
- ISO 22301 follows the Annex SL High-Level Structure and the Plan-Do-Check-Act (PDCA) continual improvement model.
- Core requirements include governance, risk assessment, Business Impact Analysis, Business Continuity planning, testing, performance evaluation, and continual improvement.
- ISO 22301 integrates effectively with other management system standards, including ISO/IEC 27001 and ISO 31000.
- Implementing ISO 22301 strengthens organizational resilience, improves recovery capabilities, supports regulatory compliance, and enhances stakeholder confidence.

# Introduction

Modern organizations rely heavily on Information and Communication Technology (ICT) to deliver products, services, and critical business operations. Business applications, cloud services, communication systems, networks, databases, and digital infrastructure support nearly every business process. Consequently, disruptions affecting ICT can quickly lead to operational downtime, financial losses, regulatory consequences, and reputational damage.

While **ISO 22301** establishes the overall framework for a Business Continuity Management System (BCMS), organizations also require guidance on ensuring that their technology infrastructure can support business recovery objectives. **ISO/IEC 27031 – Information Technology – Security Techniques – Guidelines for Information and Communication Technology Readiness for Business Continuity (ICT Readiness for Business Continuity)** provides this guidance.

ISO 27031 helps organizations prepare ICT systems to support Business Continuity by improving resilience, recovery capabilities, disaster recovery planning, and operational readiness for disruptive events.

---

# What is ISO/IEC 27031?

ISO/IEC 27031 is an international standard that provides guidance for establishing ICT Readiness for Business Continuity (IRBC).

Its primary objective is to ensure that information and communication technologies can support the continuity and recovery of critical business services during and after disruptive incidents.

The standard helps organizations:

- Improve ICT resilience.
- Protect critical information systems.
- Develop Disaster Recovery capabilities.
- Reduce technology downtime.
- Support Business Continuity objectives.
- Strengthen cyber resilience.
- Improve operational readiness.
- Enhance recovery planning.

Rather than replacing ISO 22301, ISO 27031 complements it by focusing specifically on ICT preparedness.

---

# Relationship Between ISO 22301 and ISO/IEC 27031

Although closely related, the two standards have different areas of focus.

| ISO 22301 | ISO/IEC 27031 |
|------------|---------------|
| Business Continuity Management System (BCMS) | ICT Readiness for Business Continuity |
| Business processes and organizational resilience | Technology infrastructure and ICT resilience |
| Business continuity planning | ICT recovery planning |
| Organizational recovery | Technology recovery |
| Overall Business Continuity governance | ICT operational readiness |

Together, these standards provide a comprehensive framework for ensuring both business and technology resilience.

---

Following these practices helps ensure that ICT systems remain available and recoverable during disruptive events.

# Practical Example

A multinational healthcare provider operates electronic medical record systems, telemedicine platforms, and cloud-based diagnostic applications that are essential to patient care. To strengthen Business Continuity, the organization adopts the guidance provided by ISO/IEC 27031 and aligns its ICT recovery program with its ISO 22301-certified Business Continuity Management System.

Critical ICT services are identified through the Business Impact Analysis, recovery objectives are established, and Disaster Recovery procedures are developed for data centers, cloud infrastructure, and network services. The organization conducts regular recovery exercises, validates backup integrity, and continuously monitors ICT readiness through performance dashboards.

When a major data center experiences a power failure, critical healthcare applications are successfully restored using cloud-based recovery infrastructure within the defined Recovery Time Objectives (RTOs). Patient services continue with minimal disruption, demonstrating how ICT readiness directly supports Business Continuity and organizational resilience.

# Introduction

Cyber incidents are inevitable, regardless of how mature an organization's cybersecurity program may be. Even with strong preventive controls, organizations must be prepared to restore operations quickly following ransomware attacks, system failures, data breaches, insider threats, or other disruptive cyber events.

The **NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF) 2.0** provides a flexible and risk-based framework for managing cybersecurity risks. One of its six core functions is the **Recover Function**, which focuses on restoring capabilities and services affected by cybersecurity incidents.

The Recover Function supports organizations in minimizing operational disruption, restoring critical business services, maintaining stakeholder confidence, and improving resilience through continual learning. When integrated with Business Continuity Management (BCM), Disaster Recovery (DR), and Incident Response (IR), it enables organizations to recover efficiently while strengthening their overall operational resilience.

---

# What is the Recover Function?

The Recover Function defines the activities required to restore systems, services, and business operations following a cybersecurity incident.

Its primary objectives are to:

- Restore critical business services.
- Recover information systems.
- Resume normal business operations.
- Reduce downtime.
- Minimize business impact.
- Improve organizational resilience.
- Capture lessons learned.
- Enhance future recovery capabilities.

Recovery focuses not only on restoring technology but also on ensuring that business operations return to an acceptable level of performance.

---

# Relationship to Business Continuity

The Recover Function complements Business Continuity Management by providing cybersecurity-specific recovery guidance.

| Business Continuity Management | NIST CSF Recover Function |
|-------------------------------|----------------------------|
| Maintains critical business operations | Restores systems after cyber incidents |
| Addresses all types of disruptions | Focuses on cybersecurity-related disruptions |
| Business recovery planning | Cyber recovery planning |
| Crisis management | Technical recovery activities |
| Organizational resilience | Cyber resilience |

Together, these disciplines provide a coordinated approach to recovering from cyber disruptions.

---

# Categories within the Recover Function

The Recover Function in NIST CSF 2.0 consists of two primary categories:

| Category | Purpose |
|----------|---------|
| **Recovery Execution (RC.RP)** | Execute recovery activities to restore affected assets and services efficiently and securely. |
| **Recovery Communication (RC.CO)** | Coordinate and communicate recovery activities with internal and external stakeholders. |

These categories ensure that recovery efforts are both technically effective and properly coordinated across the organization.

---

# Recovery Activities

Organizations implementing the Recover Function should establish structured recovery activities.

These include:

- Activate recovery plans.
- Restore critical systems.
- Recover business data.
- Validate restored services.
- Resume business operations.
- Communicate recovery progress.
- Monitor for recurring threats.
- Conduct post-incident reviews.
- Update recovery documentation.
- Implement improvement actions.

These activities should be aligned with Business Continuity and Disaster Recovery objectives.

---

# Best Practices for Implementing the Recover Function

Organizations can improve recovery capabilities by adopting recognized best practices.

Recommended practices include:

- Define Recovery Time Objectives (RTOs).
- Define Recovery Point Objectives (RPOs).
- Maintain secure and tested backups.
- Develop documented recovery procedures.
- Conduct regular recovery exercises.
- Coordinate Business Continuity and Incident Response teams.
- Establish clear communication plans.
- Continuously monitor recovery effectiveness.
- Perform lessons learned reviews after incidents.
- Update recovery strategies based on evolving risks.

These practices improve both cyber resilience and overall organizational resilience.

# Practical Example

A multinational financial services organization experiences a ransomware attack that disrupts several customer-facing applications and internal payment processing systems. After the Incident Response Team successfully contains the attack, the organization activates its recovery procedures based on the NIST CSF 2.0 Recover Function.

The Disaster Recovery Team restores critical systems from verified backups while the Business Continuity Team coordinates alternate business processes to maintain essential customer services. Throughout the recovery effort, executive leadership, regulators, customers, and business units receive regular updates regarding service restoration progress. Once recovery is complete, technical teams validate system integrity, confirm that Recovery Time Objectives (RTOs) and Recovery Point Objectives (RPOs) have been achieved, and conduct a comprehensive lessons learned review.

The findings are used to improve recovery documentation, strengthen backup strategies, enhance cyber resilience, and refine future Business Continuity and Incident Response planning.

- Successful recovery depends on documented recovery plans, tested backups, clear communication, and coordination across Business Continuity, Disaster Recovery, and Incident Response teams.
- Lessons learned and continual improvement are essential for strengthening future recovery capabilities.
- Integrating the Recover Function into the Business Continuity Management System enhances cyber resilience, minimizes operational disruption, and supports the rapid restoration of critical business services.

# Introduction

As financial institutions become increasingly dependent on digital technologies, cloud services, and third-party ICT providers, operational disruptions caused by cyberattacks, system failures, or supplier outages have become significant business risks. Traditional Business Continuity and Disaster Recovery practices remain essential, but regulators now expect organizations to demonstrate a higher level of operational resilience that enables critical services to continue during severe disruptions.

The **Digital Operational Resilience Act (DORA)** is a European Union regulation designed to strengthen the digital operational resilience of financial entities. It establishes a harmonized framework for managing Information and Communication Technology (ICT) risks, improving cyber resilience, strengthening Business Continuity capabilities, and ensuring that critical financial services remain operational during disruptive events.

DORA represents a shift from simply protecting systems to ensuring that organizations can withstand, respond to, recover from, and adapt to ICT-related disruptions while maintaining critical business operations.

---

# What is DORA?

The Digital Operational Resilience Act (DORA) is an EU regulation that establishes mandatory requirements for managing ICT risks across the financial sector.

DORA applies to a wide range of regulated financial entities, including:

- Banks.
- Credit institutions.
- Insurance companies.
- Investment firms.
- Payment service providers.
- Electronic money institutions.
- Crypto-asset service providers.
- Financial market infrastructures.
- Certain ICT third-party service providers supporting financial entities.

The regulation promotes a consistent approach to operational resilience across the European financial ecosystem.

---

# Objectives of DORA

DORA aims to ensure that financial organizations can continue delivering critical services despite ICT-related disruptions.

Its objectives include:

- Strengthen ICT risk management.
- Improve cyber resilience.
- Enhance Business Continuity capabilities.
- Standardize incident reporting.
- Improve digital operational resilience testing.
- Strengthen third-party ICT risk management.
- Increase supervisory oversight.
- Improve overall financial sector resilience.

These objectives reduce systemic risk while increasing confidence in digital financial services.

---

# The Five Pillars of DORA

DORA is built around five key pillars.

| Pillar | Purpose |
|---------|---------|
| ICT Risk Management | Establish governance and controls to manage ICT risks throughout the organization |
| ICT-Related Incident Management & Reporting | Detect, classify, manage, and report significant ICT incidents to regulators |
| Digital Operational Resilience Testing | Regularly test resilience through vulnerability assessments, Disaster Recovery exercises, and advanced threat-led testing where applicable |
| ICT Third-Party Risk Management | Manage risks arising from cloud providers and other critical ICT suppliers |
| Information Sharing | Encourage trusted sharing of cyber threat intelligence and best practices among financial entities |

Together, these pillars provide a comprehensive framework for managing operational resilience.

---

# Relationship Between DORA and Business Continuity

DORA complements traditional Business Continuity Management by expanding resilience beyond recovery planning.

| Business Continuity Management | DORA |
|--------------------------------|------|
| Focuses on maintaining critical business operations | Focuses on maintaining critical financial services through digital resilience |
| Includes Business Continuity and Disaster Recovery planning | Requires integrated ICT risk management, cyber resilience, and operational resilience |
| Addresses multiple disruption scenarios | Places strong emphasis on ICT-related disruptions |
| Supports organizational recovery | Requires continuous resilience testing and regulatory reporting |
| Promotes continual improvement | Mandates ongoing monitoring, governance, and supervisory oversight |

Organizations subject to DORA should integrate its requirements into their existing Business Continuity Management System (BCMS).

---

# DORA and Operational Resilience

Operational resilience extends beyond recovering from incidents—it ensures that organizations can continue delivering important business services throughout a disruption.

To achieve this, organizations should:

- Identify critical business services.
- Map ICT dependencies.
- Define impact tolerances.
- Maintain Business Continuity Plans.
- Develop Disaster Recovery procedures.
- Strengthen cyber resilience.
- Assess third-party ICT risks.
- Perform resilience testing.
- Continuously monitor ICT risks.
- Review and improve resilience capabilities.

These activities support both regulatory compliance and long-term organizational resilience.

# Practical Example

A multinational bank operating across several European Union member states adopts DORA to strengthen its digital operational resilience program. The organization establishes an ICT governance committee, identifies critical business services, maps dependencies on cloud providers and payment platforms, and integrates ICT risk management into its existing Business Continuity Management System.

The bank conducts Business Impact Analyses, defines impact tolerances for critical services, implements continuous monitoring of ICT risks, and performs regular Disaster Recovery exercises and cyber resilience testing. Critical ICT suppliers are assessed through a structured third-party risk management program, while significant ICT incidents are classified and reported in accordance with regulatory requirements.

During a regional cloud service disruption, the bank activates its Business Continuity and Disaster Recovery plans, transfers critical workloads to alternate infrastructure, and maintains essential customer banking services with minimal interruption. Following the event, management reviews recovery performance, updates resilience strategies, and implements corrective actions, further strengthening compliance with DORA and improving long-term operational resilience.
