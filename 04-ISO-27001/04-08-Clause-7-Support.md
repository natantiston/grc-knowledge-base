# Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

- Explain the purpose of Clause 7 in ISO/IEC 27001.
- Understand why support functions are essential to an effective Information Security Management System (ISMS).
- Identify the five subclauses of Clause 7.

- Understand how Clause 7 enables the successful operation of the ISMS.

---

# Introduction

After defining the organization's context, establishing leadership, and completing risk-based planning, the organization must ensure that it has everything necessary to operate the Information Security Management System (ISMS).

This is the purpose of **Clause 7 – Support**.

An ISMS cannot succeed without:

- Sufficient resources.
- Competent personnel.
- Security awareness.
- Effective communication.
- Properly controlled documentation.

Clause 7 provides these enabling elements.

Think of the ISMS as a high-performance vehicle:

- **Clause 4** defines where the organization wants to go.
- **Clause 5** provides leadership and direction.
- **Clause 6** creates the route and travel plan.
- **Clause 7** supplies the fuel, driver, maintenance, and operating manual.

Without these supporting components, even the best-designed ISMS will fail during implementation. Clause 7 ensures that organizations provide the resources, competence, awareness, communication, and documented information needed to establish, implement, maintain, and continually improve the ISMS. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

---

# Why Clause 7 Is Important

Many organizations focus heavily on technology while overlooking the people and processes required to operate security effectively.

For example:

An organization may purchase:

- Security Information and Event Management (SIEM)
- Endpoint Detection and Response (EDR)
- Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)
- Data Loss Prevention (DLP)

However, if:

- Employees are not trained,
- Roles are unclear,
- Documentation is outdated,
- Security messages are not communicated,

the organization may still experience security incidents despite significant investment.

Clause 7 ensures that information security is supported not only by technology but also by governance, people, communication, and documentation.

---

# The Five Subclauses of Clause 7

Clause 7 consists of five supporting requirements.

| Subclause | Title | Purpose |
|-----------|-------|---------|
| **7.1** | Resources | Ensure sufficient people, budget, infrastructure, and tools are available |
| **7.2** | Competence | Ensure personnel have the necessary knowledge, skills, and experience |
| **7.3** | Awareness | Ensure personnel understand the Information Security Policy and their responsibilities |
| **7.4** | Communication | Define what, when, how, and with whom information security is communicated |
| **7.5** | Documented Information | Control ISMS documents and records throughout their lifecycle |

Together, these requirements ensure the ISMS has the organizational support needed to operate effectively. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

---

# Relationship Between Clause 6 and Clause 7

Clause 6 answers:

> **"What should we do?"**

Clause 7 answers:

> **"How will we support doing it?"**

For example:

Risk Treatment Plan:

- Deploy Multi-Factor Authentication.

Clause 7 provides:

- Budget for licenses.
- Engineers to deploy MFA.
- User awareness training.
- Communication to employees.
- Documentation updates.

Without Clause 7, the organization would have plans but lack the capability to execute them.

---

# Support as an Enabler

Clause 7 does not introduce new security controls.

Instead, it enables all other ISMS activities by ensuring the organization has:

- Qualified personnel.
- Management support.
- Reliable documentation.
- Effective communication.
- Ongoing awareness.

These supporting elements increase the likelihood that security controls are implemented consistently and remain effective over time.

# Practical Example

A global financial services company has completed its risk assessment and developed a comprehensive Risk Treatment Plan.

Before implementing the identified controls, the organization ensures it has the necessary support by:

- Allocating budget for new cybersecurity technologies.
- Hiring additional security analysts.
- Delivering role-based security training.
- Launching an organization-wide security awareness campaign.
- Publishing a formal communication plan for reporting security incidents.
- Implementing a document management system to control ISMS policies and procedures.

Because these supporting activities are established before operational implementation, the organization is better positioned to deploy and sustain its Information Security Management System successfully.

In the following lessons, we will examine each of the five support requirements in detail, beginning with **Clause 7.1 – Resources**, which focuses on ensuring that sufficient people, technology, infrastructure, and financial resources are available to establish, implement, maintain, and continually improve the ISMS.

# Clause 7.1 – Resources

An Information Security Management System (ISMS) can only be effective if the organization provides the resources needed to establish, implement, maintain, and continually improve it.

This is the purpose of **Clause 7.1 – Resources**.

Although Clause 7.1 is one of the shortest clauses in ISO/IEC 27001, it is one of the most important because every other ISMS activity depends on having adequate people, budget, technology, infrastructure, and management support.

In simple terms:

> **An ISMS without adequate resources is only a set of documents—it cannot operate effectively.**

Clause 7.1 requires organizations to **determine and provide** the resources needed throughout the entire ISMS lifecycle, from implementation through continual improvement. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

---

# Purpose of Clause 7.1

The objective of Clause 7.1 is to ensure that the organization has sufficient resources to:

- Establish the ISMS.
- Implement security controls.
- Maintain ISMS processes.
- Monitor performance.
- Conduct internal audits.
- Respond to security incidents.
- Improve the ISMS continuously.

Without appropriate resources, organizations may struggle to achieve their information security objectives or maintain compliance with ISO/IEC 27001.

---

# What Are Resources?

ISO/IEC 27001 does not define a fixed list of resources.

Instead, organizations should determine what resources are necessary based on:

- Organizational size.
- Business objectives.
- Risk profile.
- Regulatory requirements.
- Technology environment.
- Available expertise.

Typical resource categories include:

- People
- Budget
- Technology
- Infrastructure
- External expertise
- Time

The quantity and type of resources will vary from one organization to another.

---

# Human Resources

People are the most important resource within an ISMS.

Organizations should ensure they have personnel capable of performing activities such as:

- Risk assessments.
- Security monitoring.
- Incident response.
- Internal auditing.
- Compliance monitoring.
- Security awareness training.
- Vendor security management.

Typical roles include:

- Chief Information Security Officer (CISO)
- Information Security Manager
- IT Administrators
- Risk Managers
- Internal Auditors
- Business Process Owners
- Executive Management

Smaller organizations may assign multiple responsibilities to the same individual, while larger organizations often have dedicated teams.

---

# Financial Resources

Security initiatives require adequate funding.

Examples include:

- Security software licenses.
- Cloud security services.
- Consulting services.
- Penetration testing.
- Certification audits.
- Employee training.
- Hardware upgrades.
- Disaster recovery solutions.

Management should allocate sufficient budget to support both operational security activities and continual improvement.

---

# Technology Resources

Technology enables the organization to implement and maintain security controls.

Examples include:

- Firewalls
- Endpoint Detection and Response (EDR)
- Security Information and Event Management (SIEM)
- Identity and Access Management (IAM)
- Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)
- Vulnerability scanners
- Backup systems
- Data Loss Prevention (DLP)
- Encryption technologies

Technology investments should be based on identified risks rather than purchasing tools without a defined business need.

---

# Infrastructure Resources

Organizations also require appropriate infrastructure to support the ISMS.

Examples include:

- Secure data centers.
- Cloud environments.
- Office facilities.
- Network infrastructure.
- Physical security systems.
- Backup locations.
- Secure communication platforms.

Infrastructure should support both business operations and information security objectives.

# Determining Resource Needs

Organizations should determine resource requirements by considering:

- Results of the information security risk assessment.
- Risk Treatment Plan.
- Information security objectives.
- Legal and regulatory obligations.
- Business priorities.
- Customer requirements.
- Existing capabilities and skill gaps.

Resource planning should be reviewed regularly because business needs and security risks change over time.

---

# Practical Example

A healthcare organization plans to implement a new Security Operations Center (SOC).

Before implementation, management identifies the resources required:

- **People:** Two security analysts and one SOC manager.
- **Technology:** SIEM platform, EDR solution, and threat intelligence service.
- **Budget:** Annual funding for software licenses, hardware, and staffing.
- **Infrastructure:** Secure monitoring room and redundant network connectivity.
- **External Expertise:** A cybersecurity consulting firm to assist with implementation.

Executive management approves the required funding and resources before the project begins.

By ensuring that the necessary people, technology, infrastructure, and budget are available, the organization complies with Clause 7.1 and provides a strong foundation for implementing and maintaining an effective Information Security Management System. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

# Clause 7.2 – Competence

People are one of the most critical assets in an Information Security Management System (ISMS).

Even with advanced security technologies and well-designed policies, an organization remains vulnerable if the people responsible for implementing and maintaining security lack the necessary knowledge and skills.

This is the purpose of **Clause 7.2 – Competence**.

Clause 7.2 requires organizations to ensure that **persons performing work under their control that affects information security are competent**, based on appropriate education, training, or experience. Where competence gaps exist, organizations must take action to address them and retain evidence that competence has been achieved. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

---

# Purpose of Clause 7.2

The objective of Clause 7.2 is to ensure that individuals performing information security-related activities have the competence needed to perform their responsibilities effectively.

Competence helps organizations to:

- Reduce human error.
- Improve information security performance.
- Support regulatory compliance.
- Strengthen risk management.
- Improve incident response.
- Ensure consistent implementation of the ISMS.

Competence is about more than attending training courses—it is about demonstrating the ability to perform assigned responsibilities successfully.

---

# Who Must Be Competent?

Clause 7.2 applies to **any person whose work can affect the performance of the ISMS**.

This includes:

- Employees.
- Contractors.
- Consultants.
- Temporary workers.
- Outsourced service providers.
- Third-party personnel working under the organization's control.

Examples of roles requiring competence include:

- Chief Information Security Officer (CISO)
- Information Security Manager
- IT Administrators
- Security Analysts
- Internal Auditors
- Risk Managers
- System Owners
- Human Resources personnel handling security responsibilities
- Business Process Owners

Competence requirements should be based on the responsibilities of each role rather than job titles alone. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

---

# The Four Requirements of Clause 7.2

ISO/IEC 27001 identifies four key activities.

## 1. Determine Required Competence

The organization should define the knowledge, skills, education, training, and experience needed for each role.

Examples:

| Role | Required Competence |
|------|----------------------|
| Security Analyst | Incident response, SIEM, malware analysis |
| Internal Auditor | ISO/IEC 27001 auditing techniques |
| System Administrator | Access control, patch management |
| Risk Manager | Information security risk assessment |
| HR Manager | Secure onboarding and offboarding processes |

Defining competence expectations provides a benchmark for assessing personnel.

---

## 2. Ensure Personnel Are Competent

Organizations should verify that individuals possess the required competence through:

- Formal education.
- Professional certifications.
- Relevant work experience.
- Practical skills.
- On-the-job training.
- Mentoring.

Competence is demonstrated through the ability to perform assigned responsibilities—not solely through certificates.

---

## 3. Address Competence Gaps

If an organization identifies a gap between required and existing competence, it should take appropriate action.

Possible actions include:

- Security awareness training.
- Technical training courses.
- Professional certification programs.
- Coaching and mentoring.
- Job rotation.
- Hiring experienced personnel.
- Engaging external specialists.

After taking action, the organization should evaluate whether the competence gap has been effectively addressed. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

---

## 4. Retain Documented Evidence

Organizations must maintain documented information demonstrating competence.

Examples include:

- Training records.
- Certificates.
- Professional licenses.
- Skills assessments.
- Competency matrices.
- Performance evaluations.
- Records of work experience.

These records provide objective evidence during certification audits.

---

# Education, Training, and Experience

Competence may be achieved through one or more of the following.

### Education

Examples:

- Computer Science degree.
- Information Security degree.
- Engineering degree.
- Business Management degree.

---

### Training

Examples:

- ISO/IEC 27001 Lead Implementer.
- ISO/IEC 27001 Internal Auditor.
- Secure coding workshops.
- Incident response exercises.
- Security awareness training.

---

### Experience

Examples:

- Previous cybersecurity roles.
- Security operations experience.
- Risk assessment experience.
- Audit experience.
- Cloud security implementation.

Organizations should consider all three factors when determining whether personnel are competent.

During an ISO/IEC 27001 certification audit, auditors typically verify that:

- Competence requirements have been defined for relevant roles.
- Personnel performing ISMS-related work are competent.
- Training needs have been identified and addressed.
- The effectiveness of training or other competence-building activities has been evaluated.
- Records demonstrating competence are maintained as documented information. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

# Practical Example

A technology company plans to expand its cloud infrastructure.

Management identifies that several system administrators lack experience with cloud security and identity management.

To address this gap, the organization:

- Defines the required cloud security competencies.
- Enrolls administrators in cloud security training.
- Assigns senior engineers as mentors during implementation.
- Evaluates competence through practical exercises and successful deployment of cloud security controls.
- Retains training records, competency assessments, and certification records as evidence.

As a result, the organization demonstrates compliance with Clause 7.2 while ensuring that employees possess the skills needed to implement and maintain security controls effectively.

This proactive approach reduces implementation risks, improves operational performance, and strengthens the overall effectiveness of the Information Security Management System.

# Clause 7.3 – Awareness

Having competent employees is essential, but competence alone does not guarantee secure behavior.

An employee may know **how** to perform a task securely but may still fail to follow security requirements if they do not understand **why information security matters**.

This is where **Clause 7.3 – Awareness** becomes critical.

The purpose of Clause 7.3 is to ensure that every person working under the organization's control understands the importance of information security, their role in supporting the Information Security Management System (ISMS), and the consequences of failing to comply with security requirements.

Unlike Clause 7.2 (Competence), which focuses on developing skills and knowledge, Clause 7.3 focuses on creating a **security-aware culture** where secure behavior becomes part of everyday work. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

---

# Purpose of Clause 7.3

The objective of Clause 7.3 is to ensure that people understand:

- Why information security is important.
- Their role in protecting organizational information.
- How their daily actions affect the effectiveness of the ISMS.
- The consequences of failing to follow security requirements.

Security awareness reduces human error, which remains one of the leading causes of cybersecurity incidents.

---

# Who Must Be Aware?

Clause 7.3 applies to **all persons working under the organization's control**, not just permanent employees.

This includes:

- Employees
- Executive management
- Contractors
- Consultants
- Temporary staff
- Interns
- Outsourced personnel working within the ISMS scope

Everyone who can influence information security should receive appropriate awareness activities. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

---

# What Must People Be Aware Of?

ISO/IEC 27001 identifies three mandatory awareness topics.

## 1. Information Security Policy

Personnel should understand:

- That the Information Security Policy exists.
- Why it exists.
- Where to access it.
- How it applies to their daily work.

Employees are not expected to memorize the policy, but they should understand its purpose and their responsibilities.

---

## 2. Their Contribution to the ISMS

Everyone should understand how their work contributes to the effectiveness of the Information Security Management System.

Examples include:

- Creating strong passwords.
- Protecting confidential information.
- Reporting suspicious emails.
- Following clean desk policies.
- Using approved software only.
- Following secure remote working procedures.

Employees should recognize that their daily behavior directly influences the organization's security posture.

---

## 3. Consequences of Nonconformity

Personnel should understand what happens if security requirements are ignored.

Possible consequences include:

- Data breaches.
- Financial loss.
- Regulatory penalties.
- Customer dissatisfaction.
- Operational disruption.
- Disciplinary action.
- Reputational damage.

Understanding these consequences encourages employees to take information security seriously. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

---

# Awareness vs Competence

These two clauses are closely related but have different objectives.

| Clause 7.2 – Competence | Clause 7.3 – Awareness |
|--------------------------|-------------------------|
| Focuses on knowledge and skills | Focuses on understanding and behavior |
| Answers: "Can the person perform the task?" | Answers: "Does the person understand why security matters?" |
| Often achieved through training and experience | Reinforced through communication and awareness activities |
| Applies to role-specific capabilities | Applies to everyone within the ISMS scope |

Both competence and awareness are necessary for an effective ISMS.

---

# Building a Security Awareness Program

Organizations should create an ongoing awareness program rather than relying on a single annual training session.

Common awareness activities include:

- Employee onboarding sessions.
- Annual security awareness training.
- Phishing simulation exercises.
- Security newsletters.
- Posters and digital signage.
- Cybersecurity awareness month campaigns.
- Email security tips.
- Executive security messages.
- Short awareness videos.
- Team meetings discussing recent threats.

The objective is to make information security part of everyday organizational culture rather than an occasional compliance exercise.

# Evidence Auditors Look For

Unlike Clause 7.2, Clause 7.3 does **not** require a specific documented procedure.

However, auditors will look for evidence that awareness activities are effective.

Typical evidence includes:

- Security awareness training records.
- Attendance logs.
- Phishing simulation results.
- Awareness campaign materials.
- Newsletters and security bulletins.
- Employee interviews.
- Quiz or assessment results.
- Screenshots of awareness communications.

During interviews, auditors often ask employees questions such as:

- Where can you find the Information Security Policy?
- What is your role in protecting information?
- What would you do if you received a suspicious email?
- How do you report an information security incident?

Employees should be able to answer confidently based on the organization's awareness program. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

---

# Practical Example

A multinational financial institution launches an annual information security awareness program.

The program includes:

- Mandatory onboarding security training for all new employees.
- Quarterly phishing simulation exercises.
- Monthly cybersecurity newsletters highlighting emerging threats.
- Posters reminding employees to lock their computers and protect confidential information.
- Short videos explaining how to recognize phishing emails and report security incidents.

The organization tracks participation, measures phishing simulation results, and periodically surveys employees to assess awareness levels.

When auditors interview staff during the ISO/IEC 27001 certification audit, employees can clearly explain the Information Security Policy, describe how they contribute to protecting information, and understand the consequences of failing to follow security procedures.

This demonstrates that the organization has successfully embedded information security awareness into its culture, fulfilling the intent of Clause 7.3.
