# Introduction

No Information Security Management System (ISMS) is ever perfect.

Technology evolves, cyber threats become more sophisticated, business processes change, and new legal or regulatory requirements emerge. An ISMS that remains unchanged will gradually become less effective.

This is why **Clause 10 – Improvement** is the final requirement of ISO/IEC 27001.

Clause 10 represents the **"Act"** phase of the **Plan-Do-Check-Act (PDCA)** cycle.

It requires organizations to:

- Continually improve the ISMS.
- Address nonconformities.
- Implement corrective actions.
- Prevent problems from recurring.
- Enhance the effectiveness of information security processes.

Rather than treating certification as the end goal, Clause 10 ensures that the ISMS continues to evolve throughout its lifecycle. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

Organizations operate in constantly changing environments.

Examples include:

- Emerging technologies.

An ISMS that is not regularly improved will eventually fail to address these changes effectively.

Clause 10 ensures that organizations learn from:

- Security incidents.
- Internal audits.
- Management reviews.
- Monitoring and measurement activities.
- Employee feedback.
- Customer feedback.

These lessons become opportunities to strengthen the ISMS over time.

Clause 10 contains two key requirements.

# Practical Example

A software company experiences an increase in phishing-related security incidents over a six-month period.

Performance monitoring (Clause 9.1) identifies the trend, while an internal audit (Clause 9.2) reveals inconsistent email security configurations across several departments.

During the management review (Clause 9.3), senior leadership approves a comprehensive improvement initiative that includes enhanced email filtering, mandatory Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA), updated awareness training, and revised incident response procedures.

Over the following months, the organization tracks the effectiveness of these improvements and records a significant reduction in successful phishing attacks.

This illustrates the purpose of Clause 10: transforming monitoring results and audit findings into structured improvement activities that strengthen the Information Security Management System and help it remain effective as the organization and its threat landscape continue to evolve.

# Clause 10.1 – Continual Improvement

An Information Security Management System (ISMS) should never remain static.

Cyber threats evolve, technologies change, business processes mature, and regulatory requirements continue to expand. To remain effective, the ISMS must evolve alongside these changes.

This is the purpose of **Clause 10.1 – Continual Improvement**.

Clause 10.1 contains one of the shortest requirements in ISO/IEC 27001, but it carries significant importance. It requires the organization to **continually improve the suitability, adequacy, and effectiveness of the Information Security Management System (ISMS).**

Unlike other clauses, ISO/IEC 27001 does not prescribe exactly how continual improvement should be achieved. Instead, organizations are expected to establish a culture where improvement becomes an ongoing part of business operations rather than a one-time project. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

---

# Purpose of Clause 10.1

The objective of continual improvement is to ensure that the ISMS continues to:

- Support business objectives.
- Address emerging security threats.
- Meet changing legal and regulatory requirements.
- Improve operational efficiency.
- Strengthen information security controls.
- Increase organizational resilience.

Continual improvement ensures that the ISMS remains relevant throughout the organization's lifecycle rather than becoming outdated after certification.

---

# Understanding "Suitability, Adequacy, and Effectiveness"

Clause 10.1 specifically requires improvement in three areas.

### Suitability

The ISMS should continue to fit the organization's needs.

Questions include:

- Does the ISMS still support business objectives?
- Does it address current threats?
- Is the scope still appropriate?

---

### Adequacy

The ISMS should provide sufficient protection.

Questions include:

- Are enough security controls implemented?
- Are sufficient resources available?
- Are policies and procedures complete?

---

### Effectiveness

The ISMS should achieve its intended results.

Questions include:

- Are security objectives being achieved?
- Are incidents decreasing?
- Are controls performing as expected?

Improvement efforts should consider all three aspects rather than focusing only on technology.

# Continual Improvement Process

Many organizations implement a structured improvement cycle.

A common approach includes:

1. Identify an improvement opportunity.
2. Evaluate the potential benefits.
3. Prioritize the improvement.
4. Assign ownership.
5. Implement the improvement.
6. Measure the results.
7. Verify effectiveness.
8. Standardize successful improvements.

This structured approach helps ensure that improvements are completed, evaluated, and incorporated into normal business operations.

# Practical Example

A multinational logistics company reviews its annual ISMS performance and identifies that manual user access reviews are consuming significant time and occasionally result in delayed account removals.

As part of its continual improvement initiative, the organization:

- Implements an automated Identity and Access Management (IAM) solution.
- Integrates user provisioning with the Human Resources system.
- Configures automated reminders for quarterly access reviews.
- Updates the Access Control Procedure to reflect the new process.
- Trains system administrators on the new workflow.
- Measures the results over six months.

After implementation, the organization reduces access review completion time by **60%**, eliminates delayed account deactivations, and improves compliance with internal access management requirements.

During the next ISO/IEC 27001 surveillance audit, the organization demonstrates how monitoring results led to a measurable improvement initiative and provides evidence of the enhanced process and improved performance.

This illustrates the intent of Clause 10.1: continually improving the suitability, adequacy, and effectiveness of the ISMS so that it remains aligned with changing business needs, evolving threats, and organizational objectives. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

# Clause 10.2 – Nonconformity and Corrective Action

Even in a well-designed Information Security Management System (ISMS), problems will occur.

An organization may discover that:

- A security control was not implemented correctly.
- An internal procedure was not followed.
- An audit identifies a weakness.
- A security incident exposes a process failure.
- A regulatory requirement has not been met.

These situations are known as **nonconformities**.

The purpose of **Clause 10.2 – Nonconformity and Corrective Action** is to ensure that organizations not only fix these problems but also identify and eliminate their underlying causes so they do not happen again.

Rather than simply correcting the immediate issue, Clause 10.2 establishes a structured process for learning from failures and continually strengthening the ISMS. It requires organizations to react to nonconformities, determine their root causes, implement corrective actions, verify effectiveness, and retain documented evidence of the entire process. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

---

# Purpose of Clause 10.2

The primary objective of Clause 10.2 is to ensure that organizations:

- Correct identified problems.
- Address the consequences of those problems.
- Identify the root cause.
- Prevent recurrence.
- Improve the effectiveness of the ISMS.

Corrective action is not about assigning blame.

Instead, it focuses on improving processes so that similar issues are less likely to occur in the future.

---

# What Is a Nonconformity?

A **nonconformity** is a failure to meet a requirement.

The requirement may come from:

- ISO/IEC 27001.
- Internal policies and procedures.
- Legal or regulatory obligations.
- Customer contractual requirements.
- Information Security Objectives.
- Risk Treatment Plans.

Examples include:

- An annual internal audit was not performed.
- User accounts were not removed after employees left the organization.
- Backup restoration testing was not completed.
- Security awareness training records are incomplete.
- Risk assessments were not updated after a major system change.
- An Information Security Policy has not been reviewed according to the organization's document control procedure.

---

# Correction vs Corrective Action

These two terms are often confused, but they have different meanings.

| Correction | Corrective Action |
|------------|-------------------|
| Fixes the immediate problem | Eliminates the root cause |
| Short-term response | Long-term prevention |
| Restores compliance | Prevents recurrence |

### Example

**Nonconformity**

A terminated employee's account remains active.

**Correction**

Disable the account immediately.

**Corrective Action**

Investigate why the account was not removed, update the employee offboarding process, automate account deprovisioning through the Identity and Access Management (IAM) system, and train Human Resources and IT personnel on the revised process.

The correction solves today's problem, while the corrective action reduces the likelihood of the issue occurring again. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

---

# The Corrective Action Process

Clause 10.2 follows a logical sequence.

### Step 1 – Identify the Nonconformity

The issue may be discovered through:

- Internal audits.
- External audits.
- Security incidents.
- Monitoring activities.
- Customer complaints.
- Employee reports.
- Regulatory inspections.

---

### Step 2 – Control and Correct the Issue

Immediate action should be taken to:

- Stop the problem.
- Reduce the impact.
- Restore normal operations.

---

### Step 3 – Determine the Root Cause

Organizations should investigate why the problem occurred.

Common root cause analysis techniques include:

- **5 Whys**
- Fishbone (Ishikawa) Diagram
- Fault Tree Analysis
- Process Mapping

The objective is to identify the underlying process weakness rather than simply blaming individuals.

---

### Step 4 – Implement Corrective Action

Actions are taken to eliminate the root cause.

Examples include:

- Updating procedures.
- Automating manual activities.
- Providing additional training.
- Strengthening approval workflows.
- Improving security controls.
- Revising responsibilities.

---

### Step 5 – Verify Effectiveness

After implementation, the organization confirms that:

- The corrective action has been completed.
- The issue has not recurred.
- Similar problems have been prevented elsewhere in the ISMS.

If the problem continues, additional corrective actions may be necessary. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

# Documented Information

ISO/IEC 27001 requires organizations to retain documented information demonstrating:

- The nature of the nonconformity.
- Immediate corrections performed.
- Root cause analysis results.
- Corrective actions implemented.
- Evidence of effectiveness reviews.
- Changes made to the ISMS.

Typical records include:

- Corrective Action Reports (CARs).
- Root Cause Analysis reports.
- Audit finding logs.
- Nonconformity Register.
- Improvement action tracker.
- Updated procedures and policies.
- Verification records.
- Management review minutes discussing corrective actions. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

---

# Evidence Auditors Look For

During an ISO/IEC 27001 certification audit, auditors typically verify that:

- Nonconformities are formally recorded.
- Immediate corrections are performed promptly.
- Root causes have been identified.
- Corrective actions are appropriate to the severity of the issue.
- Corrective actions are assigned to responsible owners with target dates.
- Effectiveness has been verified.
- Similar issues have been considered across the ISMS.
- Records are retained as evidence.

Auditors pay particular attention to **repeat findings**, as recurring nonconformities may indicate ineffective corrective action or unresolved root causes. :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}

# Practical Example

During an internal audit, a financial services company discovers that several privileged user accounts remained active after employees transferred to different departments.

The organization immediately disables the unnecessary accounts, completing the **correction**.

A root cause analysis using the **5 Whys** reveals that the Human Resources system was not integrated with the Identity and Access Management (IAM) platform, causing role changes to rely on manual notifications.

As a **corrective action**, the organization integrates the HR and IAM systems, updates the user access management procedure, provides refresher training to HR and IT staff, and introduces automated quarterly access reviews.

Three months later, a follow-up audit confirms that user role changes are processed automatically and no similar issues have occurred.

The organization documents the nonconformity, root cause analysis, corrective actions, and verification results, demonstrating full compliance with Clause 10.2 while strengthening the effectiveness of its Information Security Management System.
