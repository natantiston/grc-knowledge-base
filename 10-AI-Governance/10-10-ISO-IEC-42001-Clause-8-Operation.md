# Introduction

After establishing the context of the organization (Clause 4), leadership commitment (Clause 5), planning activities (Clause 6), and support processes (Clause 7), ISO/IEC 42001 moves into the operational phase of the Artificial Intelligence Management System (AIMS). Clause 8 focuses on how organizations plan, implement, control, and monitor operational activities that support the development, deployment, operation, maintenance, and retirement of AI systems. It ensures that governance principles are translated into consistent day-to-day operational practices.

Operational planning is essential because AI systems are dynamic by nature. Unlike traditional software, AI models continuously interact with changing data, evolving environments, and new business requirements. Organizations must therefore establish controlled operational processes that manage AI risks, maintain system performance, support regulatory compliance, and ensure that AI systems remain aligned with organizational objectives throughout their lifecycle.

---

# Purpose of Clause 8

Clause 8 ensures that organizations establish controlled operational processes for managing AI systems.

Its primary objectives are to:

- Execute AI governance plans.
- Implement AI risk treatment measures.
- Operate AI systems under defined controls.
- Monitor operational performance.
- Maintain trustworthy AI throughout the lifecycle.
- Manage operational changes.
- Support continual compliance.
- Enable continual improvement.

Clause 8 transforms governance planning into operational execution.

---

# What is Operational Planning?

Operational planning is the process of defining how AI systems will be developed, deployed, operated, monitored, maintained, and retired under controlled conditions.

Operational planning ensures that:

- Governance requirements are implemented.
- AI risks are appropriately managed.
- Roles and responsibilities are clearly assigned.
- Operational procedures are documented.
- Resources are available.
- Controls remain effective.
- Business objectives are achieved.
- Regulatory obligations are supported.

Without operational planning, governance cannot be consistently implemented.

---

# Operational Planning Across the AI Lifecycle

Operational planning should cover every stage of the AI lifecycle.

These stages include:

| AI Lifecycle Stage | Operational Focus |
|-------------------|-------------------|
| Planning | Define objectives, scope, governance requirements |
| Design & Development | Apply governance and development controls |
| Testing & Validation | Verify performance, fairness, and security |
| Deployment | Implement controlled production release |
| Operations | Monitor performance and manage changes |
| Maintenance | Update models, data, and controls |
| Retirement | Securely decommission AI systems |

Operational governance continues until the AI system is formally retired.

Organizations should:

- Develop documented operational plans for all AI systems.
- Integrate operational planning with AI risk management activities.
- Clearly define operational roles and responsibilities.
- Ensure operational controls are proportionate to AI risks.
- Maintain documented operating procedures for all critical AI processes.
- Continuously monitor operational performance and governance effectiveness.

- Align operational activities with the organization's Artificial Intelligence Management System and business objectives.

# Practical Example

A multinational telecommunications provider deploys AI systems to optimize network traffic, detect cybersecurity threats, and automate customer service operations. Before each AI system enters production, operational teams develop a detailed operational plan covering deployment procedures, governance controls, monitoring activities, model validation requirements, incident response processes, access control, and human oversight responsibilities. The operational plan is reviewed by the AI Governance Committee to verify alignment with organizational policies, AI risk assessments, and regulatory obligations before approval is granted.

Once the AI systems are operational, dedicated teams continuously monitor performance, model accuracy, cybersecurity events, and operational metrics through centralized dashboards. Formal change management procedures govern updates to AI models, while operational incidents trigger predefined response processes and post-incident reviews. By implementing structured operational planning and control in accordance with ISO/IEC 42001 Clause 8, the organization ensures that AI systems remain secure, reliable, compliant, and aligned with business objectives throughout their operational lifecycle.

# AI Development Controls

Artificial Intelligence systems require disciplined development practices to ensure they are reliable, secure, transparent, and aligned with organizational objectives. Unlike conventional software development, AI development involves additional considerations such as data quality, model selection, bias mitigation, explainability, model validation, and ongoing performance monitoring. ISO/IEC 42001 Clause 8 requires organizations to establish operational controls that govern these activities throughout the AI development lifecycle.

AI development controls provide a structured approach to designing, building, testing, and validating AI systems before they are deployed into production. These controls help reduce technical, operational, ethical, legal, and security risks while ensuring that AI systems meet business requirements and comply with organizational governance policies. Well-defined development controls also improve consistency, repeatability, and stakeholder confidence in AI solutions.

---

# Purpose of AI Development Controls

Development controls ensure that AI systems are designed and built in a controlled and repeatable manner.

The objectives include:

- Producing reliable AI systems.
- Managing AI-related risks.
- Ensuring regulatory compliance.
- Supporting responsible AI principles.
- Improving model quality.
- Protecting organizational data.
- Reducing development errors.
- Maintaining governance throughout development.

Development controls establish the foundation for trustworthy AI.

# Practical Example

A financial services organization develops an AI model to identify potentially fraudulent credit card transactions. Before development begins, the project team defines business objectives, governance requirements, and AI risks. Training data is reviewed to ensure quality, completeness, representativeness, and compliance with privacy regulations. During development, secure coding practices, controlled development environments, and strict access controls protect the model and supporting data. The AI model undergoes extensive testing to verify accuracy, fairness across customer groups, robustness against manipulation, and explainability of its decisions.

Following technical validation, the AI Governance Committee reviews the model documentation, validation reports, AI risk assessment, security testing results, and compliance evidence. Only after confirming that governance requirements have been satisfied is the model approved for production deployment. By implementing structured AI development controls aligned with ISO/IEC 42001 Clause 8, the organization reduces operational risk, improves stakeholder confidence, and ensures that the AI solution is reliable, secure, and trustworthy before entering production.

# AI Deployment and Monitoring

Deploying an Artificial Intelligence system into production marks the transition from development to operational use. At this stage, AI models begin interacting with real users, business processes, and live data, making effective governance even more critical. ISO/IEC 42001 Clause 8 requires organizations to establish controlled deployment processes and continuous monitoring activities to ensure that AI systems operate as intended, remain aligned with business objectives, and continue to satisfy governance, security, privacy, and regulatory requirements.

Unlike traditional software applications, AI systems may experience changes in performance over time due to evolving data, user behavior, environmental conditions, or model drift. Continuous monitoring enables organizations to detect operational issues early, measure AI performance, identify emerging risks, and initiate corrective actions before significant business impacts occur. Effective deployment and monitoring help maintain trustworthy, reliable, and responsible AI throughout the operational lifecycle.

---

# Purpose of AI Deployment Controls

- Protecting production environments.
- Reducing deployment risks.

# Practical Example

A healthcare provider deploys an AI system that assists clinicians by prioritizing medical imaging cases based on suspected severity. Before deployment, the organization completes security testing, validates model performance using representative clinical data, conducts privacy and regulatory reviews, and obtains formal approval from the AI Governance Committee. The deployment follows a documented change management process, including version control, rollback procedures, operational handover, and updates to system documentation.

Once in production, the AI system is continuously monitored through centralized dashboards that track diagnostic accuracy, response times, system availability, model drift, and security events. Automated alerts notify operations teams when predefined performance thresholds are exceeded, prompting immediate investigation and corrective action. Periodic reviews assess whether retraining is required due to changes in clinical data or patient demographics. By implementing structured deployment and continuous monitoring practices aligned with ISO/IEC 42001 Clause 8, the healthcare provider ensures that the AI system remains accurate, secure, reliable, and compliant throughout its operational lifecycle.

# Third-Party AI Governance

Modern organizations increasingly rely on third-party Artificial Intelligence solutions, including cloud-based AI platforms, foundation models, machine learning services, Software-as-a-Service (SaaS) applications, and AI-enabled vendors. While these services accelerate innovation and reduce development costs, they also introduce additional governance, security, privacy, legal, and operational risks. ISO/IEC 42001 Clause 8 requires organizations to ensure that externally provided AI products and services are governed with the same level of oversight as internally developed AI systems.

Third-party AI governance extends beyond supplier selection. Organizations must assess vendor capabilities, evaluate AI-related risks, establish contractual governance requirements, monitor supplier performance, and ensure ongoing compliance throughout the relationship. Effective governance enables organizations to benefit from external AI technologies while maintaining accountability for the decisions and outcomes generated by those systems.

---

# Purpose of Third-Party AI Governance

Third-party governance ensures that externally provided AI systems operate in accordance with organizational policies and regulatory requirements.

The objectives include:

- Managing supplier risks.
- Protecting organizational information.
- Ensuring regulatory compliance.
- Verifying security controls.
- Protecting personal data.
- Maintaining service reliability.
- Supporting business continuity.
- Preserving organizational accountability.

Organizations remain responsible for AI systems even when provided by external vendors.

---

| Third-Party AI Service | Example |
|-------------------------|---------|

| AI Infrastructure Providers | GPU, cloud computing, and AI platforms |

Each service should be evaluated according to its associated risks.

# Best Practices

Organizations should:

- Perform risk-based assessments before engaging AI suppliers.
- Evaluate third-party AI providers for security, privacy, governance, and regulatory compliance.
- Include AI governance requirements in supplier contracts and service agreements.
- Integrate third-party AI oversight into the organization's Vendor Risk Management (VRM) program.
- Continuously monitor supplier performance, compliance, and operational risks.
- Require timely notification of security incidents and significant AI-related changes.
- Periodically reassess supplier risks based on changing business requirements and regulatory developments.
- Maintain complete documentation supporting supplier evaluations, approvals, monitoring activities, and governance decisions.

---

# Practical Example

A global financial institution integrates a cloud-based generative AI platform to assist customer service representatives in responding to client inquiries and summarizing financial documents. Before procurement, the organization performs a comprehensive supplier risk assessment covering information security, AI governance maturity, privacy controls, regulatory compliance, service availability, and the provider's approach to responsible AI. Independent audit reports, security certifications, and contractual commitments regarding data protection, incident notification, audit rights, and service levels are reviewed before approval is granted.

Following implementation, the AI provider is incorporated into the organization's Vendor Risk Management program. Quarterly governance reviews evaluate service performance, security metrics, regulatory compliance, AI model updates, and contractual obligations. Any significant service changes or security incidents trigger formal reassessments and management reviews. By applying structured third-party AI governance aligned with ISO/IEC 42001 Clause 8, the organization maintains accountability for externally provided AI services while reducing operational, security, and compliance risks.
