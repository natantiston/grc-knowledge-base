# Introduction

An effective Artificial Intelligence Management System (AIMS) must be capable of identifying, responding to, and learning from problems that occur during the operation of AI systems. Even with well-designed governance processes, organizations may encounter situations where policies, procedures, operational controls, regulatory requirements, or AI system performance fail to meet established expectations. ISO/IEC 42001 Clause 10 addresses these situations by requiring organizations to identify nonconformities, take appropriate action, and use the lessons learned to strengthen the AI Management System.

A nonconformity is not limited to technical failures within an AI model. It may involve governance weaknesses, incomplete documentation, ineffective operational controls, security incidents, privacy violations, regulatory breaches, or failures to follow established procedures. Identifying and addressing nonconformities promptly helps reduce risk, improve governance effectiveness, and maintain stakeholder confidence in the organization's AI systems.

---

# Purpose of Clause 10

Clause 10 ensures that organizations continuously improve the effectiveness of their Artificial Intelligence Management System.

Its objectives include:

- Identifying nonconformities.
- Responding to governance failures.
- Correcting identified issues.
- Preventing recurrence.
- Strengthening governance controls.
- Improving operational effectiveness.
- Supporting regulatory compliance.
- Driving continual improvement.

Improvement is a continuous process rather than a one-time activity.

# What is a Nonconformity?

A nonconformity is the failure to meet a specified requirement.

Within an AI Management System, a nonconformity may occur when:

- Organizational policies are not followed.
- ISO/IEC 42001 requirements are not satisfied.
- AI governance controls are ineffective.
- Operational procedures are not implemented.
- Regulatory obligations are not met.
- Required documentation is incomplete.
- AI risks are not properly managed.
- AI system performance falls below approved criteria.

Nonconformities may be identified during routine operations, monitoring activities, audits, incident investigations, or management reviews.

# Best Practices

Organizations should:

- Establish formal procedures for identifying and reporting nonconformities.
- Encourage employees to report governance issues without fear of reprisal.
- Investigate all significant nonconformities promptly and objectively.
- Document evidence supporting investigations and corrective actions.

# Practical Example

A logistics company uses AI to optimize delivery routes across multiple countries. During an internal audit, auditors discover that one AI model was deployed into production without completing the required governance approval process. Although the model performs accurately, the missing approval represents a nonconformity because it violates the organization's documented AI governance procedures and ISO/IEC 42001 requirements.

The organization immediately records the nonconformity, assesses its operational impact, and performs a root cause investigation. The investigation reveals weaknesses in the deployment workflow that allowed governance approval to be bypassed. Management updates the deployment process by introducing automated approval checkpoints within the change management system and provides additional training to development teams. A follow-up review confirms that the new controls are operating effectively. By treating the issue as an opportunity for improvement rather than simply correcting the immediate problem, the organization strengthens its Artificial Intelligence Management System and reduces the likelihood of similar governance failures in the future.

# Corrective Action

Identifying a nonconformity is only the first step in improving an Artificial Intelligence Management System (AIMS). ISO/IEC 42001 Clause 10 requires organizations to take appropriate corrective actions that not only resolve the immediate issue but also eliminate its underlying cause to prevent recurrence. Corrective actions strengthen AI governance by ensuring that lessons learned from incidents, audits, monitoring activities, and operational failures are incorporated into the organization's governance processes.

Corrective action differs from simply correcting a problem. A correction addresses the immediate issue, while a corrective action focuses on identifying and eliminating the root cause. For example, correcting an inaccurate AI prediction may involve updating a model, whereas corrective action may require improving data validation procedures, strengthening governance reviews, or enhancing monitoring controls to prevent similar issues from occurring again.

- Driving continual improvement.

Corrective actions address systemic issues rather than isolated events.

# Correction vs Corrective Action

Although the terms are often used together, they have different meanings.

| Activity | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| Correction | Fixes the immediate problem or nonconformity |
| Corrective Action | Eliminates the root cause to prevent recurrence |

Both activities are important, but corrective action provides long-term governance improvement.

# Practical Example

A financial institution operates an AI model that automatically reviews loan applications. During routine monitoring, the governance team discovers that recent model updates have increased the rejection rate for a specific customer segment beyond acceptable thresholds. An immediate correction temporarily rolls back the model to the previous approved version, restoring normal operations. However, management recognizes that the deployment process itself requires further investigation.

A root cause analysis reveals that changes to the training dataset were introduced without completing the required fairness assessment during model validation. As a corrective action, the organization updates its AI development procedures to require mandatory fairness testing before every production deployment, enhances approval workflows within the change management system, and provides additional training to AI development teams. A follow-up internal audit confirms that the new controls are operating effectively, reducing the likelihood of similar governance failures and strengthening compliance with ISO/IEC 42001.

# Continual Improvement

Continual improvement is one of the fundamental principles of every ISO management system and represents the ultimate objective of ISO/IEC 42001 Clause 10. Rather than viewing AI governance as a static set of policies and controls, organizations should continually evaluate, refine, and enhance their Artificial Intelligence Management System (AIMS) to respond to evolving technologies, emerging risks, changing regulations, and business priorities. Continual improvement ensures that AI governance remains effective, relevant, and capable of supporting responsible AI throughout its lifecycle.

Artificial Intelligence evolves much faster than many traditional technologies. New AI models, changing datasets, evolving cybersecurity threats, regulatory developments, and increased stakeholder expectations require organizations to continuously assess whether existing governance processes remain appropriate. Through continual improvement, organizations strengthen governance maturity, improve operational efficiency, reduce AI-related risks, and increase confidence in AI systems.

# Practical Example

A multinational telecommunications company operates AI systems for network optimization, predictive maintenance, and cybersecurity analytics. Through quarterly management reviews and annual internal audits, the organization identifies opportunities to improve governance by enhancing model monitoring, automating AI asset inventories, and strengthening third-party AI oversight. Performance metrics indicate that although the AI systems meet technical objectives, governance processes can be made more efficient through better integration with the organization's Governance, Risk, and Compliance (GRC) platform.

Management approves a continual improvement program that includes updating AI governance policies, implementing automated compliance dashboards, expanding employee training, refining AI risk assessment methodologies, and improving documentation standards. Six months later, follow-up performance measurements demonstrate faster incident response times, improved audit results, increased governance maturity, and higher stakeholder confidence. By embedding continual improvement into everyday operations, the organization ensures that its Artificial Intelligence Management System remains effective, resilient, and aligned with ISO/IEC 42001 as AI technologies and business requirements continue to evolve.

# AI Governance Maturity

An Artificial Intelligence Management System (AIMS) should evolve over time as an organization gains experience, improves governance processes, and adapts to new technologies, risks, and regulatory requirements. ISO/IEC 42001 encourages organizations to continually improve their governance capabilities, and one of the most effective ways to measure progress is through an AI governance maturity assessment. Governance maturity evaluates how well an organization has established, implemented, monitored, and continually improved its AI governance program.

Organizations with low governance maturity often rely on informal processes, inconsistent documentation, and reactive decision-making. As maturity increases, governance becomes standardized, measurable, integrated into business operations, and continuously optimized. Measuring governance maturity enables organizations to identify strengths, prioritize improvement initiatives, allocate resources effectively, and demonstrate progress to executive leadership, regulators, customers, and certification bodies.

---

# Purpose of AI Governance Maturity

Governance maturity assessments help organizations understand the effectiveness of their Artificial Intelligence Management System.

The objectives include:

- Measuring governance capability.
- Identifying strengths and weaknesses.
- Prioritizing improvement initiatives.
- Supporting strategic planning.
- Demonstrating governance progress.
- Improving operational consistency.
- Supporting certification readiness.
- Driving continual improvement.

Maturity assessments provide a roadmap for long-term governance development.

---

# AI Governance Maturity Levels

Many organizations adopt a five-level maturity model to evaluate governance capabilities.

| Maturity Level | Description |
|----------------|-------------|
| Level 1 – Initial | Governance processes are informal, inconsistent, and largely reactive. |
| Level 2 – Developing | Basic governance policies and procedures have been established but are applied inconsistently. |
| Level 3 – Defined | Governance processes are documented, standardized, and implemented across the organization. |
| Level 4 – Managed | Governance activities are measured, monitored, and actively managed using defined performance metrics. |
| Level 5 – Optimized | Governance is continually improved through automation, analytics, lessons learned, and strategic integration. |

Organizations may progress gradually through these levels over several years.

# Practical Example

A multinational technology company completes its first AI governance maturity assessment one year after implementing ISO/IEC 42001. The assessment evaluates leadership commitment, governance policies, AI risk management, operational controls, internal audits, performance monitoring, documentation, and continual improvement activities. The organization determines that most governance domains are operating at **Level 3 (Defined)**, while monitoring automation and third-party AI governance remain at **Level 2 (Developing)**. Executive management uses these results to prioritize investments in governance dashboards, supplier oversight, and automated compliance reporting.

Over the following year, improvement initiatives are implemented through structured project plans, additional employee training, enhanced monitoring capabilities, and stronger integration with the organization's Governance, Risk, and Compliance (GRC) platform. A follow-up maturity assessment demonstrates measurable progress toward **Level 4 (Managed)**, supported by improved audit results, stronger governance metrics, and greater executive visibility into AI risks. By conducting regular maturity assessments, the organization ensures that its Artificial Intelligence Management System continues to evolve alongside changing business objectives, technological advancements, and regulatory expectations.
