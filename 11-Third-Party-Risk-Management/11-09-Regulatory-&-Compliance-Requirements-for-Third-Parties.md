# Introduction

Organizations increasingly depend on third-party vendors to provide cloud services, software, payment processing, logistics, consulting, customer support, and many other business functions. While outsourcing provides operational and financial benefits, it does not transfer an organization's legal or regulatory responsibilities.

Regulators across industries expect organizations to manage risks introduced by third parties with the same level of diligence applied to internal operations. If a vendor experiences a data breach, violates privacy regulations, or fails to implement required security controls, the organization may still face legal penalties, financial losses, reputational damage, and regulatory enforcement.

For this reason, regulatory compliance must be integrated into every stage of the Third-Party Risk Management (TPRM) lifecycle—from vendor selection and due diligence to continuous monitoring and contract termination.

---

# Why Regulatory Compliance Matters

Third-party relationships often involve access to sensitive information, critical business processes, or regulated services.

Regulatory compliance helps organizations:

- Protect customer information.
- Reduce legal and financial risks.
- Demonstrate due diligence.
- Meet industry requirements.
- Improve vendor accountability.
- Strengthen cybersecurity governance.
- Build stakeholder confidence.
- Support business continuity.

Effective compliance management protects both the organization and its customers.

---

# Third-Party Regulatory Risk

Third-party regulatory risk refers to the possibility that a vendor's actions, omissions, or security weaknesses could cause an organization to violate applicable laws, regulations, or contractual obligations.

Examples include:

- Unauthorized disclosure of personal information.
- Failure to implement required security controls.
- Non-compliance with industry regulations.
- Improper data transfers.
- Weak cybersecurity practices.
- Poor record management.
- Failure to report security incidents.
- Inadequate subcontractor oversight.

Organizations remain accountable even when regulated activities are outsourced.

---

# Common Regulatory Requirements

Organizations should identify all regulations that apply to their third-party relationships.

Examples include:

| Regulation or Standard | Primary Focus |
|------|------|
| GDPR | Protection of personal data and privacy |
| NIS2 Directive | Cybersecurity risk management for essential and important entities |
| HIPAA | Protection of healthcare information |
| PCI DSS | Security of payment card information |
| SOX | Financial reporting and internal controls |
| ISO/IEC 27001 | Information Security Management Systems |
| ISO/IEC 27701 | Privacy Information Management |
| DORA | Digital operational resilience for financial entities |

The applicable requirements depend on the organization's industry, location, and business activities.

---

# Regulatory Expectations

Although regulations differ across jurisdictions, many share common expectations for third-party risk management.

Organizations are generally expected to:

- Perform vendor due diligence.
- Assess cybersecurity risks.
- Protect sensitive information.
- Monitor vendor compliance.
- Report significant incidents.
- Maintain audit records.
- Implement contractual security requirements.
- Review vendor performance regularly.

Meeting these expectations demonstrates sound governance and responsible risk management.

---

# Integrating Compliance into the Vendor Lifecycle

Compliance should be embedded throughout the vendor lifecycle.

| Vendor Lifecycle Stage | Compliance Activity |
|------|------|

| Due Diligence | Evaluate compliance capabilities and certifications |

| Onboarding | Verify required controls before service begins |

| Periodic Reassessment | Validate ongoing compliance and risk management |
| Renewal | Review changes in regulations and vendor obligations |
| Offboarding | Ensure secure data handling and regulatory closure |

Continuous compliance reduces the likelihood of regulatory violations.

The organization is responsible for:

- Defining compliance requirements.
- Selecting appropriate vendors.
- Monitoring compliance.
- Maintaining governance.
- Responding to regulatory inquiries.

The vendor is responsible for:

- Meeting contractual obligations.
- Maintaining required controls.
- Protecting organizational data.
- Reporting incidents promptly.
- Supporting audits.
- Demonstrating ongoing compliance.

Clear responsibilities improve accountability and reduce misunderstandings.

# Best Practices

Organizations should:

- Identify regulatory requirements before selecting vendors.
- Perform compliance-focused due diligence.
- Include regulatory obligations in vendor contracts.
- Maintain evidence of compliance assessments.
- Monitor vendor compliance continuously.
- Conduct periodic compliance reviews.
- Require timely reporting of regulatory or security incidents.
- Coordinate legal, compliance, procurement, and cybersecurity teams.
- Maintain complete records for audits and regulatory inspections.
- Continuously update compliance processes as regulations evolve.

# Practical Example

A European financial services company engages a cloud service provider to process customer financial data. Because the organization operates in a regulated industry, it evaluates the provider's compliance with GDPR, DORA, ISO/IEC 27001, and other applicable financial regulations before signing the contract.

The contract includes data protection requirements, incident notification timelines, audit rights, and compliance reporting obligations. Throughout the contract period, the organization reviews annual audit reports, monitors regulatory changes, and conducts periodic reassessments to verify continued compliance. By integrating regulatory requirements into every stage of the vendor lifecycle, the organization reduces legal and operational risks while maintaining compliance with applicable regulations.

# Introduction

Many organizations share personal information with third-party vendors to support business operations such as cloud computing, payroll, customer support, software development, marketing, healthcare services, and payment processing. While outsourcing these services improves efficiency, it also increases the organization's responsibility to protect personal data.

Privacy regulations around the world require organizations to ensure that vendors process personal information lawfully, securely, and only for authorized purposes. A failure by a vendor to protect personal data can expose the organization to regulatory penalties, legal claims, financial losses, and reputational damage.

For this reason, privacy protection is a core component of Third-Party Risk Management (TPRM). Organizations must evaluate vendor privacy practices before onboarding, monitor compliance throughout the relationship, and ensure secure handling of personal information when the contract ends.

---

# Why Privacy Matters in Third-Party Risk Management

Third-party vendors often process sensitive information on behalf of organizations.

Examples include:

- Customer records.
- Employee information.
- Financial data.
- Healthcare records.
- Identity documents.
- Contact information.
- Payment information.
- Behavioral and analytics data.

Protecting this information is essential for maintaining customer trust and meeting legal obligations.

---

# Common Privacy Risks

Organizations should identify privacy risks before engaging vendors.

Examples include:

- Unauthorized disclosure of personal information.
- Excessive collection of personal data.
- Inadequate encryption.
- Weak access controls.
- Improper international data transfers.
- Inadequate data retention practices.
- Insufficient subcontractor oversight.
- Delayed breach notification.

These risks should be evaluated during vendor due diligence and monitored throughout the relationship.

---

# Privacy Requirements for Vendors

Organizations should define clear privacy expectations for vendors.

Common requirements include:

| Privacy Requirement | Purpose |
|------|------|
| Lawful Processing | Ensure personal data is processed according to applicable laws |
| Data Minimization | Collect and process only the information necessary for business purposes |
| Access Controls | Restrict access to authorized individuals |
| Encryption | Protect personal data during storage and transmission |
| Data Retention | Retain personal information only for approved periods |
| Incident Notification | Report privacy incidents within agreed timeframes |
| Subprocessor Management | Ensure subcontractors meet equivalent privacy requirements |
| Secure Disposal | Securely delete or return personal data when no longer required |

These controls help reduce privacy risks across the vendor lifecycle.

---

# Privacy Assessments

Organizations should evaluate vendor privacy capabilities before sharing personal information.

Assessment activities may include:

- Reviewing privacy policies.
- Evaluating data processing practices.
- Assessing access management.
- Reviewing encryption controls.
- Verifying regulatory compliance.
- Reviewing incident response procedures.
- Assessing data retention processes.
- Evaluating subcontractor oversight.

Privacy assessments provide assurance that vendors can protect personal information appropriately.

---

# Contractual Privacy Controls

Vendor contracts should clearly define privacy obligations.

Contracts commonly include:

- Data Processing Agreements (DPAs).
- Confidentiality clauses.
- Data ownership provisions.
- Cross-border data transfer requirements.
- Data breach notification timelines.
- Data retention requirements.
- Audit rights.
- Secure data return and destruction requirements.

Well-defined contracts strengthen accountability and support regulatory compliance.

---

# Continuous Privacy Monitoring

Privacy oversight should continue throughout the vendor relationship.

Organizations should monitor:

- Privacy incidents.
- Data breaches.
- Regulatory changes.
- Audit findings.
- Privacy complaints.
- Data processing activities.
- Compliance certifications.
- Corrective action status.

Continuous monitoring helps identify emerging privacy risks before they become significant compliance issues.

# Common Challenges

|------|------|

| International Data Transfers | Different countries have different privacy requirements |

# Best Practices

Organizations should:

- Identify personal data processed by each vendor.
- Conduct privacy assessments before onboarding.
- Execute appropriate Data Processing Agreements (DPAs).
- Apply the principle of data minimization.
- Require encryption for sensitive personal information.
- Monitor vendor privacy compliance continuously.
- Include privacy incident reporting requirements in contracts.
- Review international data transfer arrangements regularly.
- Verify secure deletion or return of personal information during offboarding.
- Continuously improve privacy governance based on regulatory changes and lessons learned.

---

# Practical Example

A multinational retailer contracts a cloud-based Human Resources (HR) platform to manage employee records across several countries. Before implementation, the organization performs a privacy assessment to determine how the vendor collects, stores, processes, and transfers personal information.

The contract includes a Data Processing Agreement, encryption requirements, strict access controls, data retention limits, breach notification obligations, and requirements for managing subcontractors. During annual reviews, the organization verifies compliance through audit reports and privacy assessments. When the contract ends, the vendor securely returns employee records and provides evidence that all remaining copies have been permanently deleted. This structured approach enables the organization to meet privacy obligations while reducing risks associated with third-party data processing.

---

- Third-party vendors play a critical role in protecting personal information.
- Privacy risks should be assessed before onboarding and monitored throughout the vendor lifecycle.
- Contracts should clearly define privacy obligations, data protection requirements, and breach notification responsibilities.
- Continuous monitoring helps identify changes that may affect privacy compliance.
- Secure return or destruction of personal information is an essential part of vendor offboarding.
- Effective privacy governance strengthens Third-Party Risk Management by protecting personal data, supporting regulatory compliance, and maintaining stakeholder trust.

# Introduction

Organizations rarely develop their Third-Party Risk Management (TPRM) programs from scratch. Instead, they rely on internationally recognized standards, regulatory frameworks, and industry best practices to establish consistent security, privacy, and compliance expectations for vendors.

These frameworks provide guidance on risk management, information security, business continuity, privacy protection, operational resilience, and regulatory compliance. They also offer a common language that organizations and vendors can use to demonstrate security maturity and support independent assurance.

While compliance with a recognized framework does not eliminate all risks, it provides confidence that vendors have implemented structured governance processes and accepted cybersecurity practices.

---

# Why Industry Standards Matter

Industry standards help organizations:

- Establish consistent security expectations.
- Evaluate vendor maturity.
- Simplify vendor assessments.
- Demonstrate regulatory compliance.
- Improve governance.
- Support independent assurance.
- Strengthen stakeholder confidence.
- Promote continual improvement.

Using recognized standards also reduces the need to create custom security requirements for every vendor.

---

|------|------|

| NIST SP 800-53 | Security and privacy controls for information systems |
| CIS Controls | Prioritized cybersecurity safeguards |
| PCI DSS | Security requirements for payment card data |

Organizations should select standards appropriate to their industry, regulatory environment, and risk profile.

# Common Regulatory Frameworks

- GDPR.

- DORA.

- CCPA and CPRA.
- National cybersecurity regulations.

Regulatory requirements often mandate effective third-party oversight, even when services are outsourced.

# Using Certifications in Vendor Assessments

Vendor certifications provide evidence that independent assessments have been performed.

Common certifications and assurance reports include:

- ISO/IEC 27001 certification.
- ISO/IEC 27701 certification.
- SOC 1 reports.
- SOC 2 Type II reports.
- PCI DSS Attestation of Compliance (AOC).
- Cybersecurity maturity certifications.
- Independent penetration test reports.
- External audit reports.

Organizations should review the scope, validity period, and any limitations of these reports rather than relying solely on the existence of a certificate.

---

# Mapping Standards to Vendor Requirements

Organizations often align vendor requirements with recognized frameworks.

Examples include:

| Vendor Requirement | Supporting Standard |
|------|------|
| Information Security Management | ISO/IEC 27001 |
| Access Control | ISO/IEC 27002, NIST SP 800-53 |
| Cloud Security | ISO/IEC 27017 |
| Privacy Protection | ISO/IEC 27701, GDPR |
| Business Continuity | ISO 22301 |
| Cybersecurity Governance | NIST CSF, COBIT |
| AI Governance | ISO/IEC 42001 |
| Payment Security | PCI DSS |

Framework mapping improves consistency across vendor assessments and contract requirements.

---

# Limitations of Certifications

Although certifications provide valuable assurance, organizations should recognize their limitations.

Examples include:

- Certifications represent a point-in-time assessment.
- Audit scope may not cover all services.
- Some controls may rely on sampling.
- New risks may emerge after certification.
- Certifications do not guarantee the absence of vulnerabilities.
- Vendors may operate multiple environments with different security controls.

Organizations should combine certifications with continuous monitoring, risk assessments, and periodic reviews.

# Practical Example

A multinational insurance company evaluates a cloud service provider that will host customer policy information. However, rather than relying solely on these certifications, the organization also reviews vulnerability management processes, incident response capabilities, contractual security requirements, and continuous monitoring arrangements. This layered approach provides greater confidence that the vendor can support both regulatory compliance and long-term cybersecurity objectives.

# Introduction

Regulatory compliance does not end with implementing security controls or signing vendor contracts. Organizations must also demonstrate that they have exercised appropriate oversight of third-party vendors and can provide objective evidence that regulatory requirements are being met.

Regulators, internal auditors, and external auditors frequently review an organization's Third-Party Risk Management (TPRM) program to determine whether vendor risks are properly identified, assessed, monitored, and managed. During these reviews, organizations are expected to present policies, risk assessments, contracts, audit reports, monitoring records, and evidence of corrective actions.

Effective compliance reporting enables organizations to demonstrate due diligence, maintain regulatory confidence, and support informed governance decisions.

---

# Why Compliance Reporting Matters

Compliance reporting provides evidence that an organization is actively managing third-party risks.

Effective reporting helps organizations:

- Demonstrate regulatory compliance.
- Support internal and external audits.
- Improve management oversight.
- Identify compliance gaps.
- Track remediation activities.
- Support executive decision-making.
- Maintain accountability.
- Build stakeholder confidence.

Accurate reporting also reduces the time and effort required during regulatory inspections.

---

# Regulatory Audit Objectives

Regulatory audits seek to verify that organizations have implemented effective governance over third-party relationships.

Auditors commonly evaluate whether the organization:

- Performs vendor due diligence.
- Assesses third-party risks.
- Includes security requirements in contracts.
- Monitors vendor performance.
- Reviews regulatory compliance.
- Maintains appropriate documentation.
- Responds effectively to incidents.
- Continuously improves its TPRM program.

The objective is to determine whether third-party risks are managed in a structured and consistent manner.

Because the organization maintains complete and well-organized records throughout the vendor lifecycle, it is able to demonstrate effective governance and satisfy regulatory expectations with minimal disruption.
