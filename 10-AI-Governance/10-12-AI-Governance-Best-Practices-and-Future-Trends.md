# Introduction

Artificial Intelligence continues to evolve at an unprecedented pace, creating new opportunities for innovation while introducing increasingly complex governance challenges. Organizations are rapidly adopting AI to improve decision-making, automate business processes, enhance customer experiences, and gain competitive advantages. However, the rapid advancement of AI technologies also requires organizations to continuously strengthen governance practices to address emerging risks, changing regulations, evolving cybersecurity threats, and growing public expectations regarding transparency, fairness, and accountability.

ISO/IEC 42001 provides a strong governance foundation, but achieving long-term success requires organizations to move beyond minimum compliance requirements. Mature organizations continuously refine their governance processes, adopt industry best practices, invest in employee competence, strengthen governance culture, and prepare for future technological developments. By embracing continual improvement and proactive governance, organizations can maintain trustworthy AI systems while supporting sustainable innovation in an increasingly regulated environment.

# Best Practices

Organizations should:

- Regularly assess AI governance maturity using structured frameworks.
- Benchmark governance practices against ISO/IEC 42001 and other recognized standards.
- Promote leadership commitment and organizational accountability.
- Integrate AI governance into enterprise Governance, Risk, and Compliance (GRC) programs.
- Foster a culture of ethics, transparency, and responsible AI.
- Continuously monitor emerging technologies, regulations, and industry best practices.
- Invest in employee competence and governance awareness.
- Treat continual improvement as a strategic business objective rather than a compliance activity.

# Practical Example

A multinational technology company operates AI platforms supporting software development, cybersecurity analytics, customer support, and business intelligence across global markets. Rather than focusing solely on regulatory compliance, the organization establishes a long-term AI governance maturity program aligned with ISO/IEC 42001. Annual maturity assessments evaluate leadership commitment, governance processes, AI risk management, operational controls, compliance performance, employee competence, and governance culture. Results are benchmarked against international standards and industry-leading practices to identify areas for improvement.

Based on the assessment results, executive leadership approves investments in governance automation, AI ethics training, advanced monitoring capabilities, and enhanced risk management processes. Governance dashboards measure progress through KPIs and KRIs, while internal audits verify that improvements have been successfully implemented. Over time, the organization advances from a developing governance program to a mature, optimized Artificial Intelligence Management System capable of supporting innovation, maintaining regulatory readiness, and strengthening stakeholder trust in accordance with ISO/IEC 42001.

# Practical Example

A global retail and e-commerce company uses generative AI for customer support, demand forecasting, product recommendations, and software development assistance. As AI adoption expands, the organization modernizes its governance program by implementing automated compliance monitoring, centralized AI inventories, real-time governance dashboards, and continuous monitoring of model performance, cybersecurity events, privacy risks, and fairness indicators. Governance teams also introduce structured oversight for third-party AI providers, including vendor risk assessments, contractual compliance reviews, and ongoing performance evaluations.

Executive management reviews governance metrics quarterly to evaluate AI maturity, regulatory readiness, operational risks, and opportunities for improvement. As new AI regulations and technologies emerge, governance policies, risk assessment methodologies, and employee training programs are updated to reflect changing requirements. Through proactive adoption of emerging governance practices, the organization strengthens its Artificial Intelligence Management System, supports responsible innovation, and maintains long-term alignment with ISO/IEC 42001 while remaining prepared for future developments in AI governance.

# Building a Sustainable AI Governance Program

Establishing an Artificial Intelligence Management System (AIMS) is not a one-time project but an ongoing organizational commitment. As AI technologies continue to evolve, organizations must ensure that their governance programs remain effective, adaptable, and aligned with business objectives. A sustainable AI governance program combines strong leadership, well-defined governance processes, skilled personnel, effective risk management, continuous monitoring, and a culture of continual improvement. These elements enable organizations to manage AI responsibly while supporting innovation and long-term business growth.

ISO/IEC 42001 promotes sustainability by embedding AI governance into normal business operations rather than treating it as a standalone compliance initiative. Organizations should continuously evaluate governance performance, respond to new risks, incorporate lessons learned, and update governance practices as technologies, regulations, and stakeholder expectations evolve. Sustainable governance ensures that AI systems remain trustworthy throughout their lifecycle.
