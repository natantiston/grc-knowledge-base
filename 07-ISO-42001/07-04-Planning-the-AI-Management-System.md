# Introduction

Planning is one of the most important stages of an Artificial Intelligence Management System (AIMS). While leadership establishes the strategic direction for AI governance, planning translates that direction into practical actions that help organizations manage risks, seize opportunities, and achieve governance objectives.

Clause 6 of ISO/IEC 42001 requires organizations to identify AI-related risks and opportunities, establish measurable objectives, and develop plans to achieve those objectives. Effective planning ensures that AI governance is proactive rather than reactive, enabling organizations to anticipate challenges before they become significant problems.

Unlike traditional project planning, AI governance planning considers technical, ethical, legal, operational, privacy, cybersecurity, and societal impacts throughout the AI lifecycle.

---

# Purpose of Clause 6

Clause 6 focuses on planning activities that support the effectiveness of the Artificial Intelligence Management System.

The clause requires organizations to:

- Identify AI risks.
- Identify opportunities.
- Plan governance activities.
- Establish AI objectives.
- Develop action plans.
- Support continual improvement.

The outputs of Clause 6 become inputs for operational controls implemented in Clause 8.

|---------------|-------------|------|

Governance planning seeks to maximize opportunities while reducing unacceptable risks.

# Risk-Based Thinking

ISO/IEC 42001 promotes a risk-based approach to governance.

Organizations should:

- Identify AI risks early.
- Evaluate potential impacts.
- Prioritize governance efforts.
- Allocate resources according to risk.
- Monitor changes over time.

Higher-risk AI systems generally require stronger governance controls than lower-risk systems.

# Best Practices

Organizations should:

- Integrate AI governance planning into existing enterprise planning, risk management, and strategic decision-making processes.
- Identify both risks and opportunities before implementing new AI systems or significantly changing existing ones.
- Apply a risk-based approach so that governance activities are proportionate to the potential impact of each AI use case.
- Consider operational, ethical, legal, privacy, cybersecurity, and societal factors when planning AI governance activities.
- Align AI planning with organizational objectives, regulatory requirements, business priorities, and stakeholder expectations.
- Review planning assumptions regularly as AI technologies, regulations, and business environments continue to evolve.
- Engage multidisciplinary teams during planning to ensure that technical, business, legal, compliance, and risk perspectives are represented.
- Document planning decisions and use them as inputs for operational controls, monitoring activities, and continual improvement initiatives.

These practices help organizations build an Artificial Intelligence Management System that proactively manages AI risks while enabling innovation, resilience, and long-term business value.

# Practical Example

A multinational logistics company plans to introduce Artificial Intelligence to optimize delivery routes, forecast customer demand, and automate warehouse operations. Before deployment, the AI Governance Committee conducts a planning exercise to identify operational risks, cybersecurity threats, privacy considerations, regulatory obligations, and potential ethical concerns associated with each AI initiative. The team also identifies opportunities to improve delivery efficiency, reduce transportation costs, and enhance customer satisfaction through responsible AI adoption.

Based on the planning results, the organization establishes governance objectives for AI transparency, human oversight, model performance, and regulatory compliance. Action plans are developed to perform AI risk assessments, strengthen cybersecurity controls, provide employee training, and implement continuous monitoring for AI models. By integrating AI governance planning into its broader business strategy, the organization ensures that innovation is supported by structured governance and effective risk management.

# AI Risk Assessment and Planning Actions

Effective planning depends on understanding how Artificial Intelligence can affect the organization. After identifying organizational objectives and AI opportunities, ISO/IEC 42001 requires organizations to identify, analyze, evaluate, and plan actions to address AI-related risks.

Unlike traditional IT risk assessments, AI risk assessments consider not only cybersecurity and operational issues but also ethical concerns, fairness, transparency, human oversight, privacy, regulatory compliance, and societal impact. The objective is to ensure that AI systems remain trustworthy throughout their lifecycle.

Risk assessment is not a one-time activity. It should be performed before AI systems are introduced, whenever significant changes occur, and periodically throughout the operation of the Artificial Intelligence Management System (AIMS).

---

# Purpose of AI Risk Assessment

AI risk assessments help organizations:

- Understand AI-related threats.
- Identify potential business impacts.
- Support informed decision-making.
- Prioritize governance activities.
- Determine appropriate controls.
- Allocate governance resources.
- Support continual improvement.

The results become key inputs into governance planning and operational controls.

# Risk Analysis

Risk analysis estimates the significance of each identified risk.

Organizations commonly evaluate:

### Likelihood

How likely is the event to occur?

Examples:

- Rare
- Unlikely
- Possible
- Likely
- Almost Certain

---

### Impact

What would happen if the event occurred?

Examples:

- Insignificant
- Minor
- Moderate
- Major
- Severe

Combining likelihood and impact helps determine overall risk levels.

---

# Example Risk Matrix

| Likelihood | Low Impact | Medium Impact | High Impact |
|------------|------------|---------------|-------------|
| High | Medium | High | Extreme |
| Medium | Low | Medium | High |
| Low | Low | Low | Medium |

Organizations may adapt the matrix to suit their risk management methodology.

# Risk Evaluation

Typical decisions include:

- Accept the risk.
- Reduce the risk.
- Transfer the risk.
- Avoid the activity.

The chosen treatment depends on organizational risk appetite and business objectives.

# Best Practices

Organizations should:

- Perform AI risk assessments before deploying new AI systems and whenever significant changes are made to existing solutions.
- Evaluate AI risks across technical, operational, ethical, legal, privacy, cybersecurity, and business dimensions rather than focusing solely on technology.
- Use a consistent and documented methodology for identifying assets, threats, vulnerabilities, likelihood, impact, and treatment decisions.
- Maintain an AI Risk Register with clearly assigned owners, treatment plans, review dates, and current risk status.
- Integrate AI risk assessments into enterprise risk management processes to support consistent governance across the organization.
- Regularly review risk assessments to account for evolving AI technologies, emerging threats, changing regulations, and operational experience.
- Involve multidisciplinary teams—including business, legal, compliance, cybersecurity, privacy, and AI specialists—to ensure comprehensive risk identification and evaluation.
- Use risk assessment results to prioritize governance controls, monitoring activities, resource allocation, and continual improvement initiatives.

These practices help organizations proactively manage AI-related risks while supporting trustworthy, secure, and responsible use of Artificial Intelligence.

---

# Practical Example

A financial institution plans to implement a machine learning model to automate loan approval decisions. Before deployment, the AI Governance Committee performs an AI risk assessment to identify potential threats such as biased training data, unauthorized access to customer information, model drift, inaccurate credit recommendations, and evolving regulatory requirements. The assessment also evaluates vulnerabilities including insufficient model testing, limited documentation, and inadequate human oversight during high-value lending decisions.

Using a standardized risk matrix, the organization rates algorithmic bias and unauthorized access as high-priority risks. To reduce these risks, the institution implements fairness testing, strengthens access controls, introduces human review for high-risk loan applications, and establishes continuous monitoring of model performance. All findings are recorded in the AI Risk Register and reviewed periodically as part of the organization's Artificial Intelligence Management System, ensuring that governance remains aligned with both business objectives and regulatory expectations.
