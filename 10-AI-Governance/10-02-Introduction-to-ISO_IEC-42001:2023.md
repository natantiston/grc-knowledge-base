# Introduction

Artificial Intelligence has become one of the fastest-growing technologies across every industry. Organizations are increasingly using AI to automate business processes, improve customer experiences, enhance cybersecurity, optimize operations, and support strategic decision-making. While AI offers tremendous opportunities, it also introduces significant risks involving ethics, privacy, cybersecurity, bias, transparency, accountability, and regulatory compliance.

Historically, organizations managed AI using existing governance frameworks such as information security, IT governance, data governance, and enterprise risk management. However, these frameworks were not specifically designed to address the unique characteristics of AI systems, including machine learning models, autonomous decision-making, generative AI, and continuously evolving algorithms.

To address this gap, the **International Organization for Standardization (ISO)** and the **International Electrotechnical Commission (IEC)** published **ISO/IEC 42001:2023**, the world's first international management system standard dedicated to Artificial Intelligence. The standard provides organizations with a structured framework for establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving an **Artificial Intelligence Management System (AIMS)**.

Rather than prescribing how AI models should be developed, ISO/IEC 42001 focuses on governing the entire AI lifecycle. It enables organizations to manage AI risks, establish accountability, promote responsible AI, comply with legal and regulatory requirements, and build trust among customers, regulators, business partners, and society.

---

# What is ISO/IEC 42001?

**ISO/IEC 42001:2023** is an international management system standard that specifies requirements for establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving an Artificial Intelligence Management System (AIMS).

The standard provides organizations with a systematic approach to governing AI by integrating:

- Leadership and governance
- AI risk management
- Responsible AI principles
- Operational controls
- Performance evaluation
- Continuous improvement

Like other ISO management system standards, ISO/IEC 42001 follows a risk-based approach and promotes continual improvement through the Plan-Do-Check-Act (PDCA) methodology.

---

# Why ISO/IEC 42001 Was Developed

The rapid growth of AI created new governance challenges that traditional management systems could not fully address.

Organizations required guidance for:

- Managing AI-specific risks
- Promoting ethical AI
- Protecting privacy
- Reducing algorithmic bias
- Ensuring transparency
- Establishing accountability
- Supporting regulatory compliance
- Building stakeholder trust

ISO/IEC 42001 provides organizations with internationally recognized best practices for addressing these challenges.

# Understanding the Artificial Intelligence Management System (AIMS)

At the core of ISO/IEC 42001 is the **Artificial Intelligence Management System (AIMS)**. Similar to how ISO/IEC 27001 establishes an Information Security Management System (ISMS) and ISO 9001 establishes a Quality Management System (QMS), ISO/IEC 42001 introduces a structured management system specifically designed to govern Artificial Intelligence.

An AIMS is more than a collection of policies or technical controls. It is a comprehensive management framework that enables organizations to establish governance, define responsibilities, manage AI risks, monitor performance, and continually improve AI operations throughout the entire AI lifecycle. Rather than focusing on individual AI models, AIMS governs the people, processes, technology, and organizational structures that support responsible AI.

---

# What is an Artificial Intelligence Management System (AIMS)?

An Artificial Intelligence Management System (AIMS) is a structured framework of policies, processes, governance mechanisms, and controls that enables an organization to manage AI responsibly and consistently.

An effective AIMS helps organizations:

- Govern AI across the enterprise
- Identify and manage AI risks
- Define organizational responsibilities
- Support ethical AI
- Ensure regulatory compliance
- Monitor AI performance
- Improve AI decision-making
- Promote continual improvement

The objective is to ensure that AI systems remain trustworthy, secure, transparent, and aligned with organizational goals.

# The Plan-Do-Check-Act (PDCA) Model for AI Governance

ISO/IEC 42001 adopts the **Plan-Do-Check-Act (PDCA)** methodology, a well-established management approach used across many ISO standards, including ISO 9001 (Quality Management) and ISO/IEC 27001 (Information Security Management). The PDCA model enables organizations to manage Artificial Intelligence systematically through continual planning, implementation, evaluation, and improvement rather than treating governance as a one-time compliance exercise.

Because AI technologies evolve rapidly and their risks can change over time, governance must be continuous. New regulations, emerging threats, changing business objectives, model drift, evolving datasets, and technological advancements require organizations to regularly review and improve their AI governance processes. The PDCA cycle provides the structured framework that supports this continual improvement.

---

# Why PDCA is Important for AI Governance

Artificial Intelligence systems continuously learn, adapt, and interact with changing environments. Unlike traditional software, AI models may experience declining performance, unintended bias, new security vulnerabilities, or regulatory changes after deployment.

The PDCA methodology enables organizations to:

- Continuously manage AI risks
- Improve governance maturity
- Adapt to changing regulations
- Monitor AI performance
- Strengthen accountability
- Improve operational efficiency
- Support responsible AI innovation
- Maintain stakeholder trust

Rather than reacting to problems, organizations proactively improve AI governance through regular review and refinement.

---

# PLAN

The **Plan** phase establishes the foundation of the Artificial Intelligence Management System.

Organizations should:

- Define AI governance objectives
- Establish AI policies
- Identify stakeholders
- Determine the scope of the AIMS
- Inventory AI systems
- Identify legal and regulatory obligations
- Perform AI risk assessments
- Define AI performance objectives
- Allocate governance responsibilities

Effective planning ensures AI initiatives align with business strategy and organizational risk appetite.

---

# DO

During the **Do** phase, organizations implement the governance framework and operational controls defined during planning.

Typical activities include:

- Develop or acquire AI systems
- Implement AI governance policies
- Deploy AI operational controls
- Conduct model validation
- Deliver AI awareness and training
- Implement cybersecurity controls
- Protect personal data
- Document AI processes
- Deploy AI systems into production

This phase transforms governance plans into operational practices.

---

# CHECK

The **Check** phase evaluates whether the Artificial Intelligence Management System is operating effectively.

Organizations should monitor:

- AI model performance
- AI risk indicators
- Compliance status
- Security events
- Bias assessments
- Internal audit findings
- AI incidents
- Regulatory changes
- Business objectives

Management reviews and internal audits provide assurance that governance processes remain effective and aligned with organizational goals.

---

# ACT

The **Act** phase focuses on continual improvement.

Organizations should:

- Correct identified deficiencies
- Address audit findings
- Update AI policies
- Improve operational controls
- Enhance AI documentation
- Implement corrective actions
- Improve governance maturity
- Review lessons learned
- Update risk assessments

The objective is to ensure the Artificial Intelligence Management System evolves alongside technological, regulatory, and business changes.

---

# Continuous PDCA Cycle

The PDCA methodology is not a linear process but a continuous improvement cycle.

```text
PLAN

↓

DO

↓

CHECK

↓

ACT

↓

CONTINUAL IMPROVEMENT

↓

PLAN
```

Each completed cycle strengthens governance maturity and prepares the organization for future AI challenges.

# Practical Example

A multinational retail company deploys AI to optimize inventory forecasting, personalize customer recommendations, and automate supply chain planning. Before implementation, executive management defines governance objectives, performs AI risk assessments, assigns accountability, and establishes AI policies as part of the **Plan** phase. During the **Do** phase, development teams build and validate AI models, cybersecurity teams implement technical controls, and employees receive AI governance training before the systems are deployed into production.

Once operational, the organization enters the **Check** phase by continuously monitoring model accuracy, bias indicators, cybersecurity events, regulatory compliance, and customer feedback. Internal Audit performs periodic reviews of the Artificial Intelligence Management System, while executive management evaluates governance performance through dashboards and management reviews. During the **Act** phase, identified weaknesses lead to updated AI policies, improved security controls, revised risk assessments, and enhanced governance procedures. By continuously repeating the PDCA cycle, the organization strengthens AI governance maturity, supports responsible innovation, and maintains alignment with ISO/IEC 42001 requirements.
