# Introduction

Once an organization has identified its information security risks and determined how those risks will be treated, the next step is to establish **clear objectives** for its Information Security Management System (ISMS).

This is the purpose of **Clause 6.2 – Information Security Objectives and Planning to Achieve Them**.

Information security objectives define **what the organization wants to achieve** through its ISMS.

These objectives provide measurable targets that allow management to determine whether the ISMS is improving over time.

Without defined objectives, organizations cannot demonstrate whether their information security program is effective or whether investments in security are producing meaningful business outcomes. Clause 6.2 requires organizations to establish information security objectives at relevant functions and levels, ensure they are aligned with the information security policy, measurable where practicable, monitored, communicated, updated as appropriate, and supported by documented plans for achieving them. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

---

# Why Information Security Objectives Matter

The Information Security Policy established in Clause 5 provides strategic direction.

Clause 6.2 converts that direction into practical, measurable goals.

For example:

Information Security Policy Statement:

> "The organization is committed to protecting customer information."

Information Security Objective:

> "Reduce phishing-related security incidents by 40% within the next 12 months."

The objective is far more useful because it allows the organization to measure success.

Objectives also help organizations:

- Prioritize security initiatives.
- Measure ISMS performance.
- Allocate resources effectively.
- Demonstrate continual improvement.
- Support management decision-making.
- Meet audit requirements.

---

# Requirements of Clause 6.2

ISO/IEC 27001 requires organizations to establish information security objectives at relevant functions and levels.

These objectives must:

- Be consistent with the Information Security Policy.
- Be measurable where practicable.
- Consider applicable legal, regulatory, contractual, and business requirements.
- Consider the results of risk assessments and risk treatment.
- Be monitored.
- Be communicated.
- Be updated when necessary.
- Be maintained as documented information. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

---

# Characteristics of Good Security Objectives

Effective objectives are often based on the **SMART** principle.

| SMART Principle | Description |
|-----------------|-------------|
| **Specific** | Clearly defines what will be achieved. |
| **Measurable** | Progress can be tracked using metrics or KPIs. |
| **Achievable** | Realistic considering available resources. |
| **Relevant** | Supports business and security objectives. |
| **Time-bound** | Includes a target completion date. |

For example:

Poor Objective:

> Improve cybersecurity.

Better Objective:

> Reduce critical security vulnerabilities by 80% within six months through monthly vulnerability scanning and remediation.

The second objective provides a clear target, timeframe, and measurable outcome.

---

# Examples of Information Security Objectives

Different organizations establish objectives based on their business priorities and risk profile.

Examples include:

| Objective | Measurement |
|-----------|-------------|
| Reduce phishing incidents | 40% reduction within 12 months |
| Improve vulnerability management | Patch 95% of critical vulnerabilities within 14 days |
| Increase security awareness | 100% employee training completion annually |
| Improve incident response | Respond to critical incidents within one hour |
| Strengthen access management | 100% Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) adoption for privileged accounts |
| Improve supplier security | Complete security assessments for all critical suppliers annually |

These objectives should directly support the organization's Information Security Policy and Risk Treatment Plan.

# Relationship to Risk Management

Information security objectives should not be created independently.

Instead, they should be based on:

- Information security risks.
- Risk treatment decisions.
- Business objectives.
- Regulatory obligations.
- Customer requirements.
- Leadership priorities.

For example:

**Identified Risk:**

Frequent phishing attacks targeting employees.

**Risk Treatment:**

Implement awareness training and email filtering.

**Information Security Objective:**

Reduce successful phishing attacks by 50% within one year.

This approach ensures that objectives address real organizational risks rather than generic security goals.

---

# Practical Example

A financial institution identifies delayed vulnerability remediation as a significant information security risk.

To address this issue, management establishes the following objective:

**Objective:**

Patch **95% of critical vulnerabilities within 14 days** of identification.

To achieve this objective, the organization defines:

- **Responsible Owner:** IT Infrastructure Manager
- **Resources:** Vulnerability scanner, patch management platform, security engineers
- **Target Date:** Ongoing, measured monthly
- **Performance Indicator:** Percentage of critical vulnerabilities remediated within the target timeframe
- **Review Method:** Monthly ISMS performance reports and quarterly management reviews

By linking measurable objectives to identified risks, the organization can demonstrate continual improvement, support management decision-making, and provide objective evidence of ISMS effectiveness during ISO/IEC 27001 certification audits.

# Planning to Achieve Information Security Objectives

Establishing information security objectives is only the first step.

Clause **6.2** also requires organizations to **plan how those objectives will be achieved**.

An objective without a plan is simply an intention.

ISO/IEC 27001 therefore requires organizations to define the activities, resources, responsibilities, timelines, and measurement methods needed to successfully achieve each objective. This ensures that objectives are actionable rather than aspirational. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

---

# What Must Be Planned?

For every information security objective, the organization should determine:

- What will be done.
- What resources are required.
- Who is responsible.
- When it will be completed.
- How the results will be evaluated.

These planning elements create accountability and provide management with a structured way to monitor progress.

---

# 1. What Will Be Done?

The organization should define the activities required to achieve the objective.

Example Objective:

> Reduce phishing incidents by 50%.

Planned activities may include:

- Conduct quarterly phishing simulations.
- Deploy advanced email filtering.
- Improve spam detection.
- Update email security policies.
- Deliver employee awareness training.

Breaking objectives into specific actions makes implementation easier to manage.

---

# 2. Resources Required

Every objective requires adequate resources.

These may include:

### Financial Resources

- Security software
- Consulting services
- Training budget
- Cloud security services

---

### Human Resources

- Security analysts
- IT administrators
- Project managers
- Department managers
- External consultants

---

### Technical Resources

- SIEM platform
- Vulnerability scanners
- Endpoint Detection and Response (EDR)
- Identity and Access Management (IAM)
- Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

Without appropriate resources, objectives are unlikely to be achieved successfully.

---

# 3. Responsibility

Each objective should have a clearly assigned owner.

Possible owners include:

- Chief Information Security Officer (CISO)
- Information Security Manager
- IT Manager
- Infrastructure Manager
- Risk Manager
- Department Manager
- Process Owner

Assigning ownership improves accountability and helps ensure progress is monitored and reported.

---

# 4. Completion Timeline

Objectives should include realistic target dates.

Examples:

| Objective | Completion Target |
|-----------|------------------|
| Complete annual awareness training | 31 December |
| Deploy MFA to privileged accounts | 30 September |
| Complete supplier security assessments | 30 November |
| Reduce critical vulnerabilities | Within six months |

Defined timelines help management track implementation and identify delays early.

---

# 5. Performance Evaluation

Organizations should establish how success will be measured.

Examples include:

| Objective | Performance Indicator |
|-----------|----------------------|
| Reduce phishing incidents | Number of successful phishing attacks |
| Improve vulnerability management | Percentage of critical vulnerabilities patched within SLA |
| Improve awareness | Employee training completion rate |
| Improve incident response | Mean Time to Respond (MTTR) |
| Improve supplier security | Percentage of critical suppliers assessed |

These measurements provide objective evidence that the ISMS is delivering results.

# Monitoring Progress

Objectives should not be reviewed only during annual audits.

Organizations should monitor progress regularly through:

- Monthly KPI dashboards.
- Security performance reports.
- Management review meetings.
- Internal audits.
- Risk review meetings.
- Executive reporting.

If progress falls behind schedule, management should determine:

- Why the objective is not being achieved.
- Whether additional resources are required.
- Whether corrective actions should be implemented.
- Whether the objective itself needs to be revised.

Regular monitoring ensures objectives remain relevant and achievable.

---

# Practical Example

A telecommunications company establishes the following information security objective:

**Objective:**

Implement **Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)** for all privileged user accounts.

The implementation plan includes:

| Planning Element | Example |
|------------------|---------|
| **What will be done** | Deploy MFA across all administrative systems |
| **Resources Required** | MFA software, implementation team, training materials |
| **Responsible Owner** | Infrastructure Manager |
| **Target Completion** | 30 September |
| **Performance Measurement** | 100% of privileged accounts protected by MFA |

Progress is reviewed monthly during ISMS meetings, and results are presented during quarterly management reviews.

By documenting and monitoring this plan, the organization demonstrates compliance with Clause 6.2 while ensuring that its security objectives are measurable, achievable, and aligned with business priorities. This structured approach enables continual improvement and provides auditors with clear evidence that the organization not only defines information security objectives but also manages them effectively. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

# Clause 6.3 – Planning of Changes

Organizations are constantly evolving.

They may introduce new technologies, launch new services, migrate to the cloud, restructure departments, or adopt new regulatory requirements.

Each of these changes can affect the effectiveness of the **Information Security Management System (ISMS).**

For this reason, ISO/IEC 27001:2022 introduced **Clause 6.3 – Planning of Changes**.

The clause is short, but its purpose is significant:

> **When an organization determines that changes to the ISMS are necessary, those changes must be carried out in a planned manner.**

This prevents organizations from making unplanned or uncontrolled changes that could introduce new security risks or reduce the effectiveness of existing controls. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

---

# Why Planning Changes Is Important

Every significant change has the potential to affect:

- Information security risks.
- Security controls.
- Policies and procedures.
- Roles and responsibilities.
- Regulatory compliance.
- Business operations.

If changes are made without proper planning, they can result in:

- Security gaps.
- Loss of compliance.
- Service disruptions.
- Inconsistent documentation.
- Increased operational risk.
- Failed certification audits.

Planning changes ensures that the ISMS continues to achieve its intended outcomes even as the organization evolves. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

---

# Examples of Changes Affecting the ISMS

Not every organizational change requires formal ISMS planning.

However, significant changes often do.

Examples include:

### Business Changes

- Organizational restructuring.
- Business mergers or acquisitions.
- Opening new offices.
- Expanding into new countries.
- Launching new services.

---

### Technology Changes

- Cloud migration.
- Data center relocation.
- Network redesign.
- New cybersecurity platforms.
- Identity and Access Management (IAM) implementation.
- Artificial Intelligence integration.

---

### Regulatory Changes

Examples include:

- New privacy legislation.
- NIS2 compliance.
- DORA implementation.
- Industry regulatory updates.
- Customer contractual security requirements.

---

### ISMS Changes

Examples include:

- Updating the ISMS scope.
- Changing the risk assessment methodology.
- Revising the Information Security Policy.
- Implementing new Annex A controls.
- Updating the Statement of Applicability (SoA).

Each of these changes should be assessed before implementation to ensure they do not negatively affect the ISMS.

# What Should Be Planned?

Although Clause 6.3 is brief, organizations should typically consider the following before implementing significant changes:

### Purpose of the Change

Why is the change necessary?

Examples:

- Reduce risk.
- Improve efficiency.
- Meet regulatory requirements.
- Support business growth.
- Address audit findings.

---

### Impact Assessment

Determine how the change may affect:

- Business processes.
- Information assets.
- Existing controls.
- Risk levels.
- Documentation.
- Employees.
- Customers.
- Suppliers.

A simple impact assessment helps identify unintended consequences before implementation.

---

### Resources

Organizations should confirm that adequate resources are available.

Examples include:

- Budget.
- Personnel.
- Technology.
- Training.
- Time.

Without sufficient resources, even well-planned changes may fail.

---

### Roles and Responsibilities

The organization should identify:

- Who approves the change.
- Who implements the change.
- Who reviews the results.
- Who communicates the change.

Clear accountability improves coordination and reduces implementation risks. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

# Integrating Change Management with the ISMS

Many organizations already have an IT Change Management process.

Clause 6.3 does **not** require creating a completely separate process.

Instead, organizations can integrate ISMS planning with existing change management processes by ensuring that information security considerations are included whenever significant changes are proposed.

For example, before deploying a new cloud platform, the organization may:

- Perform an information security risk assessment.
- Review applicable Annex A controls.
- Update the Statement of Applicability (SoA).
- Revise affected security policies.
- Train employees on new procedures.
- Verify that regulatory requirements continue to be met.

This integration avoids duplicated effort while maintaining compliance with ISO/IEC 27001. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

---

# Evidence Auditors Look For

During an ISO/IEC 27001 certification audit, auditors typically verify that:

- Significant ISMS changes are planned before implementation.
- Information security risks are considered during change planning.
- Responsibilities for implementing changes are assigned.
- Relevant documentation is updated after changes.
- Risk assessments and the Risk Treatment Plan are updated where necessary.
- Major changes are reviewed and approved by management when appropriate.

Evidence may include:

- Change requests.
- Change approval records.
- Management review minutes.
- Updated risk assessments.
- Revised policies and procedures.
- Updated Statement of Applicability (SoA).
- Implementation and testing records. :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}

# Practical Example

A software company decides to migrate its customer application from an on-premises data center to a public cloud provider.

Before the migration, the organization:

- Performs a security risk assessment for the cloud environment.
- Identifies new risks related to identity management, data residency, and shared responsibility.
- Updates the Risk Treatment Plan.
- Revises the Statement of Applicability (SoA) to include additional cloud security controls.
- Updates security policies and operating procedures.
- Trains system administrators on the new cloud platform.
- Conducts testing before the production rollout.

Following implementation, management reviews the results to confirm that the migration achieved its business objectives without weakening the ISMS.

This planned approach demonstrates compliance with Clause 6.3 by ensuring that significant organizational changes are managed systematically, risks are addressed before implementation, and the integrity of the Information Security Management System is maintained throughout the change process.

# Clause 6 – Planning Summary and Key Takeaways

Clause 6 is often considered the **planning engine** of the Information Security Management System (ISMS).

Everything established in the previous clauses—understanding the organization (Clause 4) and leadership commitment (Clause 5)—is transformed into practical actions through planning.

By the end of Clause 6, the organization should know:

- What its information security risks are.
- How those risks will be treated.
- Which security controls will be implemented.
- What information security objectives it intends to achieve.
- How progress will be measured.
- How future changes to the ISMS will be managed.

This makes Clause 6 one of the most important sections of ISO/IEC 27001 because it provides the roadmap for implementing and continually improving the ISMS. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

---

# The Clause 6 Journey

The planning process follows a logical sequence.

### Step 1 – Understand Risks

The organization identifies:

- Internal and external issues.
- Information security risks.
- Business opportunities.
- Legal and regulatory obligations.

↓

### Step 2 – Assess Risks

The organization:

- Defines risk criteria.
- Identifies assets.
- Identifies threats and vulnerabilities.
- Calculates risk levels.
- Determines acceptable risk.

↓

### Step 3 – Treat Risks

Management decides whether to:

- Reduce
- Avoid
- Transfer
- Accept

appropriate risks and selects suitable security controls.

↓

### Step 4 – Establish Objectives

The organization creates measurable information security objectives that support:

- Business goals.
- Risk treatment.
- Regulatory compliance.
- Continual improvement.

↓

### Step 5 – Plan Changes

Any significant modification to the ISMS is planned before implementation to ensure that security effectiveness is maintained.

# Common Mistakes Organizations Make

Organizations frequently encounter the following issues when implementing Clause 6:

### Risk Assessments Without a Defined Methodology

Some organizations identify risks but do not establish a consistent process for evaluating likelihood, impact, and risk acceptance.

---

### Generic Objectives

Objectives such as:

> Improve cybersecurity.

are difficult to measure.

Better objectives are specific and measurable, such as:

> Patch 95% of critical vulnerabilities within 14 days.

---

### Weak Link Between Risks and Controls

Security controls should be selected because they address identified risks—not simply because they appear in Annex A.

---

### Outdated Documentation

Risk assessments, the Statement of Applicability, and security objectives should be reviewed and updated whenever significant business or technology changes occur.

---

### Lack of Management Involvement

Although security teams perform much of the planning work, leadership is responsible for approving risk treatment decisions, allocating resources, and monitoring progress.

These are among the most common findings identified during ISO/IEC 27001 certification audits. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

# Practical Example

A software-as-a-service (SaaS) company is preparing for ISO/IEC 27001 certification.

After completing Clause 6, the organization has produced:

- A documented risk assessment methodology.
- A Risk Register containing 65 identified risks.
- A Risk Treatment Plan assigning actions and owners.
- A Statement of Applicability documenting selected Annex A controls.
- Five measurable information security objectives.
- A documented change planning process for cloud infrastructure updates.

When the certification auditor reviews the ISMS, every implemented security control can be traced back to an identified risk, every objective has measurable performance indicators, and every significant change follows a documented planning process.

This demonstrates a mature and well-governed planning process that fully supports the organization's Information Security Management System.
