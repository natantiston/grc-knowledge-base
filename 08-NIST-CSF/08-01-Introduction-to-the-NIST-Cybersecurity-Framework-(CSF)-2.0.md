# Introduction

Cybersecurity is no longer solely an information technology concern—it is a business risk that affects organizations of every size, sector, and geography. Organizations face increasingly sophisticated cyber threats, evolving regulatory requirements, expanding digital ecosystems, and growing dependence on cloud services, Artificial Intelligence, operational technology (OT), and third-party suppliers.

To help organizations manage these challenges, the **National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST)** developed the **Cybersecurity Framework (CSF)**. Rather than prescribing specific technologies or security products, the framework provides a flexible, risk-based approach for identifying, protecting, detecting, responding to, and recovering from cybersecurity risks.

In February 2024, NIST released **Cybersecurity Framework (CSF) 2.0**, expanding the framework beyond critical infrastructure to organizations of all types and introducing a new **Govern (GV)** function that strengthens executive accountability and cybersecurity governance.

---

# What is the NIST Cybersecurity Framework?

The NIST Cybersecurity Framework is a voluntary framework that helps organizations:

- Understand cybersecurity risks.
- Establish cybersecurity governance.
- Protect critical assets.
- Detect cyber threats.
- Respond effectively to incidents.
- Recover from disruptions.
- Continually improve cybersecurity capabilities.

The framework provides a common language that enables executives, technical teams, regulators, auditors, suppliers, and business stakeholders to communicate about cybersecurity consistently.

Unlike detailed technical standards, the CSF focuses on **desired cybersecurity outcomes**, allowing organizations to select controls and technologies that best fit their environment.

---

# History of the Framework

The Cybersecurity Framework was developed following the **United States Executive Order 13636**, which directed NIST to improve the cybersecurity of critical infrastructure.

Major milestones include:

| Version | Release | Highlights |
|----------|----------|------------|
| CSF 1.0 | 2014 | Initial framework for critical infrastructure |
| CSF 1.1 | 2018 | Enhanced identity management, supply chain risk, and authentication guidance |
| CSF 2.0 | 2024 | Introduced Govern function, expanded applicability to all organizations, improved governance and implementation guidance |

CSF 2.0 reflects lessons learned from nearly a decade of global adoption and addresses emerging cybersecurity risks and governance expectations.

Cybersecurity activities are prioritized according to organizational risk.

### Outcome-Focused

# NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF) 2.0 Structure and Core Concepts

The NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF) 2.0 is designed to help organizations manage cybersecurity risk using a common language that can be understood by executives, business leaders, risk managers, auditors, regulators, and technical teams.

Unlike many technical cybersecurity standards, the CSF does not prescribe specific security controls or technologies. Instead, it defines **desired cybersecurity outcomes** that organizations should achieve to protect their operations, information, and services.

CSF 2.0 is organized into three integrated components:

- CSF Core
- Organizational Profiles
- Implementation Tiers

Together, these components enable organizations to assess their current cybersecurity posture, define future goals, prioritize investments, and continuously improve cyber resilience.

---

# The Three Components of CSF 2.0

```
NIST Cybersecurity Framework 2.0

│

├── CSF Core

├── Organizational Profiles

└── Implementation Tiers
```

Each component serves a different purpose while supporting the overall cybersecurity program.

---

# 1. CSF Core

The **CSF Core** is the heart of the framework.

It provides a structured set of cybersecurity outcomes organized into six high-level functions.

These functions represent the lifecycle of managing cybersecurity risk.

The six functions are:

| Function | Purpose |
|----------|---------|
| Govern (GV) | Establish cybersecurity governance and oversight |
| Identify (ID) | Understand organizational assets, risks, and business environment |
| Protect (PR) | Safeguard systems, data, and services |
| Detect (DE) | Discover cybersecurity events quickly |
| Respond (RS) | Take action during cybersecurity incidents |
| Recover (RC) | Restore operations and improve resilience after incidents |

These functions are not sequential steps. They operate continuously and support one another throughout the organization's cybersecurity lifecycle.

---

# The New Govern Function

One of the most significant changes introduced in CSF 2.0 is the addition of the **Govern (GV)** function.

In CSF 1.1, governance activities were distributed across other functions. CSF 2.0 elevates governance into its own dedicated function to emphasize executive responsibility for cybersecurity risk management.

The Govern function includes activities such as:

- Establishing cybersecurity policies.
- Defining governance structures.
- Assigning roles and responsibilities.
- Integrating cybersecurity into enterprise risk management.
- Managing third-party cybersecurity risks.
- Measuring cybersecurity performance.
- Supporting continual improvement.

This addition reinforces the idea that cybersecurity is an organizational responsibility, not solely an IT function.

---

# Categories and Subcategories

Each CSF Function is divided into **Categories**, which group related cybersecurity outcomes.

Each Category contains multiple **Subcategories** describing specific outcomes organizations should achieve.

The structure can be visualized as:

```

```

This hierarchical structure makes the framework flexible and scalable.

# 2. Organizational Profiles

Profiles help organizations compare their current cybersecurity capabilities with their desired future state.

Two types of profiles are commonly developed:

### Current Profile

Represents:

- Existing cybersecurity capabilities.
- Implemented controls.
- Current governance practices.
- Present level of maturity.

---

### Target Profile

Represents:

- Desired cybersecurity outcomes.
- Future governance objectives.
- Planned improvements.
- Strategic priorities.

Comparing these profiles enables organizations to identify capability gaps and prioritize improvement initiatives.

---

```

↓

```

The gap analysis helps answer questions such as:

- Which cybersecurity outcomes have already been achieved?
- Which outcomes require improvement?
- Which initiatives should be prioritized based on business risk?

Gap analysis forms the foundation for strategic cybersecurity planning.

---

# 3. Implementation Tiers

Implementation Tiers help organizations evaluate the maturity of their cybersecurity risk management practices.

The four tiers are:

| Tier | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Tier 1 | Partial |
| Tier 2 | Risk Informed |
| Tier 3 | Repeatable |
| Tier 4 | Adaptive |

These tiers do **not** represent compliance levels or certification grades. Instead, they describe how effectively cybersecurity risk management is integrated into organizational decision-making and operations.

# Best Practices

Organizations should:

- Understand the purpose of each CSF component before beginning implementation to ensure the framework is applied consistently across the organization.
- Use the CSF Core as the primary reference for defining cybersecurity outcomes rather than as a checklist of technical controls.
- Develop Current and Target Profiles that accurately reflect business priorities, organizational risk tolerance, regulatory obligations, and strategic objectives.
- Perform regular gap analyses to identify capability weaknesses and prioritize cybersecurity initiatives according to business risk and available resources.
- Treat Implementation Tiers as indicators of cybersecurity risk management maturity rather than compliance or certification levels.
- Integrate the Govern function into enterprise governance processes to strengthen executive accountability and strategic oversight of cybersecurity.
- Review Profiles and Tiers periodically to ensure they remain aligned with evolving technologies, cyber threats, organizational changes, and regulatory requirements.
- Use the framework's common language to improve communication between executives, business leaders, cybersecurity professionals, auditors, regulators, suppliers, and other stakeholders.

These practices enable organizations to implement the NIST Cybersecurity Framework in a structured, scalable, and business-focused manner while supporting continual improvement of cybersecurity governance and resilience.

# Practical Example

A multinational financial services company adopts the NIST Cybersecurity Framework 2.0 to strengthen enterprise cybersecurity governance across its online banking, payment processing, cloud infrastructure, and customer service platforms. The cybersecurity team begins by mapping existing security practices to the six CSF Core functions and develops a Current Profile that reflects the organization's existing capabilities. Executive leadership then defines a Target Profile aligned with business objectives, regulatory requirements, and the organization's risk appetite. # NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF) 2.0 and Its Relationship with Other Cybersecurity Frameworks

One of the greatest strengths of the NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF) 2.0 is its ability to integrate with other cybersecurity, governance, risk management, privacy, and compliance frameworks. Most organizations do not rely on a single standard to manage cybersecurity. Instead, they build a governance ecosystem where multiple frameworks work together to address business objectives, regulatory obligations, operational risks, and technical security requirements.

Rather than replacing existing standards, the CSF acts as an overarching framework that helps organizations organize cybersecurity activities, communicate risk, prioritize investments, and measure cybersecurity performance. This flexibility has made the NIST CSF one of the most widely adopted cybersecurity governance frameworks globally.

These practices enable organizations to build a mature cybersecurity program that aligns governance, operational security, regulatory compliance, and enterprise risk management into a cohesive and sustainable framework.

# Practical Adoption of the NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF) 2.0

Understanding the NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF) 2.0 is only the first step toward improving an organization's cybersecurity posture. # Best Practices

Organizations should:

- Begin implementation by understanding business objectives, organizational context, regulatory obligations, and enterprise risk rather than focusing solely on technical security controls.
- Develop accurate Current and Target Profiles that reflect both existing cybersecurity capabilities and long-term strategic goals.
- Prioritize cybersecurity initiatives using a risk-based approach that considers business impact, critical assets, legal requirements, available resources, and organizational priorities.
- Establish strong executive sponsorship and governance structures to ensure cybersecurity is managed as an enterprise-wide business responsibility.
- Integrate the NIST Cybersecurity Framework with existing governance, risk management, privacy, business continuity, and compliance programs to maximize efficiency and consistency.
- Define measurable KPIs and KRIs that enable leadership to evaluate cybersecurity performance, monitor improvement initiatives, and support informed decision-making.
- Conduct regular reviews of cybersecurity capabilities, threat intelligence, emerging technologies, and organizational changes to keep the implementation aligned with evolving risks.
- Promote a culture of continual improvement through employee awareness, cross-functional collaboration, lessons learned, and periodic reassessment of cybersecurity objectives.

These practices help organizations transform the NIST Cybersecurity Framework from a reference document into an effective enterprise cybersecurity management program that supports resilience, regulatory compliance, and long-term business success.

# Practical Example

A global retail organization operates thousands of physical stores, multiple e-commerce platforms, cloud-based payment systems, and regional distribution centers. To strengthen cybersecurity governance, executive leadership adopts the NIST Cybersecurity Framework 2.0 as the foundation of its enterprise cybersecurity program. The organization begins by identifying critical business services, digital assets, payment systems, customer information, and third-party service providers. A Current Profile assessment reveals strong endpoint security and network monitoring capabilities but identifies weaknesses in supplier risk management, executive governance, cloud security visibility, and incident response coordination across international business units.

Using a structured gap analysis, leadership prioritizes cybersecurity initiatives according to business risk and strategic objectives. Governance committees are established under the Govern function, supplier security assessments become mandatory for critical vendors, cloud monitoring capabilities are expanded, and cybersecurity performance dashboards are introduced for executive reporting. Over the following year, internal assessments demonstrate improved incident response times, greater visibility into enterprise cyber risk, increased compliance with regulatory requirements, and stronger collaboration between business leaders and cybersecurity teams. By following the NIST Cybersecurity Framework implementation lifecycle, the organization successfully transforms cybersecurity into an integrated enterprise governance capability that continuously supports business resilience and digital growth.
