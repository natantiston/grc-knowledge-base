# Introduction

Artificial Intelligence (AI) has rapidly become one of the most transformative technologies of the modern era. Organizations across every industry now use AI to automate processes, improve decision-making, enhance customer experiences, detect fraud, optimize supply chains, generate content, and support strategic planning.

However, the growing adoption of AI also introduces significant risks. AI systems can produce inaccurate outputs, amplify bias, violate privacy, create cybersecurity vulnerabilities, generate harmful content, or make decisions that are difficult to explain. These risks can affect customers, employees, regulators, and society as a whole.

To help organizations govern AI responsibly, the International Organization for Standardization (ISO) and the International Electrotechnical Commission (IEC) published **ISO/IEC 42001**, the world's first international management system standard specifically designed for Artificial Intelligence.

---

# What is ISO/IEC 42001?

**ISO/IEC 42001:2023** is an international standard that specifies requirements for establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving an **Artificial Intelligence Management System (AIMS).**

An AIMS provides a structured framework that enables organizations to:

- Govern AI responsibly.
- Identify and manage AI-related risks.
- Promote ethical AI practices.
- Ensure transparency and accountability.
- Support legal and regulatory compliance.
- Monitor AI performance.
- Continually improve AI governance.

Like ISO/IEC 27001 and ISO 22301, ISO/IEC 42001 follows the High-Level Structure (HLS) used by ISO management system standards.

---

# Why Was ISO/IEC 42001 Developed?

As AI adoption accelerated, organizations faced new governance challenges.

Examples include:

- AI-generated misinformation.
- Algorithmic bias.
- Lack of explainability.
- Privacy violations.
- Intellectual property concerns.
- Automated decision-making risks.
- Model security vulnerabilities.
- Regulatory uncertainty.

Traditional information security and risk management frameworks were not designed to address these AI-specific issues comprehensively.

ISO/IEC 42001 fills this gap by providing a dedicated governance framework for managing AI systems throughout their lifecycle.

---

# What is an Artificial Intelligence Management System (AIMS)?

An Artificial Intelligence Management System (AIMS) is a structured set of policies, processes, controls, and governance mechanisms used to manage AI responsibly.

An AIMS helps organizations:

- Define AI governance policies.
- Assign accountability for AI.
- Assess AI risks.
- Manage AI system lifecycles.
- Monitor AI performance.
- Ensure human oversight.
- Comply with applicable regulations.
- Continually improve AI governance.

Rather than focusing only on technology, an AIMS addresses people, processes, governance, ethics, and organizational culture.

---

# Best Practices

Organizations beginning their AI governance journey should:

- Treat AI governance as a strategic business initiative rather than solely a technology project.
- Establish executive ownership and accountability for AI-related decisions.
- Identify all AI systems used across the organization, including internally developed and third-party solutions.
- Develop policies that promote responsible, transparent, and ethical AI use.
- Consider AI risks alongside cybersecurity, privacy, legal, and operational risks.
- Encourage collaboration between business units, technology teams, legal advisors, compliance professionals, and risk managers.
- Align AI governance with existing management systems such as ISO/IEC 27001, ISO 31000, and ISO 22301.
- Build a culture of continual improvement to ensure AI governance evolves with technological advancements and regulatory expectations.

These practices provide a strong foundation for establishing an effective Artificial Intelligence Management System that supports innovation while protecting the organization and its stakeholders.

# Practical Example

A multinational financial institution introduces generative AI tools to assist customer service representatives, automate document reviews, and support fraud detection. While these systems improve operational efficiency, executive leadership recognizes new risks related to inaccurate outputs, biased recommendations, customer privacy, and regulatory compliance. To address these challenges, the organization decides to implement an Artificial Intelligence Management System based on ISO/IEC 42001.

By integrating AI governance with its existing ISO/IEC 27001 Information Security Management System and enterprise risk management framework, the organization ensures that innovation is balanced with accountability, transparency, and responsible use of artificial intelligence.

# Best Practices

Organizations adopting Artificial Intelligence should:

- Develop a clear understanding of how AI systems operate before deploying them in business processes.
- Identify and classify all AI systems used across the organization, including internally developed, third-party, and cloud-based AI services.

# Practical Example

A healthcare provider introduces an AI-powered diagnostic assistant to support physicians in identifying potential medical conditions from patient imaging data. Although the AI model demonstrates high accuracy during testing, the organization recognizes that incorrect predictions, biased training data, and insufficient transparency could affect patient safety and regulatory compliance. Before deploying the system, the organization establishes governance processes to validate the model, assess risks, define human oversight responsibilities, and monitor performance throughout its lifecycle.

After deployment, physicians remain responsible for final clinical decisions while the AI system serves as a decision-support tool rather than an autonomous decision-maker. Regular monitoring identifies changes in model performance as new patient data becomes available, prompting periodic retraining and validation. By applying governance across the entire AI lifecycle, the healthcare provider improves patient outcomes while maintaining accountability, transparency, and trust in its use of artificial intelligence.

# ISO/IEC 42001 Structure and Relationship with Other Frameworks

ISO/IEC 42001 is more than a technical standard for Artificial Intelligence. It is a management system standard designed to help organizations establish governance, manage AI-related risks, and continually improve the way Artificial Intelligence is developed, deployed, acquired, and used.

Like ISO/IEC 27001, ISO 9001, ISO 22301, and ISO 14001, ISO/IEC 42001 follows the **ISO High-Level Structure (HLS)**, allowing organizations to integrate AI governance into existing management systems.

---

# The High-Level Structure (HLS)

ISO management system standards share a common structure known as the **High-Level Structure (HLS)**.

This standardized structure makes it easier to implement multiple ISO standards together.

The operational clauses are:

| Clause | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **4** | Context of the Organization |
| **5** | Leadership |
| **6** | Planning |
| **7** | Support |
| **8** | Operation |
| **9** | Performance Evaluation |
| **10** | Improvement |

Because ISO/IEC 42001 uses the same framework as ISO/IEC 27001 and ISO 22301, organizations can integrate governance activities instead of managing separate systems.

---

# Relationship with ISO/IEC 27001

Many organizations already operate an Information Security Management System (ISMS) based on ISO/IEC 27001.

ISO/IEC 42001 complements—not replaces—ISO/IEC 27001.

| ISO/IEC 27001 | ISO/IEC 42001 |
|----------------|----------------|
| Information Security | Artificial Intelligence Governance |
| Confidentiality | Responsible AI |
| Integrity | Fairness |
| Availability | Transparency |
| Security Controls | AI Governance Controls |
| Cybersecurity Risks | AI Risks |

Both standards share common governance principles while addressing different objectives.

# Best Practices

Organizations implementing ISO/IEC 42001 should:

- Use the High-Level Structure (HLS) to integrate AI governance with existing management systems rather than creating isolated processes.
- Align AI governance with information security, enterprise risk management, business continuity, privacy, and quality management initiatives.
- Apply the PDCA model to ensure AI governance remains adaptive as technologies, business objectives, and regulations evolve.
- Establish common governance processes such as document control, internal audits, management reviews, and corrective actions across multiple ISO standards.
- Include AI-related risks within enterprise risk registers and organizational governance forums.
- Coordinate AI governance activities across business, technology, legal, compliance, privacy, and cybersecurity teams.
- Monitor emerging AI regulations and use ISO/IEC 42001 as a structured framework to support regulatory readiness.
- Build an integrated management system that enables consistent governance, reduces duplication, and improves organizational efficiency.

These practices help organizations manage Artificial Intelligence as part of a mature governance framework while supporting innovation, resilience, and continual improvement.

# Practical Example

A global telecommunications company already maintains certified management systems for information security (ISO/IEC 27001), business continuity (ISO 22301), and quality management (ISO 9001). As the organization expands its use of generative AI for customer support, network optimization, and software development, it chooses to implement ISO/IEC 42001 as part of its existing Integrated Management System rather than creating a separate governance program.

The organization reuses established governance processes—including document control, internal audits, management reviews, risk management, and corrective actions—while introducing AI-specific controls such as AI risk assessments, model inventories, human oversight procedures, and AI performance monitoring. This integrated approach reduces implementation effort, improves consistency across governance programs, and enables executive leadership to oversee cybersecurity, business continuity, risk management, and artificial intelligence through a unified governance framework.

- New AI capabilities.
- Emerging threats.

Many organizations struggle to find professionals with expertise in:

### Shadow AI

Employees may begin using public AI tools without organizational approval.

Examples include:

- Uploading confidential documents to AI platforms.
- Using AI-generated code without review.
- Relying on AI-generated business decisions.
- Sharing sensitive customer information with AI assistants.

---

### Third-Party AI Services

Many organizations consume AI as a service rather than developing models internally.

Examples include:

- Cloud AI platforms.
- AI-powered software.
- Large Language Models (LLMs).
- AI APIs.
- Embedded AI features in business applications.

Governance should extend to third-party AI providers through due diligence, contractual requirements, and ongoing monitoring.

---

### Explainability

Some advanced AI models produce accurate results while making it difficult to understand how decisions were reached.

Organizations should determine when explainability is necessary, particularly for high-impact decisions involving areas such as finance, healthcare, employment, or public services.

# Practical Example

A multinational insurance company introduces generative AI to assist with customer support, claims processing, policy analysis, and internal knowledge management. Although the technology improves efficiency and reduces administrative workloads, executive leadership recognizes the need to govern AI consistently across business units. The organization establishes an Artificial Intelligence Management System based on ISO/IEC 42001, defines an enterprise AI policy, creates an inventory of AI systems, and introduces approval processes for new AI use cases.

Through continual improvement, the company builds a mature AI governance capability that supports innovation while protecting customers, employees, regulators, and the organization itself.
