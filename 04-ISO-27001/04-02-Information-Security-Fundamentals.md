# What Is Information Security?

Information security is the practice of protecting information from unauthorized access, disclosure, alteration, destruction, or disruption.

Its purpose is to ensure that information remains:

- **Confidential** – accessible only to authorized individuals.
- **Accurate** – protected from unauthorized modification.
- **Available** – accessible when needed by authorized users.

Information security is often associated with technology, but it extends far beyond IT systems. It includes people, business processes, physical security, governance, and organizational culture.

An effective information security program protects information regardless of where it is stored or how it is transmitted.

# Why Information Is a Valuable Asset

Every organization depends on information to achieve its business objectives.

Without reliable information, organizations cannot:

- Serve customers.
- Process financial transactions.
- Deliver products and services.
- Make strategic decisions.
- Comply with regulations.
- Maintain business operations.

For many organizations, information is one of their most valuable assets.

Examples include:

- Customer records.
- Employee information.
- Financial reports.
- Intellectual property.
- Product designs.
- Source code.
- Contracts.
- Business strategies.
- Research data.
- Operational procedures.

The loss, theft, corruption, or unauthorized disclosure of this information can result in financial loss, legal penalties, reputational damage, and operational disruption.

# Information Exists in Many Forms

One common misconception is that information security only protects digital data.

In reality, information exists in many different forms.

Examples include:

### Digital Information

- Databases.
- Email.
- Cloud storage.
- Applications.
- Mobile devices.
- Servers.
- Virtual machines.

---

### Physical Information

- Printed reports.
- Contracts.
- Employee files.
- Customer records.
- Engineering drawings.
- Identity documents.

---

### Verbal Information

Information shared through:

- Meetings.
- Telephone conversations.
- Video conferences.
- Interviews.
- Customer discussions.

---

### Intellectual Knowledge

Information stored in people's experience and expertise, such as:

- Business processes.
- Technical knowledge.
- Research findings.
- Operational procedures.
- Strategic planning.

ISO/IEC 27001 requires organizations to protect information in all of these forms—not just electronic data.

# Information Security vs Cybersecurity

Although the terms are often used interchangeably, **information security** and **cybersecurity** are not identical.

### Information Security

Information security focuses on protecting information in any form.

It addresses:

- Governance.
- Risk management.
- Policies.
- Physical security.
- People.
- Processes.
- Technology.

---

### Cybersecurity

Cybersecurity focuses specifically on protecting digital systems, networks, applications, and electronic information from cyber threats.

It typically includes:

- Firewalls.
- Endpoint protection.
- Security monitoring.
- Network security.
- Threat detection.
- Incident response.

Cybersecurity is therefore a subset of information security.

An organization may have strong cybersecurity controls, but without governance, policies, physical security, awareness, and risk management, its overall information security program may still be ineffective.

# The Information Lifecycle

Information passes through several stages during its lifetime.

Each stage presents unique security risks and requires appropriate controls.

A typical information lifecycle includes:

1. **Creation** – Information is generated or collected.
2. **Storage** – Information is stored electronically or physically.
3. **Use** – Authorized users access and process information.
4. **Sharing** – Information is transmitted internally or externally.
5. **Archiving** – Information is retained for future reference or legal requirements.
6. **Disposal** – Information is securely destroyed when no longer required.

Security controls should be applied at every stage to maintain confidentiality, integrity, and availability.

Failure to protect information at any stage of its lifecycle can expose the organization to unnecessary risk.
