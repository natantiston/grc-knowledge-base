# Introduction

Artificial Intelligence governance is not achieved through policies alone. Organizations must establish governance controls that translate leadership objectives, risk management decisions, and ethical principles into day-to-day operational practices. These controls ensure that AI systems are developed, deployed, operated, monitored, and retired in a consistent, secure, transparent, and accountable manner. Without appropriate controls, organizations may struggle to manage AI risks, demonstrate regulatory compliance, or maintain stakeholder trust.

ISO/IEC 42001 adopts a control-based approach by encouraging organizations to implement organizational, technical, operational, security, privacy, and compliance measures that are appropriate to the risks associated with each AI system. These controls should be integrated with the organization's Artificial Intelligence Management System (AIMS) and aligned with existing governance frameworks such as ISO/IEC 27001, ISO/IEC 27701, ISO 31000, and enterprise Governance, Risk, and Compliance (GRC) programs. Effective governance controls provide assurance that AI systems operate in accordance with organizational objectives, legal obligations, and responsible AI principles.

# What are AI Governance Controls?

AI governance controls are the policies, procedures, technical safeguards, and organizational measures implemented to ensure AI systems operate safely, securely, ethically, and in compliance with applicable requirements.

Governance controls help organizations:

- Manage AI risks
- Support responsible AI
- Protect information assets
- Maintain regulatory compliance
- Improve operational consistency
- Strengthen accountability
- Protect stakeholders
- Support continual improvement

Controls should be appropriate to the organization's size, complexity, and AI risk profile.

---

# Objectives of AI Governance Controls

Well-designed governance controls aim to:

- Ensure AI systems achieve intended business objectives.
- Reduce operational and cybersecurity risks.
- Protect personal and sensitive information.
- Promote fairness and ethical decision-making.
- Maintain regulatory and contractual compliance.
- Improve transparency and accountability.
- Support business continuity and resilience.
- Enable continual improvement of AI governance.

These objectives contribute to a trustworthy Artificial Intelligence Management System.

# Categories of AI Governance Controls

Organizations should implement a balanced set of governance controls.

| Control Category | Examples |
|------------------|----------|
| Governance Controls | AI policies, governance committees, approval processes |
| Administrative Controls | Roles and responsibilities, training, procedures |
| Technical Controls | Access control, encryption, authentication, logging |
| Operational Controls | Change management, monitoring, incident management |
| Security Controls | Vulnerability management, secure development, network protection |
| Privacy Controls | Data minimization, consent management, anonymization |
| Compliance Controls | Audits, regulatory reviews, documentation |
| Ethical Controls | Bias assessments, fairness testing, human oversight |

Applying multiple categories of controls creates a layered governance approach.

---

# Governance by Design

AI governance should be incorporated into AI systems from the earliest stages of development rather than being added after deployment.

Governance by design includes:

- Defining governance requirements during planning.
- Performing AI risk assessments before development.
- Embedding security and privacy controls into system design.
- Planning for explainability and accountability.
- Including human oversight requirements.
- Documenting governance decisions throughout development.

Building governance into AI systems from the beginning is more effective than retrofitting controls later.

# Best Practices

Organizations should:

- Implement governance controls that are proportionate to AI risks.
- Integrate AI governance controls into every phase of the AI lifecycle.
- Align AI governance controls with ISO/IEC 42001, ISO/IEC 27001, ISO/IEC 27701, ISO 31000, and enterprise GRC programs.
- Clearly define ownership and accountability for governance controls.
- Regularly review and update controls to address emerging technologies, threats, and regulations.
- Document governance decisions and control implementation activities.
- Measure the effectiveness of controls through monitoring, audits, and management reviews.
- Promote continual improvement through corrective and preventive actions.

# Practical Example

A multinational insurance company develops AI systems to automate claims processing and detect fraudulent transactions. Before development begins, the organization establishes governance controls covering AI risk management, secure software development, privacy protection, human oversight, model validation, bias testing, and regulatory compliance. Executive management approves governance policies, while the AI Governance Committee oversees implementation across business, technology, cybersecurity, legal, and compliance teams. These controls are embedded into each phase of the AI lifecycle, from planning and development through deployment, monitoring, and eventual retirement.

Following deployment, governance dashboards continuously measure the effectiveness of implemented controls by tracking security incidents, model performance, audit findings, fairness metrics, and compliance activities. Internal Audit periodically reviews governance documentation and evaluates whether controls remain effective as technologies, regulations, and business objectives evolve. Management reviews use this information to prioritize improvements, allocate resources, and strengthen the organization's Artificial Intelligence Management System. Through a structured control framework aligned with ISO/IEC 42001, the organization achieves secure, responsible, and well-governed AI operations.
