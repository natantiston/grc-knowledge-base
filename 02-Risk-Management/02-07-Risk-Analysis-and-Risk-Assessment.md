# Introduction

After risks have been identified, organizations must determine which risks deserve the greatest attention.

Not every identified risk poses the same level of threat.

Some risks may have catastrophic consequences but occur very rarely, while others occur frequently but have only minor impacts.

Risk analysis and risk assessment help organizations understand these differences and make informed decisions about where to focus resources.

Without a structured assessment process, organizations may spend excessive effort managing low-priority risks while overlooking those that pose the greatest threat to achieving business objectives.

Risk assessment transforms a list of identified risks into meaningful information that supports governance, strategic planning, operational decision-making, and risk treatment.

---

# What Is Risk Analysis?

Risk analysis is the process of developing an understanding of each identified risk.

It involves examining:

- The likelihood of occurrence.
- The potential consequences.
- Existing controls.
- Factors influencing the risk.
- Possible future changes.
- Areas of uncertainty.

The objective is to estimate the level of risk so that management can determine whether additional action is necessary.

Risk analysis provides the information required to prioritize risks and allocate resources appropriately.

---

# What Is Risk Assessment?

The terms **risk analysis** and **risk assessment** are often used interchangeably, but they have different meanings.

**Risk assessment** is the broader process that includes:

1. Risk Identification.
2. Risk Analysis.
3. Risk Evaluation.

Risk assessment answers questions such as:

- What risks exist?
- How significant are they?
- Which risks require treatment?
- Which risks are acceptable?

In other words:

- **Risk identification** discovers the risks.
- **Risk analysis** estimates the level of each risk.
- **Risk evaluation** determines what actions should be taken.

Together, these activities form the complete risk assessment process.

# Factors Considered During Risk Analysis

Risk analysis considers numerous factors that influence the significance of a risk.

Common factors include:

- Likelihood of occurrence.
- Business impact.
- Existing controls.
- Control effectiveness.
- Threat capability.
- System vulnerabilities.
- Financial consequences.
- Regulatory implications.
- Operational dependencies.
- Organizational resilience.

These factors provide a comprehensive understanding of how a particular risk may affect the organization.

---

# Likelihood

Likelihood estimates the probability that a risk event will occur within a specified period.

Organizations may define likelihood using descriptive ratings or numerical values.

Common qualitative ratings include:

- Rare.
- Unlikely.
- Possible.
- Likely.
- Almost Certain.

Some organizations also assign percentage ranges or frequency estimates to improve consistency.

Factors affecting likelihood include:

- Historical incident frequency.
- Current threat environment.
- Effectiveness of preventive controls.
- Human behavior.
- Technology maturity.
- Environmental conditions.

Likelihood should be evaluated using the best available information while recognizing that uncertainty always exists.

---

# Impact

Impact represents the consequences if the identified risk occurs.

Organizations should evaluate impacts across multiple dimensions rather than focusing solely on financial loss.

Typical impact categories include:

- Financial loss.
- Operational disruption.
- Strategic objectives.
- Regulatory compliance.
- Legal liability.
- Customer trust.
- Reputation.
- Health and safety.
- Environmental damage.
- Information confidentiality, integrity, and availability.

For example, a ransomware attack may simultaneously affect financial performance, customer confidence, regulatory compliance, and business operations.

Considering multiple impact categories enables organizations to develop a more complete understanding of risk exposure.

---

# Risk Assessments Support Better Decisions

The primary purpose of risk assessment is to improve decision-making.

Accurate assessments help leadership:

- Prioritize resources.
- Focus on significant risks.
- Improve governance.
- Strengthen resilience.
- Support strategic planning.
- Justify investment decisions.
- Improve communication with stakeholders.

Rather than relying on assumptions or intuition, organizations use structured risk assessments to make objective, evidence-based decisions that align with business objectives and risk appetite.
