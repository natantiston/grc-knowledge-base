# Introduction

Not every disruption becomes a crisis.

Many incidents can be resolved through routine operational procedures or standard incident management processes. However, when an event threatens the organization's people, reputation, critical operations, or long-term viability, a structured crisis management approach becomes essential.

While **Business Continuity Management (BCM)** focuses on maintaining and recovering critical business operations, **crisis management** focuses on making strategic decisions, coordinating organizational response, communicating with stakeholders, and protecting the organization's reputation during major disruptive events.

Emergency response, crisis management, and business continuity work together to provide a comprehensive approach to organizational resilience.

---

# Understanding Emergencies, Incidents, and Crises

Although these terms are often used interchangeably, they have different meanings.

| Event | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Emergency** | An immediate situation requiring urgent action to protect people, property, or the environment. |
| **Incident** | An event that disrupts normal operations but can often be managed through established operational procedures. |
| **Crisis** | A significant event that threatens strategic objectives, critical operations, reputation, finances, or stakeholder confidence and requires executive-level coordination. |

Understanding these differences helps organizations activate the appropriate response procedures.

# What is Emergency Response?

Emergency response focuses on protecting life, health, property, and the environment during the initial stages of an event.

Typical emergency response activities include:

- Evacuation.
- Fire suppression.
- Medical assistance.
- Hazard containment.
- Emergency communications.
- Coordination with emergency services.

Emergency response is often governed by health and safety procedures and local regulations.

---

# What is Crisis Management?

Crisis management is the coordinated process of directing and controlling an organization during a major disruptive event.

Its objectives include:

- Protecting people.
- Protecting the organization's reputation.
- Supporting strategic decision-making.
- Coordinating organizational response.
- Managing stakeholder communication.
- Allocating resources.
- Supporting business continuity.
- Restoring organizational confidence.

Crisis management is typically led by senior leadership.

---

# What is Business Continuity?

Business continuity focuses on maintaining or restoring critical business operations during and after a disruption.

Examples include:

- Continuing customer services.
- Recovering IT systems.
- Activating alternate workplaces.
- Managing supplier disruptions.
- Restoring business processes.

Business continuity ensures that essential products and services remain available while the crisis is being managed.

# Best Practices

Organizations should:

- Clearly distinguish between emergency response, incident management, crisis management, and business continuity processes.
- Define objective criteria for escalating incidents into crises requiring executive oversight.
- Establish a multidisciplinary Crisis Management Team with clearly assigned roles and decision-making authority.
- Integrate crisis management procedures with Business Continuity Plans, Disaster Recovery Plans, and Incident Response Plans.
- Ensure communication procedures support employees, customers, regulators, suppliers, and other stakeholders throughout the crisis lifecycle.
- Conduct regular crisis management exercises involving executive leadership and key business functions.
- Document lessons learned after crises and exercises and incorporate improvements into the BCMS.
- Foster strong leadership, coordination, and communication to strengthen organizational resilience.

These practices help organizations respond effectively to major disruptive events while protecting people, maintaining stakeholder confidence, and ensuring continuity of critical business operations.

---

# Practical Example

A multinational manufacturing company experiences a fire at one of its primary production facilities. Emergency response teams immediately evacuate employees, coordinate with local fire services, and secure the affected area. Once the immediate threat to life and safety is under control, executive management declares a crisis and activates the Crisis Management Team. The team assesses operational impacts, coordinates communication with customers and regulators, authorizes production at alternate facilities, and allocates resources to support recovery.

At the same time, Business Continuity Plans are activated to maintain supply chain operations and fulfill customer orders through backup manufacturing sites. Following the incident, the organization conducts a lessons learned review, updates its crisis management procedures, and enhances emergency response training to improve preparedness for future events.

# Crisis Management Structure, Roles, and Communication

Effective crisis management depends on more than responding quickly—it requires a well-defined organizational structure, clear responsibilities, and reliable communication.

During a major disruption, uncertainty, time pressure, and incomplete information can make decision-making difficult. A structured Crisis Management Team (CMT) enables organizations to coordinate activities, allocate resources, communicate consistently, and support business continuity throughout the crisis.

ISO 22301 emphasizes that organizations should establish clear responsibilities and communication arrangements before a disruptive event occurs.

# Best Practices

Organizations should:

- Establish a formal Crisis Management Team with clearly defined roles, responsibilities, and decision-making authority.
- Maintain documented crisis communication procedures for employees, customers, regulators, suppliers, media, and other stakeholders.
- Ensure that only authorized spokespersons communicate externally on behalf of the organization.
- Use multiple communication channels to improve message delivery during disruptions.
- Define escalation criteria so that incidents requiring executive oversight are identified promptly.
- Produce regular situation reports to support informed decision-making throughout the crisis.
- Train Crisis Management Team members through realistic exercises and simulations.
- Review communication effectiveness after every exercise or incident and update procedures based on lessons learned.

These practices strengthen leadership coordination, improve stakeholder communication, and enable organizations to manage crises effectively while supporting business continuity and protecting organizational reputation.

---

# Practical Example

A multinational retail company experiences a ransomware attack that disrupts online sales and payment processing across several countries. The Chief Executive Officer activates the Crisis Management Team, while the Business Continuity Manager coordinates continuity procedures to maintain customer support and essential warehouse operations. The IT Recovery Team works to restore critical systems, the Communications Lead prepares updates for employees and customers, and the Legal and Compliance Representative coordinates mandatory regulatory notifications related to the cybersecurity incident.

Throughout the crisis, the Crisis Management Team receives scheduled situation reports summarizing system recovery progress, operational impacts, customer service performance, and emerging risks. By following predefined roles, communication procedures, and escalation processes, the organization maintains stakeholder confidence, supports informed executive decision-making, and restores critical business operations in a coordinated and efficient manner.

# Crisis Decision-Making, Recovery, and Post-Crisis Activities

Managing a crisis requires organizations to make timely decisions under conditions of uncertainty, limited information, and significant operational pressure.

While emergency response focuses on protecting life and immediate safety, crisis management extends throughout the disruption by coordinating strategic decisions, supporting business continuity, managing stakeholder expectations, and guiding the organization toward recovery.

ISO 22301 emphasizes that organizations should establish decision-making processes that enable effective leadership before, during, and after disruptive events.

---

# Decision-Making During a Crisis

Crisis situations often evolve rapidly.

Leaders may need to make important decisions before complete information is available.

Effective crisis decision-making should be:

- Timely.
- Risk-based.

- Coordinated.
- Transparent.
- Well documented.

Recovery begins when the immediate crisis is stabilized.

- Returning employees to normal duties.
- Closing temporary workarounds.

# Returning to Normal Operations

Returning to normal operations should be controlled rather than immediate.

Organizations should verify:

- Systems are stable.
- Critical services are functioning.
- Employees are prepared.
- Suppliers have resumed normal operations.
- Outstanding risks are acceptable.
- Temporary controls can be safely removed.

A controlled transition reduces the likelihood of secondary disruptions.

# Post-Crisis Review

The review should answer questions such as:

- What challenges were encountered?
- Were communication procedures effective?

- Were stakeholders adequately informed?
- Were sufficient resources available?
- What improvements are required?

The objective is organizational learning rather than assigning blame.

# Practical Example

A global pharmaceutical company experiences a cyberattack that disrupts manufacturing systems used to produce essential medicines. The Crisis Management Team evaluates the operational impact, assesses regulatory obligations, and authorizes the activation of Business Continuity Plans to maintain production at alternate manufacturing sites. These improvements are incorporated into updated Business Continuity Plans, Crisis Management Plans, and future training exercises, increasing the organization's readiness for future disruptive events.

# Maintaining and Continually Improving Crisis Management and Emergency Response

An effective crisis management capability is not established simply by creating a Crisis Management Plan or appointing a Crisis Management Team (CMT). Like every component of a Business Continuity Management System (BCMS), crisis management and emergency response must be regularly reviewed, exercised, maintained, and continually improved.

ISO 22301 promotes continual improvement through regular evaluations, management reviews, exercises, audits, corrective actions, and lessons learned from both real incidents and simulations.

Organizations that continuously improve their crisis management capabilities are better prepared to respond to future disruptions with speed, coordination, and confidence.

---

# Why Crisis Management Must Be Maintained

The environment in which organizations operate changes continuously.

Examples include:

- New cyber threats.
- Organizational restructuring.
- Changes in executive leadership.
- Business expansion.
- Adoption of cloud technologies.
- Artificial Intelligence (AI) integration.
- New regulatory requirements.
- Supply chain changes.
- Emerging geopolitical risks.

Without regular updates, crisis management procedures may become outdated, reducing the organization's ability to respond effectively.

---

# Best Practices

Organizations should:

- Review Crisis Management Plans regularly and update them following organizational, technological, or regulatory changes.
- Maintain current Crisis Management Team memberships, contact information, and emergency resources.
- Conduct periodic crisis management exercises involving executive leadership and key operational teams.
- Measure crisis management performance using defined KPIs and monitor trends over time.
- Document lessons learned from exercises and real incidents and convert them into measurable improvement actions.
- Track corrective actions to completion and verify their effectiveness through follow-up exercises or reviews.
- Include crisis management performance as a standing topic during management reviews.
- Foster a culture of preparedness, collaboration, and continual improvement across the organization.

These practices ensure that crisis management remains effective, responsive, and aligned with evolving business risks, enabling organizations to protect people, sustain operations, and recover successfully from future disruptive events.

---

# Practical Example

A multinational financial institution maintains an enterprise-wide crisis management program that is reviewed annually and after every significant incident. Following a large-scale cloud service outage, the organization conducts a comprehensive post-incident review involving executive management, the Crisis Management Team, IT recovery specialists, business unit leaders, and key cloud service providers. The review identifies delays in executive notifications, inconsistencies in customer communication, and opportunities to improve coordination with external technology partners.

The organization performs a root cause analysis, updates its Crisis Management Plan, enhances emergency communication procedures, and introduces executive simulation exercises focused on cloud service disruptions. Performance indicators are monitored through quarterly management reviews, and follow-up exercises confirm measurable improvements in response times, communication effectiveness, and cross-functional coordination. Through this continual improvement process, the institution strengthens its organizational resilience and enhances its ability to manage future crises effectively.
