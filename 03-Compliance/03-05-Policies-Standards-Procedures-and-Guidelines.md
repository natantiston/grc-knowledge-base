# Introduction

Every organization needs documented governance to ensure that employees understand what is expected of them and how work should be performed consistently.

Without clear documentation, employees may interpret requirements differently, leading to inconsistent practices, compliance gaps, increased operational risk, and audit findings.

To address this, organizations establish a hierarchy of governance documents that translates strategic objectives into practical day-to-day activities.

Although terminology varies slightly between organizations, the hierarchy generally includes:

- Policies
- Standards
- Procedures
- Guidelines

Some organizations also include:

- Baselines
- Work Instructions
- Technical Specifications

Each document serves a different purpose, but together they provide a structured governance framework that supports legal, regulatory, contractual, and internal compliance requirements. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

# Governance Document Hierarchy

Governance documents are developed in a logical hierarchy.

Higher-level documents establish organizational direction, while lower-level documents provide increasing levels of detail for implementation.

A typical hierarchy is:

1. Policies
2. Standards
3. Procedures
4. Guidelines

As you move down the hierarchy:

- Documents become more detailed.
- Technical specificity increases.
- Operational instructions become more explicit.
- Flexibility generally decreases.

Each lower-level document should support and align with the documents above it.

# What Is a Policy?

A **policy** is a high-level statement approved by senior management that establishes the organization's intent, direction, and expectations.

Policies answer the questions:

- **What must be achieved?**
- **Why is it important?**

Policies do not describe detailed implementation steps.

Instead, they establish mandatory principles that guide organizational decision-making.

A policy typically includes:

- Purpose.
- Scope.
- Objectives.
- Roles and responsibilities.
- High-level requirements.
- Compliance expectations.
- Governance authority.

Policies are generally stable documents that change only when significant business, legal, or regulatory changes occur. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

---

# Characteristics of Effective Policies

An effective policy should be:

- Approved by senior management.
- Aligned with organizational objectives.
- Clearly written.
- Easy to understand.
- Applicable across the organization.
- Consistent with legal and regulatory requirements.
- Periodically reviewed.
- Enforceable.

Policies establish organizational expectations without prescribing technical implementation details.

---

# Examples of Organizational Policies

Common organizational policies include:

- Information Security Policy.
- Acceptable Use Policy.
- Data Protection Policy.
- Privacy Policy.
- Risk Management Policy.
- Compliance Policy.
- Business Continuity Policy.
- Password Policy.
- Incident Management Policy.
- Supplier Security Policy.

These policies provide the foundation upon which standards, procedures, and other governance documents are developed.

A mature Compliance Management System relies on well-defined policies to communicate management's expectations, establish accountability, and ensure consistent governance across the organization.
