# Introduction

Artificial Intelligence governance is not achieved through policies alone. Organizations must establish governance controls that translate leadership objectives, risk management decisions, and ethical principles into day-to-day operational practices. These controls ensure that AI systems are developed, deployed, operated, monitored, and retired in a consistent, secure, transparent, and accountable manner. Without appropriate controls, organizations may struggle to manage AI risks, demonstrate regulatory compliance, or maintain stakeholder trust.

ISO/IEC 42001 adopts a control-based approach by encouraging organizations to implement organizational, technical, operational, security, privacy, and compliance measures that are appropriate to the risks associated with each AI system. These controls should be integrated with the organization's Artificial Intelligence Management System (AIMS) and aligned with existing governance frameworks such as ISO/IEC 27001, ISO/IEC 27701, ISO 31000, and enterprise Governance, Risk, and Compliance (GRC) programs. Effective governance controls provide assurance that AI systems operate in accordance with organizational objectives, legal obligations, and responsible AI principles.

# What are AI Governance Controls?

AI governance controls are the policies, procedures, technical safeguards, and organizational measures implemented to ensure AI systems operate safely, securely, ethically, and in compliance with applicable requirements.

Governance controls help organizations:

- Manage AI risks
- Support responsible AI
- Protect information assets
- Maintain regulatory compliance
- Improve operational consistency
- Strengthen accountability
- Protect stakeholders
- Support continual improvement

Controls should be appropriate to the organization's size, complexity, and AI risk profile.

---

# Objectives of AI Governance Controls

Well-designed governance controls aim to:

- Ensure AI systems achieve intended business objectives.
- Reduce operational and cybersecurity risks.
- Protect personal and sensitive information.
- Promote fairness and ethical decision-making.
- Maintain regulatory and contractual compliance.
- Improve transparency and accountability.
- Support business continuity and resilience.
- Enable continual improvement of AI governance.

These objectives contribute to a trustworthy Artificial Intelligence Management System.

# Governance by Design

AI governance should be incorporated into AI systems from the earliest stages of development rather than being added after deployment.

Governance by design includes:

- Defining governance requirements during planning.
- Performing AI risk assessments before development.
- Embedding security and privacy controls into system design.
- Planning for explainability and accountability.
- Including human oversight requirements.
- Documenting governance decisions throughout development.

Building governance into AI systems from the beginning is more effective than retrofitting controls later.

# Best Practices

Organizations should:

- Implement governance controls that are proportionate to AI risks.
- Integrate AI governance controls into every phase of the AI lifecycle.
- Align AI governance controls with ISO/IEC 42001, ISO/IEC 27001, ISO/IEC 27701, ISO 31000, and enterprise GRC programs.
- Clearly define ownership and accountability for governance controls.
- Regularly review and update controls to address emerging technologies, threats, and regulations.
- Document governance decisions and control implementation activities.
- Measure the effectiveness of controls through monitoring, audits, and management reviews.
- Promote continual improvement through corrective and preventive actions.

# Practical Example

A multinational insurance company develops AI systems to automate claims processing and detect fraudulent transactions. Before development begins, the organization establishes governance controls covering AI risk management, secure software development, privacy protection, human oversight, model validation, bias testing, and regulatory compliance. Executive management approves governance policies, while the AI Governance Committee oversees implementation across business, technology, cybersecurity, legal, and compliance teams. These controls are embedded into each phase of the AI lifecycle, from planning and development through deployment, monitoring, and eventual retirement.

Following deployment, governance dashboards continuously measure the effectiveness of implemented controls by tracking security incidents, model performance, audit findings, fairness metrics, and compliance activities. Internal Audit periodically reviews governance documentation and evaluates whether controls remain effective as technologies, regulations, and business objectives evolve. Management reviews use this information to prioritize improvements, allocate resources, and strengthen the organization's Artificial Intelligence Management System. Through a structured control framework aligned with ISO/IEC 42001, the organization achieves secure, responsible, and well-governed AI operations.

# Organizational Governance Controls

Organizational governance controls establish the management framework that ensures Artificial Intelligence systems are developed, operated, and maintained responsibly across the enterprise. While technical controls protect AI systems from operational and cybersecurity threats, organizational controls define how decisions are made, who is accountable, how risks are managed, and how compliance is achieved. These controls create the structure that enables consistent AI governance regardless of the technology being used.

ISO/IEC 42001 emphasizes that effective AI governance begins with leadership commitment, clearly defined responsibilities, documented policies, competent personnel, and established governance processes. Organizational controls should integrate AI governance into existing management systems, ensuring that AI initiatives align with business objectives, regulatory requirements, ethical principles, and enterprise risk management.

# Practical Example

A multinational banking group establishes an enterprise AI Governance Framework to oversee AI systems used for fraud detection, credit risk analysis, customer service automation, and financial forecasting. Executive management approves AI governance policies, while an AI Governance Committee composed of representatives from business operations, cybersecurity, legal, compliance, privacy, risk management, and data science reviews all high-risk AI initiatives before implementation. Standard operating procedures define how AI projects are approved, risk assessments are performed, models are validated, changes are managed, incidents are reported, and governance records are maintained. Employees involved in AI receive mandatory training on responsible AI, ethics, cybersecurity, and regulatory compliance.

As new AI systems are introduced or existing models are updated, formal change management procedures require governance reviews before deployment. Internal Audit periodically evaluates governance documentation, committee decisions, training records, and operational procedures to verify compliance with ISO/IEC 42001. Findings from audits and management reviews are used to strengthen policies, improve employee awareness, and refine governance processes. Through these organizational controls, the bank maintains consistent oversight of AI systems while supporting responsible innovation, regulatory compliance, and long-term operational resilience.

# Technical and Operational AI Governance Controls

While organizational governance establishes leadership, accountability, and policies, technical and operational controls ensure that AI systems are implemented and managed securely throughout their lifecycle. These controls protect AI models, datasets, supporting infrastructure, and operational processes from cybersecurity threats, human error, system failures, and unauthorized activities. Together, they enable organizations to operate AI systems in a secure, reliable, and controlled manner.

ISO/IEC 42001 encourages organizations to implement technical and operational controls that are proportionate to the risks associated with each AI system. These controls should support information security, privacy, operational resilience, and responsible AI while integrating with existing management systems such as ISO/IEC 27001, ISO/IEC 27701, and enterprise Governance, Risk, and Compliance (GRC) programs.

---

# Technical Controls

Technical controls are technology-based safeguards that protect AI systems and supporting infrastructure.

Common technical controls include:

- Multi-factor authentication (MFA)
- Role-based access control (RBAC)

- Security logging and monitoring
- Network segmentation
- Endpoint protection

These controls help maintain the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of AI systems.

# Practical Example

A multinational logistics company deploys AI systems to optimize global shipping routes, warehouse automation, and predictive fleet maintenance. To protect these systems, the organization implements role-based access controls, multi-factor authentication, encrypted communications, centralized security monitoring, and secure software development practices throughout the AI lifecycle. Operational procedures govern model validation, change management, backup scheduling, incident response, and system maintenance, ensuring that AI services remain reliable and secure even as models and datasets evolve.

During routine monitoring, the security operations center detects unusual API activity targeting one of the organization's AI services. Automated monitoring generates an alert, access to the affected service is temporarily restricted, and the incident response team begins an investigation. After identifying the root cause, corrective actions are implemented, security controls are strengthened, and the AI Governance Committee reviews the incident during the next management meeting. Internal Audit later verifies that monitoring, recovery, and governance procedures functioned effectively, reinforcing compliance with ISO/IEC 42001 and improving the organization's overall AI governance maturity.

# Measuring Control Effectiveness and Continual Improvement

Implementing AI governance controls is only the beginning of an effective Artificial Intelligence Management System (AIMS). Organizations must also verify that these controls operate as intended, continue to reduce identified risks, and remain effective as AI technologies, business objectives, and regulatory requirements evolve. Without ongoing evaluation, governance controls may become outdated, ineffective, or insufficient to address emerging threats and operational challenges.

ISO/IEC 42001 emphasizes continual improvement as a core principle of AI governance. Organizations should regularly measure the effectiveness of governance controls through monitoring, performance metrics, internal audits, management reviews, and corrective actions. This continuous evaluation ensures that AI governance remains aligned with organizational objectives, supports responsible AI practices, and adapts to changes in technology, regulations, and business operations.

# Lesson Summary

Effective AI governance requires more than implementing policies and controls—it requires continuously evaluating whether those controls remain effective. Organizations should monitor AI systems, measure governance performance using KPIs and KRIs, perform internal audits, conduct management reviews, and implement corrective actions whenever weaknesses are identified. These activities provide assurance that AI systems continue to operate securely, ethically, reliably, and in compliance with organizational and regulatory requirements.

Continual improvement is a foundational principle of ISO/IEC 42001 and enables organizations to strengthen governance as AI technologies, business priorities, and regulatory expectations evolve. By integrating monitoring, audits, performance measurement, and improvement activities with enterprise Governance, Risk, and Compliance (GRC), Information Security Management Systems, and Enterprise Risk Management programs, organizations can maintain a mature and resilient Artificial Intelligence Management System that supports trustworthy and responsible AI.

---

# Best Practices

Organizations should:

- Continuously monitor the effectiveness of AI governance controls.
- Define meaningful KPIs and KRIs to measure governance performance.
- Conduct regular internal audits of AI governance processes and controls.
- Perform periodic management reviews involving executive leadership.
- Track corrective and preventive actions until they are fully implemented and verified.
- Maintain comprehensive governance documentation to support audits and regulatory compliance.
- Regularly review governance controls in response to technological, business, or regulatory changes.
- Foster a culture of continual improvement by learning from incidents, audit findings, and operational experience.

---

# Practical Example

A multinational retail organization uses AI to forecast demand, optimize pricing, detect fraud, and personalize customer recommendations across its global operations. To ensure that governance controls remain effective, the organization implements a comprehensive monitoring program that tracks AI performance, cybersecurity events, privacy compliance, model validation results, bias indicators, and operational incidents through centralized governance dashboards. Quarterly internal audits evaluate compliance with AI governance policies, risk treatment plans, documentation requirements, and technical controls, while executive management reviews KPI and KRI trends to assess whether governance objectives are being achieved.

During one review cycle, internal auditors identify recurring issues related to delayed model validation and inconsistent documentation for recently retrained AI models. Management assigns corrective actions, updates operational procedures, provides additional training for AI development teams, and introduces automated workflow controls to improve governance consistency. Follow-up audits confirm that the improvements have been successfully implemented and that governance maturity has increased. Through continuous monitoring, measurement, audits, and continual improvement, the organization strengthens its Artificial Intelligence Management System and maintains ongoing compliance with ISO/IEC 42001 while supporting trustworthy and responsible AI.
