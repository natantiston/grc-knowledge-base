# Introduction

Every management system begins with understanding the organization in which it will operate. Before an organization can effectively govern Artificial Intelligence, it must understand its business environment, strategic objectives, regulatory obligations, technology landscape, organizational culture, and stakeholder expectations.

Clause 4 of ISO/IEC 42001 requires organizations to determine the context in which AI systems are developed, acquired, deployed, and managed. This understanding ensures that AI governance is aligned with business objectives while addressing risks, opportunities, and legal obligations.

Without a clear understanding of organizational context, AI governance may become disconnected from business priorities and fail to address the organization's actual AI risks.

---

# Purpose of Clause 4

Clause 4 establishes the foundation of the Artificial Intelligence Management System (AIMS).

It requires organizations to understand:

- The organization itself.
- Internal issues.
- External issues.
- Interested parties.
- Applicable requirements.
- The scope of the AIMS.
- AI governance processes.

Everything implemented in later clauses depends on the decisions made in Clause 4.

# Why Context Matters

Two organizations may use similar AI technologies but require very different governance approaches.

For example:

A healthcare provider using AI for medical diagnosis faces different risks and regulatory obligations than a retail company using AI to recommend products.

Similarly:

A financial institution using AI for credit approval requires stronger governance than a marketing agency using AI to generate advertising content.

Understanding organizational context ensures that governance is proportionate to the organization's AI use cases and associated risks.

# Best Practices

Organizations should:

- Evaluate organizational context before establishing AI governance policies or implementing AI systems.
- Regularly review internal business strategies, organizational changes, technology environments, and workforce capabilities that may affect AI governance.
- Monitor external developments such as emerging AI regulations, technology innovations, market expectations, and societal concerns.
- Align AI governance objectives with the organization's strategic goals, risk appetite, and digital transformation initiatives.
- Ensure that context analysis reflects both technical and business perspectives by involving stakeholders from multiple disciplines.
- Document significant internal and external issues and review them periodically as part of continual improvement.
- Use context analysis to guide AI risk assessments, governance priorities, and resource allocation.
- Recognize that organizational context evolves over time and should be reassessed whenever significant business or technological changes occur.

These practices establish a strong foundation for an Artificial Intelligence Management System that remains relevant, effective, and aligned with organizational needs.

---

# Practical Example

A multinational bank plans to expand its use of Artificial Intelligence for fraud detection, customer service, credit risk analysis, and regulatory reporting. Before implementing an Artificial Intelligence Management System, the organization conducts a context analysis to understand its business objectives, regulatory obligations, technology landscape, workforce capabilities, and organizational culture. The analysis also considers external factors such as evolving AI regulations, increasing customer expectations for transparency, and the rapid advancement of generative AI technologies.

The context assessment reveals that different AI use cases present different levels of risk. AI systems supporting credit approval require extensive governance, human oversight, and explainability, while AI-powered customer service chatbots require stronger controls over privacy, accuracy, and customer communications. By understanding its organizational context before implementing governance controls, the bank develops an Artificial Intelligence Management System that is aligned with business priorities, regulatory expectations, and enterprise risk management objectives.

# Interested Parties, Their Requirements, and Defining the Scope of the AIMS

Understanding organizational context is only the first step in establishing an Artificial Intelligence Management System (AIMS). ISO/IEC 42001 also requires organizations to identify the individuals, groups, and organizations that are affected by AI systems or have expectations regarding their governance.

These stakeholders, known as **interested parties**, influence AI governance through legal requirements, contractual obligations, ethical expectations, and business relationships. Organizations must understand these expectations to ensure that AI systems are trustworthy, transparent, and aligned with organizational objectives.

---

# Who Are Interested Parties?

Interested parties are individuals or organizations that can affect, be affected by, or perceive themselves to be affected by the organization's AI activities.

Unlike traditional technology projects, AI systems often impact a broad range of stakeholders, making stakeholder identification an essential part of governance.

# Best Practices

Organizations should:

- Identify both internal and external stakeholders that influence or are affected by AI systems.
- Determine which stakeholder expectations become mandatory legal, regulatory, contractual, or organizational requirements.
- Define the scope of the Artificial Intelligence Management System using clear organizational, technological, geographical, and operational boundaries.
- Include all significant AI activities within the defined scope, regardless of whether AI systems are developed internally or provided by third-party vendors.
- Maintain documented information such as stakeholder registers, applicable requirements, and scope statements to support governance and audit activities.
- Review stakeholder expectations regularly, particularly when regulations, business strategies, or AI technologies change.
- Ensure that the defined scope aligns with the organization's strategic objectives, risk appetite, and governance framework.
- Communicate the scope and governance responsibilities clearly so that employees, management, auditors, and other stakeholders understand which AI activities are covered by the Artificial Intelligence Management System.

These practices help establish a well-defined and auditable foundation for ISO/IEC 42001 implementation while ensuring that AI governance remains aligned with organizational needs and stakeholder expectations.

---

# Practical Example

A global insurance company implements an Artificial Intelligence Management System to govern AI applications used for claims assessment, fraud detection, customer service, and internal document processing. During the context analysis, the organization identifies executive leadership, business units, AI development teams, cybersecurity, legal, compliance, customers, regulators, cloud AI providers, and shareholders as key interested parties. Each stakeholder group has different expectations, ranging from regulatory compliance and data protection to explainable AI decisions and reliable operational performance.

Based on this analysis, the organization defines the scope of its AIMS to include all AI systems developed internally or acquired from third-party providers that support insurance operations across its European subsidiaries. Supporting documentation—including an Interested Party Register, Applicable Requirements Register, AI Inventory, and Scope Statement—is maintained and reviewed during internal audits and management reviews. This structured approach ensures that the Artificial Intelligence Management System remains aligned with organizational objectives, stakeholder expectations, and evolving regulatory requirements.

# Understanding AI Systems, Organizational Boundaries, and AI Governance Environment

An Artificial Intelligence Management System (AIMS) can only be effective if an organization understands exactly **what AI systems it uses, where those systems operate, who is responsible for them, and how they interact with business processes**.

Many organizations discover during ISO/IEC 42001 implementation that AI is already being used in multiple departments without centralized governance. Marketing teams may use generative AI for content creation, Human Resources may use AI for recruitment, Finance may use AI for forecasting, and IT teams may deploy AI-powered cybersecurity tools. Without a clear inventory and governance structure, managing AI-related risks becomes difficult.

Clause 4 encourages organizations to develop a complete understanding of their AI environment before implementing governance controls.

# Best Practices

Organizations should:

- Create and maintain a comprehensive AI Inventory that includes all internally developed, purchased, embedded, and third-party AI systems.
- Assign clear business and technical ownership for every AI system to ensure accountability throughout its lifecycle.
- Define organizational boundaries so that governance responsibilities extend across all relevant business units, locations, technologies, and external service providers.
- Establish governance committees and supporting functions with clearly defined roles in AI oversight, risk management, compliance, and decision-making.
- Identify dependencies between AI systems, business processes, cloud services, data sources, and cybersecurity controls before performing AI risk assessments.
- Regularly review the AI Inventory to identify newly introduced AI technologies, unauthorized "shadow AI," and retired systems.
- Align AI governance activities with organizational objectives so that governance supports innovation, operational efficiency, and regulatory compliance.
- Use the AI Inventory as a foundational input for risk assessments, audits, management reviews, and continual improvement activities within the Artificial Intelligence Management System.

These practices help organizations establish visibility, accountability, and effective governance across their entire AI environment while supporting long-term compliance with ISO/IEC 42001.

---

# Practical Example

A multinational retail organization begins implementing ISO/IEC 42001 by creating an enterprise-wide AI Inventory. During the discovery process, the implementation team identifies more than forty AI-enabled applications across customer service, supply chain management, marketing, finance, cybersecurity, and human resources. Several business units are also found to be using public generative AI tools without formal approval, highlighting the presence of shadow AI and inconsistent governance practices.

To address these issues, the organization establishes an AI Governance Committee, assigns business and technical owners to every AI system, documents cloud service dependencies, and classifies each AI application according to its business purpose and level of risk. The AI Inventory becomes a central governance resource that supports AI risk assessments, internal audits, regulatory reporting, and management reviews. As a result, the organization gains greater visibility into its AI environment and builds a stronger foundation for responsible AI governance.

# Clause 4 Implementation, Required Documentation, and Practical Guidance

Understanding the organizational context is only valuable if it is translated into practical governance activities. Clause 4 of ISO/IEC 42001 requires organizations to document their understanding of the business environment, identify stakeholders, define the scope of the Artificial Intelligence Management System (AIMS), and establish the foundation for all subsequent governance processes.

Although Clause 4 does not prescribe a specific documentation format, organizations are expected to maintain sufficient documented information to demonstrate that context has been properly evaluated and considered during AI governance planning.

# Best Practices

Organizations should:

- Conduct a structured context analysis before developing AI governance policies or operational controls.
- Maintain documented information that clearly supports the identification of internal issues, external issues, interested parties, applicable requirements, and the defined scope of the Artificial Intelligence Management System.
- Develop and maintain a centralized AI Inventory that reflects all significant AI systems across the organization, including internally developed and third-party solutions.
- Review organizational context regularly to account for changes in business strategy, AI technologies, regulatory obligations, and stakeholder expectations.
- Establish clear ownership for maintaining Clause 4 documentation and ensure responsibilities are assigned across business and technical functions.
- Use the outputs of Clause 4 as direct inputs into leadership decisions, AI risk assessments, planning activities, and governance controls required by later clauses of ISO/IEC 42001.
- Integrate Clause 4 documentation with existing governance artifacts, such as enterprise risk registers, information security documentation, and compliance records, to support an efficient Integrated Management System.
- Treat organizational context as a living process that evolves alongside the organization's AI capabilities and governance maturity.

These practices help organizations establish a strong governance foundation that supports effective implementation, internal audits, continual improvement, and long-term compliance with ISO/IEC 42001.

# Practical Example

A multinational healthcare organization begins implementing ISO/IEC 42001 by conducting a comprehensive Clause 4 assessment. A cross-functional team representing business operations, information technology, legal, compliance, cybersecurity, privacy, and clinical services identifies internal objectives for expanding AI-assisted diagnostics while documenting external drivers such as evolving AI regulations, patient privacy requirements, and increasing public expectations for transparent and ethical AI. The team also develops an Interested Party Register, an Applicable Requirements Register, a detailed AI Inventory, and a formal scope statement covering all AI systems used in clinical decision support, patient engagement, and administrative operations.

The documented outputs from Clause 4 become the foundation for the remainder of the implementation project. Executive leadership uses the context analysis to establish governance priorities, while risk management teams perform AI risk assessments using the AI Inventory as their primary reference. During internal audits and management reviews, the organization periodically updates its context analysis to reflect new AI technologies, changing regulations, and expanding business objectives. This structured approach ensures that the Artificial Intelligence Management System remains aligned with organizational strategy and continues to support responsible AI governance as the organization's AI capabilities mature.
